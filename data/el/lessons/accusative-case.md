# Unit 5: The Accusative Case & Basic Prepositions

In Unit 2, you learned that when a noun is the **Object** (receiving the action), it drops its final **-ς** (if masculine) and its article becomes **τον, την,** or **το**. 

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Masculine 'N'**  
> We **always** keep the final **-ν** in the masculine article (**τον**) to avoid confusion with the neuter article (**το**). This is true even if the next word starts with a soft sound!
Now, we will see the Accusative Case in action through verbs and the "Big Three" prepositions.

---

## 1. Verbs that use the Accusative
Most "action" verbs require the object to be in the Accusative case.

- **Βλέπω τον** Γιώργο. (I see George).
- **Έχω την** τσάντα. (I have the bag).
- **Διαβάζω το** βιβλίο. (I read the book).

> [!CAUTION]
> Remember: The verb **Είμαι** (I am) is special. It acts as a mirror, so it keeps everything in the **Nominative** (ο, η, το).  
> - **Είμαι ο Γιώργος.** (I am George).

---

## 2. The Big Three Prepositions (σε, από, με)
In Greek, almost all prepositions force the noun that follows them into the Accusative case.

### A. Σε (To / At / In)
This word shows location or direction. In Greek, we almost **never** say "σε" and "the" separately. They merge into one word:

- σε + τον = **στον**
- σε + την = **στη(ν)**
- σε + το = **στο**

*Example:* **Πάω στο σινεμά.** (I am going to the cinema).

### B. Από (From)
Used for origins. It does **not** merge with the article, but the article must still be in the Accusative.

- **Είμαι από την Ελλάδα.** (I am from Greece).
- **Έρχομαι από τον γιατρό.** (I am coming from the doctor).

> [!TIP]
> **Blend it!** Even though it's written separately, Greeks often blend them when speaking quickly (**από το** sounds like "ap-to")!

### C. Με (With)
Used for company or instruments.

- **Είμαι με τη Μαρία.** (I am with Maria).
- **Θέλω καφέ με ζάχαρη.** (I want coffee with sugar).

---

## 3. Pronunciation: The Dropping 'N'
While the masculine articles (**τον, στον, έναν**) **always** keep their **-ν** in writing to avoid confusion with the neuter, the feminine words (**την, στην**) and particles (**δεν, μην**) are more selective. They only keep the **-ν** if the next word starts with:
1. A vowel (**α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω**)
2. A hard consonant (**κ, π, τ, ξ, ψ**)
3. A digraph (**μπ, ντ, γκ, τσ, τζ**)

- **Στη** δασκάλα (*The teacher - soft 'δ'*)
- **Στην** πόρτα (*The door - hard 'π'*)

If you aren't sure, it is usually safer to include the **-ν**—native speakers will still understand you perfectly!
