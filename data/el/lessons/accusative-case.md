# The Accusative Case (Direct Objects & Movement)

In Unit 2, you learned the theory behind the **Accusative Case**. You learned that when a noun is receiving the action (like being seen or being held), it becomes the Object. 

Because it is the Object, a masculine noun drops its final "**-ς**", and the article changes to "**τον / την / το**".

Now that you know how to conjugate verbs, it's time to put the Accusative Case into practice!

---

## 1. Verbs that Demand the Accusative

In Greek, almost every verb that performs an action *on* something requires that "something" to be in the Accusative case. 

Look at the verb **βλέπω** (I see). You can't just "see". You have to see *something*.

- Βλέπω **τον** δάσκαλο. (I see **the** teacher).
- Βλέπω **την** τηλεόραση. (I see **the** TV).
- Βλέπω **το** παιδί. (I see **the** child).

> [!TIP]
> **What about "Είμαι" (I am)?**
> The verb "To be" is a mirror. It just reflects the Subject back onto itself ("I am George"). Because nothing is being acted upon, verbs like *είμαι* DO NOT take the Accusative case. They keep everything in the Nominative case!
> - Είμαι **ο** Γιώργος. (I am George).

---

## 2. Prepositions of Movement (σε, από, με)

The Accusative case isn't just for Direct Objects. It is also triggered by little words called **Prepositions**. 

In English, prepositions are words like *in, at, to, from, with*. When you use these words in Greek, the word that comes immediately after them **must** be thrown into the Accusative case.

Here are the three most important prepositions in the Greek language:

### A. The word "Σε" (In / At / To)
The word **σε** is the ultimate location word. It means *in*, *at*, or *to*, depending on the context of the sentence!

What happens when **σε** meets a definite article (τον, την, το)? They smash together into one word!

- σε + τον = **στον** (to the / in the - masculine)
- σε + την = **στην** (to the / in the - feminine)
- σε + το = **στο** (to the / in the - neuter)

::: example-box 
**English**: I am going **to the** cinema. (cinema = *το* σινεμά)
**Greek**: Πάω **στο** σινεμά.
::: end

::: example-box 
**English**: I work **at the** bank. (bank = *η* τράπεζα)
**Greek**: Δουλεύω **στην** τράπεζα.
::: end

---

### B. The word "Από" (From)
The word **από** means "from". It is used for origins and starting points.

Unlike *σε*, **από** does NOT smash together with the article. They sit next to each other normally, but the noun is still in the Accusative!

::: example-box 
**English**: I am **from** Greece. (Greece = *η* Ελλάδα)
**Greek**: Είμαι **από την** Ελλάδα.
::: end

::: example-box 
**English**: I am coming **from the** doctor. (doctor = *ο* γιατρός)
**Greek**: Έρχομαι **από τον** γιατρό. *(Notice that "γιατρός" dropped its -ς!)*
::: end

---

### C. The word "Με" (With)
The word **με** means "with".

::: example-box 
**English**: I am **with** Maria.
**Greek**: Είμαι **με τη** Μαρία.
::: end

::: example-box 
**English**: I want coffee **with** sugar. (sugar = *η* ζάχαρη)
**Greek**: Θέλω καφέ **με** ζάχαρη. *(Notice we didn't use an article here because we don't mean "the specific sugar"!)*
::: end

- [ ] I know that **σε + τον = στον**.
