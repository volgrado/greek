# The Adjectives (Τα Επίθετα)

In English, adjectives are lazy. If you want to describe a man, a woman, or a child as "good," the word "good" never changes. 
- The **good** man.
- The **good** woman.
- The **good** child.

In Greek, adjectives are hardworking. They must perfectly match the noun they are describing in **gender, number, and case**. We call this **Agreement**.

---

## 1. The Chameleon Words

Because an adjective has to be ready to describe a masculine, feminine, or neuter noun, every single adjective in the dictionary comes in **three versions**. 

You can think of adjectives as chameleons that change their skin to match whatever noun they are standing next to.

Let's look at the word for "good" (**καλός**):

::: adjective-showcase mt-8
**Masculine Version (for -ος nouns)**
Ο καλ**ός** πατέρας *(The good father)*

**Feminine Version (for -η nouns)**
Η καλ**ή** μητέρα *(The good mother)*

**Neuter Version (for -ο nouns)**
Το καλ**ό** παιδί *(The good child)*
::: end

Notice how the ending of the adjective perfectly mirrors the ending of the noun? This is the beauty of Greek! Words rhyme with each other. 

> [!TIP]
> **Dictionary Format**
> When you look up an adjective in a Greek dictionary, it will always be listed in the masculine form (**καλός**). It is up to you to change the ending to feminine (**καλή**) or neuter (**καλό**) if the noun requires it!

---

## 2. Common Adjective Families

Almost all beginners adjectives fall into one of two spelling families: the **-ος, -η, -ο** family, or the **-ος, -α, -ο** family. 

### Family A: The "-η" Adjectives
These use an **-η** for the feminine version.

| Root | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Good** | καλ**ός** | καλ**ή** | καλ**ό** |
| **Tall** | ψηλ**ός** | ψηλ**ή** | ψηλ**ό** |
| **Small** | μικρ**ός** | μικρ**ή** | μικρ**ό** |

### Family B: The "-α" Adjectives
These use an **-α** for the feminine version. (Usually adjectives that have a vowel or a *ρ* right before the ending).

| Root | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Beautiful** | ωραί**ος** | ωραί**α** | ωραί**ο** |
| **New** | νέ**ος** | νέ**α** | νέ**ο** |
| **Cold** | κρύ**ος** | κρύ**α** | κρύ**ο** |

## 3. Case Agreement (The Flexing Adjective)

Remember Declension? Nouns change their endings when they become the Object (Accusative). 

Because adjectives are chameleons, **they must change their endings too!** If the noun flexes its grammatical muscle, the adjective has to flex with it.

Let's look at "The good man" (masculine).

**As the Subject (Nom):** 
**Ο καλός** άντρας είναι εδώ. (The good man is here).

**As the Object (Acc):** 
Βλέπω **τον καλό** άντρα. (I see the good man).

Notice what happened? The noun **άντρας** dropped its **-ς** because it became the object. Because the adjective **καλός** is attached to it, it *also* had to drop its **-ς** and become **καλό**!

> [!NOTE]
> *Does this happen to feminine and neuter words?*
> No! Remember from the declension tables: feminine and neuter words never change their endings in the singular when becoming the object. Therefore, their adjectives don't change either!
> - Nom: **Η καλή** μητέρα
> - Acc: Βλέπω **την καλή** μητέρα
