# Ancient Remnants in Modern Greek

Welcome to **Level C2: Mastery**. 

Modern Greek is a living, breathing language, but it carries the ghosts of its 3,000-year history everywhere it goes. 

In the 19th and 20th centuries, Greece had two official languages: 
1. **Demotic (Δημοτική):** The natural spoken language of the people.
2. **Katharevousa (Καθαρεύουσα):** An artificial, highly purified, "ancient-sounding" language used by the government, schools, and news anchors.

In 1976, Demotic officially won and became the sole language of Greece. However, the 150 years of Katharevousa left deep scars on the modern language. 

To achieve true C2 fluency, you must understand the "Ancient Remnants" that are still used in the news, literature, and formal speech today.

---

## 1. Dative Expressions (The Lost Case)

In Level A, you learned the 4 modern Greek cases (Nominative, Genitive, Accusative, Vocative). 

Ancient Greek had a 5th case: **The Dative Case** (used to show "to" or "for" someone). Modern Greek replaced this entirely by using "σε + Accusative".

However, dozens of Dative phrases survived as frozen, highly formal expressions. You cannot learn the grammar for these; you must simply memorize them as vocabulary.

They are characterized by the suffix **-ω** (with an iota subscript that we don't write anymore) or **-οις / -αις** in the plural.

- **Εν τω μεταξύ** (In the meantime)
- **Δόξα τω Θεώ** (Glory to God / Thank God)
- **Εν πάση περιπτώσει** (In any case / Anyway)
- **Εν τέλει** (In the end / Finally)
- **Τοις μετρητοίς** (In cash)

*(Notice the tiny word **εν** (in). It is an ancient preposition that ALWAYS triggers the Dative case, and you will see it constantly in C2 vocabulary!)*

## 2. Ancient Prepositions

In Unit 21, we learned that most modern prepositions use the Accusative Case (σε, από, με) and some use the Genitive (υπέρ, κατά).

C2 Greek resurrects several ancient prepositions that demand the **Genitive** case. They are used in formal contexts, journalism, and academic writing.

| Ancient Preposition | Meaning | Modern Equivalent | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ενώπιον** (+ Gen) | Before / In the presence of | Μπροστά σε | Ορκίστηκε **ενώπιον του** δικαστηρίου. (He swore before the court). |
| **Λόγω** (+ Gen) | Due to / Because of | Επειδή | Κλειστό **λόγω** βροχής. (Closed due to rain). |
| **Μέσω** (+ Gen) | Via / Through | Από | Έφτασα **μέσω** Αθηνών. (I arrived via Athens). |
| **Άνευ** (+ Gen) | Without | Χωρίς | Προσοχή! Διασταύρωση **άνευ** σηματοδότη. (Caution! Junction without a traffic light). |

## 3. The 3rd Declension Nouns (-ις / -εως)

In Level A1, you learned the standard noun endings (-ος, -α, -η, -ι, -ο).

However, Katharevousa dragged a group of ancient "3rd Declension" nouns into modern Greek. These are almost always abstract feminine nouns ending in **-ις** or **-η** (with ancient roots). 

Their defining feature is their weird Genitive singular ending: **-εως**.

- Η πόλις / Η πόλη (The city) ➔ Το κέντρο **της πόλεως** (The center of the city).
- Η κρίσις / Η κρίση (The crisis) ➔ Η λύση **της κρίσεως** (The solution to the crisis).
- Η κυβέρνησις / Η κυβέρνηση (The government) ➔ Ο εκπρόσωπος **της κυβερνήσεως** (The government spokesperson).

*(While it is perfectly fine to say "της πόλης" in everyday street Greek, any news anchor, politician, or formal writer will proudly say "της πόλεως" instead!)*
