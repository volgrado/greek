# Unit 1: The Greek Writing System Masterclass

Welcome to your first step toward mastering Greek. Forget everything you've heard about Greek being "impossible"—as an English speaker, learning the Greek alphabet is an incredibly satisfying and fast process. Over 25% of the English vocabulary has Greek roots; you already know more Greek than you realize. 

This comprehensive guide is designed to take you from zero to reading Modern Greek fluently. We will break the alphabet down into manageable, logical groups. 

---

## 1. The 24 Letters

The Greek alphabet consists of 24 letters. Instead of learning them in alphabetical order, let's group them by how they relate to the English alphabet. This is the fastest way to bridge the gap.

### Group 1: The Twins 
These letters are your best friends. They look and sound almost exactly like their English counterparts. 

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example (in English) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Α α** | Alpha | [a] | f**a**ther |
| **Ε ε** | Epsilon | [e] | p**e**t |
| **Ι ι** | Iota | [ee] | m**ee**t |
| **Κ κ** | Kappa | [k] | **k**ite |
| **Μ μ** | Mu | [m] | **m**om |
| **Ν ν** | Nu | [n] | **n**o |
| **Ο ο** | Omicron | [o] | n**o**t |
| **Τ τ** | Tau | [t] | **t**op |
| **Ζ ζ** | Zeta | [z] | **z**oo |

> [!TIP]
> **The Crisp Pronunciation Rule:** In English, we often release a puff of air when saying consonants like P, T, and K (this is called *aspiration*). In Greek, **π [p], τ [t], and κ [k]** are perfectly crisp. Say "spot" instead of "pot"—that crisp 'p' is exactly how Greek sounds.

### Group 2: The False Friends
These letters look familiar, but they are trying to trick you! Pay close attention to these, as they require rewiring your brain slightly.

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example (in English) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Β β** | Beta | [v] | **v**an (It is NEVER a 'b') |
| **Η η** | Eta | [ee] | m**ee**t (It is NEVER an 'h') |
| **Ρ ρ** | Rho | [r] | a lightly rolled **r** (Never a 'p') |
| **Χ χ** | Chi | [h] | Scottish lo**ch** or human **h**eat |
| **Υ υ** | Upsilon | [ee] | m**ee**t (Never a 'y' or 'u') |

### Group 3: The True Greeks
These are the iconic, unique shapes of the Greek alphabet. You've probably seen them in physics, math, or fraternities.

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example (in English) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Γ γ** | Gamma | [gh] / [y] | **y**es (soft before vowels e/i) |
| **Δ δ** | Delta | [th] | **th**is (voiced, soft) |
| **Θ θ** | Theta | [th] | **th**ink (voiceless, hard) |
| **Λ λ** | Lambda | [l] | **l**ion |
| **Ξ ξ** | Xi | [ks] | bo**x** |
| **Π π** | Pi | [p] | **p**en |
| **Σ σ, ς** | Sigma | [s] | **s**un (becomes **ς** only at the end of a word) |
| **Φ φ** | Phi | [f] | **f**ish |
| **Ψ ψ** | Psi | [ps] | li**ps** |
| **Ω ω** | Omega | [o] | n**o**t (exact same sound as Ο ο) |

> [!NOTE]
> **The Secret of Sigma:** Sigma has a special rule. When it's in the middle of a word, it looks like **σ** (e.g., θάλασ**σ**α - sea). But if it is the very last letter of a word, it drops its tail and becomes **ς** (e.g., κόσμο**ς** - world). Either way, it sounds exactly the same!

## 2. The Digraphs (Sound Combinations)

Sometimes, Greek glues two letters together to create an entirely new sound. Think of English "sh" or "th".

### The Vowel Combinations (Diphthongs)
Greek loves the "ee" sound. In fact, there are six different ways to make it!

- **αι** = [e] like in p**e**t (e.g., **αι**τία - cause)
- **ει, οι, υι** = [ee] like in m**ee**t (e.g., **εί**μαι - I am)
- **ου** = [oo] like in b**oo**t (e.g., **ου**ρανός - sky)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Chameleon Vowels (αυ & ευ):**  
> When **α** or **ε** team up with **υ**, the **υ** transforms from a vowel into a consonant! 
> - **αυ / ευ** sound like **[af] / [ef]** when followed by unvoiced consonants (like κ, π, τ).
> - **αυ / ευ** sound like **[av] / [ev]** when followed by vowels or voiced consonants (like γ, δ, β).

### The Consonant Combinations
Over thousands of years, ancient Greek sounds evolved. To write modern sounds like B, D, and G, Greeks had to get creative by combining existing letters.

- **μπ = B** (e.g., **μπ**α**μπ**άς - baba, dad)
- **ντ = D** (e.g., **ντ**ύνω - dino, to dress)
- **γκ / γγ = G** (e.g., ά**γκ**υρα - agira, anchor)
- **τσ = TS** (e.g., **τσ**έπη - tsepi, pocket)
- **τζ = J / DZ** (e.g., **τζ**άκι - jaki, fireplace)

## 3. Accents & Punctuation

Modern Greek is remarkably phonetic. Once you know the rules above, you can confidently pronounce any word you see. A huge part of this is due to the accent mark.

### The Tonos (´)
Every Greek word with two or more syllables carries a tiny mark called the **tonos** over one vowel. This tells you exactly which syllable to stress.
- **νερό** (ne-RÓ) - water
- **πρόβλημα** (PRÓ-vli-ma) - problem

If you see a tonos over a vowel combination (like **εί**), the sound doesn't change, but that's where you put your vocal energy.

### Punctuation Marks
Greek punctuation is almost identical to English, with two brilliant exceptions:
1. **The Question Mark (;)**: The Greek question mark looks exactly like an English semicolon! (e.g., Τι κάνεις; = How are you?)
2. **The Upper Dot (·)**: This acts like an English semicolon or colon, signaling a pause longer than a comma but shorter than a period.
