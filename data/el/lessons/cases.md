# The Cases (Οι Πτώσεις)

Welcome to the feature of Greek grammar that terrifies English speakers the most: **The Cases**.

Take a deep breath. Cases are actually very logical. They are just a way of tagging words so you know what job they are doing in a sentence.

Think of a sentence as a movie set. You have:
- **The Director** (The Subject doing the action)
- **The Actor** (The Direct Object receiving the action)
- **The Producer** (The person who owns something)

In English, we know who is who based on *word order*. 
**"The dog ate the cat"** means something very different than **"The cat ate the dog."**

In Greek, you can put the words in any order you want! How do you know who is the eater and who is the eaten? You tag them with **Cases**.

---

## 1. Nominative (Ονομαστική): The Subject

The **Nominative Case** is the dictionary form of a word. You use it when the noun is the **Subject** of the sentence (the one doing the action).

*Question to ask: "Who or what is doing the action?"*

::: example-box
**Ο Γιώργος** πίνει καφέ. (George drinks coffee.)
George is the one doing the drinking. He is in the Nominative case.
::: end

::: example-box
**Η Μαρία** είναι εδώ. (Maria is here.)
Maria is the subject. She is in the Nominative case.
::: end

Every noun you have learned so far (ο φίλος, η μητέρα, το βιβλίο) has been in the Nominative case.

---

## 2. Accusative (Αιτιατική): The Object

The **Accusative Case** is used when the noun is the **Direct Object** of the sentence (the one receiving the action).

*Question to ask: "Who or what is being acted upon?"*

::: example-box highlight
Ο Γιώργος βλέπει **τη Μαρία**. (George sees Maria.)
George is looking. Maria is being looked at. So, Maria changes to the Accusative form (**τη Μαρία**).
::: end

This is exactly like the difference between **"I"** and **"Me"** or **"He"** and **"Him"** in English!
- **He** sees her. (Nominative)
- She sees **him**. (Accusative)

Greek just takes this concept and applies it to *every single noun in the language*, not just pronouns.

---

## 3. Genitive (Γενική): Possession

The **Genitive Case** is used to show ownership or relationship. It replaces the English word "of" or the apostrophe-'s (John's car).

*Question to ask: "Whose is it?"*

::: example-box
Το βιβλίο **της Μαρίας**. (The book of Maria / Maria's book.)
::: end

---

## 4. Vocative (Κλητική): Addressing someone

The **Vocative Case** is used exclusively when you are calling out to someone or talking directly to them.

*Question to ask: "Hey!"*

::: example-box
Γεια σου, **Γιώργο**! (Hello, George!)
::: end

---

## Summary of Case Tags

Watch how poor George's name changes depending on his role in the movie:

1. **Nominative (Subject)**: **Ο Γιώργος** is reading a book.
2. **Accusative (Object)**: I see **τον Γιώργο**. 
3. **Genitive (Owner)**: This is the car of **του Γιώργου**.
4. **Vocative (Calling)**: Hey **Γιώργο**, come here!

For the rest of the A1 level, we are only going to focus on the first two: **Nominative** (Subject) and **Accusative** (Object). Master those, and you can say almost anything!
