# Double Letters (Digraphs)

Now that you know the 24 letters of the Greek alphabet, we need to talk about **digraphs**. Simply put, sometimes Greek takes two letters and glues them together to create a brand new sound. 

This happens in English all the time. Think about the word **s** and **h**. Together, they form **sh**. The same principle applies in Greek, and there are two types: **Vowel Combinations** and **Consonant Combinations**.

---

## 1. Vowel Combinations (Diphthongs)

Greek loves vowel combinations, but here is the good news: thanks to a linguistic process over thousands of years, most of them just make an `[ee]` sound or an `[e]` sound.

| Combination | Pronunciation | Example in English | Greek Example | Translation |
| :---: | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **αι** | `[e]` as in **p*e*t** | **E**gg | **αι**τία *(etía)* | cause |
| **ει** | `[ee]` as in **m*ee*t** | S**ee** | **εί**μαι *(ímai)* | I am |
| **οι** | `[ee]` as in **m*ee*t** | S**ee** | **οι**κογένεια *(ikoyénia)* | family |
| **υι** | `[ee]` as in **m*ee*t** | S**ee** | **υι**οθετώ *(io-thetó)* | to adopt |
| **ου** | `[oo]` as in **b*oo*t** | M**oo**n | **ου**ρανός *(ouranós)* | sky |

> [!TIP]
> **Why all the "ee" sounds?**
> You now know *six* different ways to write the `[ee]` sound in Greek (**ι**, **η**, **υ**, **ει**, **οι**, **υι**). 
> - **ει** is mostly used for Verbs (e.g., έχ**ει** = he has).
> - **οι** is mostly used for Plural Nouns (e.g., φίλ**οι** = friends).
> - **ι / η** are mostly used for Singular Nouns. 

---

## 2. The Chameleons: αυ & ευ

The combinations **αυ** and **ευ** are special. The **υ (Upsilon)** completely loses its vowel sound and turns into a consonant—either a **V** or an **F**, depending on the letter that comes *immediately after it*.

### The "F" Sound (Before Hard Consonants)
If the next letter is "hard" (voiceless), like **κ, π, τ, θ, σ, φ, χ**, the combination sounds like **af** or **ef**.
- **αυ**τοκίνητο ➔ *(**af**-tokínito)* = car
- **Ευ**ρώπη ➔ *(**Ef**-rópi)* = Europe

### The "V" Sound (Before Soft Consonants or Vowels)
If the next letter is a vowel or "soft" (voiced), like **γ, β, δ, ζ, λ, μ, ν, ρ**, it sounds like **av** or **ev**.
- **αύ**ριο ➔ *(**áv**-rio)* = tomorrow
- **Ευ**αγγέλιο ➔ *(**Ev**-angélio)* = Gospel

---

## 3. Consonant Combinations

Ancient Greek didn't have the sounds for **B**, **D**, or **G** as we know them in English today. To make these sounds in Modern Greek, you have to smash two consonants together. 

| Combination | Sound | Greek Example | Pronunciation | Translation |
| :---: | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **μπ** (m+p) | **B** as in **B**oy | **μπ**α**μπ**άς | ***b*a*b*ás** | dad |
| **ντ** (n+t) | **D** as in **D**og | **ντ**ύνω | ***d*íno** | to dress |
| **γκ / γγ** (g+k) | **G** as in **G**o | ά**γκ**υρα | **á*g*ira** | anchor |
| **τσ** (t+s) | **TS** as in i**ts** | **τσ**έπη | ***ts*épi** | pocket |
| **τζ** (t+z) | **J / DZ** as in **j**eep | **τζ**άκι | ***dz*áki** | fireplace |

> [!CAUTION]
> **Wait, I thought Beta (Β) was 'B' and Delta (Δ) was 'D'?**
> No! Remember from the previous lesson: **Β** sounds like **V** (Video), and **Δ** sounds like **TH** (There). 
> So if you want to write "Bank" in Greek, you can't use **Β**. You have to use **μπ** ➔ **Τράπεζα** (Trápeza) or the English loan word **Μπανκ**.
