# Participles in the Genitive Absolute (Γενική Απόλυτη)

In Level B2, you learned about basic Participles. But there is a "secret" third type of participle structure—a ghost from Ancient Greek that survived in modern, highly formal Katharevousa-style texts.

It is called the **Genitive Absolute (Η Γενική Απόλυτη Μετοχή)**. 

You will rarely hear this spoken in a coffee shop, but you will absolutely see it in newspapers, official documents, literature, and news broadcasts.

## 1. What is the Genitive Absolute?
Sometimes you want to connect two actions that happen at the same time, but they have two different subjects.

In normal everyday Greek, you use the word **ενώ** (while) or **επειδή** (because):
*   *Επειδή έβρεχε, μείναμε μέσα.* (Because it was raining, we stayed inside).

To sound incredibly formal and elevated, C2 speakers rip out the connector words, erase the verb, and replace the whole clause with Nouns and Participles in the **Genitive Case**.

---

## 2. The Formula & The Grammar
Instead of a normal sentence with a Nominative subject and a normal verb, you create an "Absolute" bubble out of Genitive words.

**Formula:** [Noun in Genitive] + [Participle in Genitive]

### A. The Ancient Active Participles (-ων, -ουσα, -ον)
To understand absolute expressions, you must recognize a class of ancient active participles that survive in formal Modern Greek. The stem for the Genitive forms ends in **-οντ-**. 
*   *Genitive:* του τρέχ**οντ**ος (of the running one).
*   *Plural Genitive:* των τρέχ**οντ**ων (of the running ones).

### B. Passive Participles & Reduplication (-μένος)
In highly formal C2 Greek, many passive participles feature an ancient prefix (an extra syllable) called **reduplication**.
*   **δίνω** (I give) ➔ **δεδομένος** (given).
*   **τελώ** (I perform) ➔ **τετελεσμένος** (accomplished).

> [!TIP]
> **Example in Context:**
> *Normal Greek:* Επειδή αυτά τα πράγματα είναι δεδομένα...  
> *Genitive Absolute:* **Δεδομένων αυτών των πραγμάτων...** (These things being given...)

---

## 3. The Most Common Frozen Expressions
In Modern Greek, people almost exclusively rely on a handful of highly recognizable, frozen expressions. 

| The Genitive Absolute | Literal Translation | True Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Θεού θέλοντος** | God willing | Hopefully / God willing |
| **Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων** | Things having well | All being well / Hopefully |
| **Απουσιάζοντος του καθηγητού** | The teacher being absent | With the teacher absent |
| **Τρέχοντος του έτους** | The year running | During the current year |
| **Μη γένοιτο!** | Let it not happen | God forbid! |
| **Τουναντίον...** | Of the opposite | On the contrary... |

*   *Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων, θα τελειώσουμε το έργο αύριο.* (All going well, we will finish the project tomorrow).
