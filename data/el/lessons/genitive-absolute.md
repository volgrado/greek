# Participles in the Genitive Absolute (Γενική Απόλυτη)

In Level B2 (Unit 24), you learned about Participles.
- The Active Participle (-οντας): "While running..."
- The Passive Participle (-μένος): "The broken glass..."

But there is a "secret" third type of participle structure. It is a ghost from Ancient Greek that survived in modern, highly formal Katharevousa-style texts.

It is called the **Genitive Absolute** (Η Γενική Απόλυτη Μετοχή). 

You will rarely hear this spoken in a coffee shop, but you will absolutely see it in newspapers, official documents, literature, and news broadcasts.

---

## 1. What is the Genitive Absolute?

Sometimes you want to connect two actions that happen at the same time, but they have *two different subjects*.

In normal everyday Greek, you just use the word **ενώ** (while) or **επειδή** (because):
- **Επειδή έβρεχε**, μείναμε μέσα. (Because it was raining, we stayed inside).
- **Ενώ μιλούσε ο Γιώργος**, χτύπησε το τηλέφωνο. (While George was speaking, the phone rang).

To sound incredibly formal and elevated, C2 speakers rip out the connector words (while/because), erase the verb, and replace the whole clause with **Nouns and Participles in the Genitive Case**.

---

## 2. The Formula

Instead of a normal sentence with a Nominative subject and a normal verb, you create an "Absolute" bubble out of Genitive words.

**[Noun in Genitive] + [Participle in Genitive]**

Let's look at the Passive Participle ending in Genitive: **-μένου / -μένης / -μένων**.

### Example 1: Given That...
- **Normal Greek:** Επειδή αυτά τα πράγματα είναι δεδομένα... (Since these things are given...)
- **Genitive Absolute:** **Δεδομένων αυτών των πραγμάτων...** (These things being given...)

### Example 2: In the event of...
- **Normal Greek:** Αν γίνει σεισμός... (If an earthquake happens...)
- **Genitive Absolute:** **Γινομένου σεισμού...** (An earthquake happening...)

### Example 3: God willing...
- **Normal Greek:** Αν θέλει ο Θεός... (If God wants...)
- **Genitive Absolute:** **Θεού θέλοντος...** (God willing...)

---

## 3. The Most Common Frozen Expressions

While you can technically build a Genitive Absolute phrasing from scratch, in Modern Greek, people almost exclusively rely on a handful of highly recognizable, frozen expressions. 

As a C2 learner, you simply need to memorize these phrases like vocabulary words!

| The Genitive Absolute | Literal Translation | True Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Θεού θέλοντος** | God willing | Hopefully / God willing |
| **Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων** | Things having well | **All being well / Hopefully** |
| **Μη γένοιτο!** | Let it not happen | **God forbid!** *(Actually a rare optative form, usually grouped here).* |
| **Τουναντίον...** | Of the opposite | **On the contrary...** |
| **Απουσιάζοντος του καθηγητού** | The teacher being absent | **With the teacher absent** |
| **Τρέχοντος του έτους** | The year running | **During the current year** |

::: example-box 
**Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων, θα τελειώσουμε το έργο αύριο.**
(All going well, we will finish the project tomorrow).
::: end
