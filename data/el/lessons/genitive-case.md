# Unit 15: The Genitive Case (Possession)

Welcome to the **B1 Intermediate Stage**. You’ve mastered Subjects (Nominative) and Objects (Accusative). Now, we enter the world of Ownership.

The Genitive case is the Greek equivalent of the English apostrophe "s" ('s) or the word "of". It is how you say "my friend's house" or "the color of the car."

---

## 1. The "Of" Rule (How to Build It)

In English, we usually put the owner first: "George's dog." 
Greek flips this around completely. You must always use the structure: **The Item + of the Owner**. Furthermore, **both** the item and the owner must keep their definite articles!

- **Ο σκύλος του Γιώργου** (George's dog / *The dog of the George*).
- **Το σπίτι της Μαρίας** (Maria's house / *The house of the Maria*).
- **Το χρώμα του αυτοκινήτου** (The car's color / *The color of the car*).

---

## 2. The Genitive Articles

When a person or thing becomes an "Owner," their definite article must change. There is a beautifully consistent pattern here:

| Gender | The Owner (Singular) | The Owners (Plural) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | **του** (too) | **των** (ton) |
| **Feminine** | **της** (tees) | **των** (ton) |
| **Neuter** | **του** (too) | **των** (ton) |

> [!TIP]
> **The Universal Plural (των)**  
> In the Genitive plural, gender doesn't exist! Every single plural owner uses the article **των**, and its noun will perfectly rhyme by ending in **-ων**.
> - **Των φίλων** (of the friends), **των γυναικών** (of the women), **των παιδιών** (of the children). 

---

## 3. The Noun Endings (Singular)

How does the noun itself change to show possession? 

### Masculine Nouns
- If it ends in **-ος**, it changes to **-ου** (ο φίλος ➔ του φίλ**ου**).
- If it ends in **-ας** or **-ης**, simply drop the final **-ς** (ο πατέρας ➔ του πατέρα). *(It looks just like the Accusative!)*

### Feminine Nouns
- Always **add** a final **-ς**! (η Μαρία ➔ της Μαρία**ς**, η τέχνη ➔ της τέχνη**ς**).

### Neuter Nouns
- If it ends in **-ο**, change it to **-ου** (το βιβλίο ➔ του βιβλί**ου**). 
- If it ends in **-ι**, it morphs into a strong **-ιού** (το παιδί ➔ του παιδ**ιού**). 
- If it ends in **-μα**, it bulks up by adding an extra syllable, becoming **-ματος** (το πρόβλημα ➔ του προβλή**ματος**).

> [!CAUTION]
> **The "-ος" Traps**  
> Just like in the plural, you must watch out for the disguised nouns! 
> - **Neuters in -ος:** Words like *το λάθος* (mistake) change to **-ους** in the Genitive! (*το κράτος* ➔ *του κράτους*).
> - **Feminines in -ος:** Words like *η οδός* (street) take the masculine **-ου** ending, but fiercely keep their feminine article! (*η μέθοδος* ➔ *της μεθόδου*).

---

## 4. The Shifting Accent (Pronunciation Secret)

The Genitive case is famous for moving the stress of a word. 

If a masculine or neuter noun has its accent on the **third** syllable from the end (like ο **άν**-θρω-πος), the weight of the Genitive ending is too heavy. The accent is forced to move **one syllable to the right**!

- ο **άν**θρωπος (the person) ➔ του αν**θρώ**που (of the person)
- το **θέ**ατρο (the theater) ➔ του θε**ά**τρου (of the theater)
- το **ό**νομα (the name) ➔ του ο**νό**ματος (of the name)

---

## 5. Beyond Ownership

The Genitive isn't just for physical items. It is used to express almost any relationship between two nouns:
- **Creators:** *η μουσική του Μότσαρτ* (Mozart's music).
- **Measurements & Age:** *ένα παιδί τεσσάρων χρονών* (a four-year-old child).
- **Contents:** *ένα ποτήρι νερού* (a glass of water).

You also learned in Unit 13 that the Genitive is used for **Indirect Objects** (e.g., *Σου δίνω* - I give to you).
