# The Genitive Case (Γενική Πτώση)

You already know the **Nominative Case** (Subjects) and the **Accusative Case** (Objects). 

It is time to learn the third major case: the **Genitive Case**. 

The Genitive case has one primary job in Greek: **Possession**. It is the Greek equivalent of adding an apostrophe "s" ('s) or saying "of the" in English.

---

## 1. How to form the Genitive

When a person or thing becomes an "Owner", both the article and the ending of the noun must change.

### A. The Genitive Articles

Let's review the articles for the Genitive case. They actually have a very strong pattern!

| Gender | Singular Owner | Plural Owners |
|---|:---:|:---:|
| **Masculine** | **του** (of the man) | **των** (of the men) |
| **Feminine** | **της** (of the woman) | **των** (of the women) |
| **Neuter** | **του** (of the child) | **των** (of the children) |

> [!TIP]
> Notice that **του** is used for both masculine and neuter singular owners. 
> Notice that **των** is used for ALL plural owners, regardless of gender! If you see the word **των**, you know instantly that something is owned by multiple people.

### B. The Genitive Noun Endings

How do the actual nouns change when they become owners? Let's look at the singular forms:

1. **Masculine (-ος, -ας, -ης):** The noun drops its ending and becomes **-ου** (for the -ος group) or drops its -ς (for the other groups).
   - Ο φίλος ➔ του φίλ**ου** (of the friend)
   - Ο πατέρας ➔ του πατέρ**α** (of the father)

2. **Feminine (-η, -α):** The noun ALWAYS adds an **-ς** to the end!
   - Η μητέρα ➔ της μητέρ**ας** (of the mother)
   - Η κόρη ➔ της κόρ**ης** (of the daughter)

3. **Neuter (-ο, -ι, -μα):** The noun becomes **-ου** or adds a syllable.
   - Το βιβλίο ➔ του βιβλί**ου** (of the book)
   - Το παιδί ➔ του παιδ**ιού** (of the child)
   - Το μάθημα ➔ του μαθήματ**ος** (of the lesson)

*(Don't stress too much about the plurals right now, but just as a sneak peek: almost all plural owners end in **-ων**! Example: των φίλων, των κοριτσιών).*

---

## 2. Possession in Practice 

In English, we usually put the owner first: "The teacher's book". 
In Greek, you **must** use the "of the" structure: "The book *of the teacher*".

The thing being owned comes first (usually in the Nominative or Accusative), followed by the Owner (in the Genitive). 

Let's look at some examples:

::: example-box 
**English:** The boy's dog. (Or: The dog *of the boy*).
**Greek:** Ο σκύλος **του αγοριού**. 
*(Ο σκύλος is the subject, το αγόρι changed to του αγοριού to show ownership).*
::: end

::: example-box 
**English:** I see Maria's car. (Or: I see the car *of Maria*).
**Greek:** Βλέπω το αυτοκίνητο **της Μαρίας**.
*(Notice how Maria gained an -ς? Η Μαρία ➔ της Μαρίας).*
::: end

::: example-box 
**English:** The color of the house.
**Greek:** Το χρώμα **του σπιτιού**.
::: end

---

## 3. Relationships vs Possession

The Genitive isn't just for owning physical objects; it's also used to express relationships between people, concepts, or parts of a whole.

- **Family:** Η αδερφή **του Γιώργου** (George's sister)
- **Parts:** Η πόρτα **του δωματίου** (The door of the room)
- **Names:** Το όνομα **της δασκάλας** (The teacher's name)
