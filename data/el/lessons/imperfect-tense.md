# The Imperfect Tense (Ο Παρατατικός)

In the last lesson, we learned about **Verbal Aspect**: the difference between a one-time completed event, and a continuous, repeated habit.

When talking about the Past in Greek, Aspect is everything.

In this lesson, we will explore the **Continuous Past** (also known as the Imperfect Tense). This is the tense you use to talk about:
1. Things you *used to do* regularly. ("I used to play tennis.")
2. Things you *were doing* when something else happened. ("I was sleeping when the phone rang.")

Because this tense is continuous, we use the Present Tense stem (the same stem we use for "habitual" actions).

---

## 1. The Rule of the Augment

To make a verb Past Tense, Greek does something very strange: it glues an extra letter onto the *front* of the word. 

This extra letter is called the **Augment**, and it is almost always the letter **ε** (Epsilon). 

Why does Greek do this? The rule is rhythm. Greek demands that the stress mark (the accent) must fall on the **third-to-last syllable** in any past tense verb. 

If the verb is too short (only two syllables long), it doesn't have a third-to-last syllable! So we have to artificially add one to the front.

::: example-box 
**Present:** παίζω (I play) - 2 syllables (παί - ζω)
**Past Step 1:** Add the 'ε' ➔ επαίζω (ε - παί - ζω)
**Past Step 2:** Move the accent ➔ **έ**παιζω
::: end

---

## 2. The Past Endings

We have our augmented stem (**έπαιζ-**). Now we just need the past tense endings!

The endings for the Imperfect Tense (Continuous Past) are identical for everyone, whether it's an action you did once (Simple) or repeatedly (Continuous).

| Pronoun | Past Ending | Example (έπαιζα) |
|---|:---:|---|
| (Εγώ) | **-α** | έπαιζ**α** |
| (Εσύ) | **-ες** | έπαιζ**ες** |
| (Αυτό/ή/ό) | **-ε** | έπαιζ**ε** |
| (Εμείς) | **-αμε** | παίζ**αμε** * |
| (Εσείς) | **-ατε** | παίζ**ατε** * |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-αν(ε)** | έπαιζ**αν(ε)** |

### *Wait, what happened to the 'ε' in We/You?!
Remember the rule: The accent *must* fall on the third-to-last syllable. 

Look at (Εμείς) παίζαμε. 
Let's count the syllables starting from the end: με (1), ζα (2), παί (3). 

The word is already three syllables long! It doesn't need the extra 'ε' at the front anymore! The augment naturally falls off.

---

## 3. Practice Sentences

Let's look at how the Continuous Past is used in real life. Notice how all of these situations describe something that was *ongoing* or a *repeated habit*.

::: example-box 
**Κάθε μέρα έτρεχα στο πάρκο.**
(Every day, I used to run in the park.)
*Because it happened "every day", it was a habit. We use the Continuous Past.*
::: end

::: example-box 
**Ο Γιώργος διάβαζε ενώ η Μαρία έπαιζε.**
(George was reading while Maria was playing.)
*Both actions were ongoing in the background. Continuous Past!*
::: end

::: example-box 
**Δεν ξέραμε.**
(We didn't know.)
*Knowing something is an ongoing state, not a one-time snap. We drop the 'ε' augment because the word is long enough: (ξέ-ρα-με).*
::: end
