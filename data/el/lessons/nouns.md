# Unit 2: The Basics of Nouns & Articles

Now that you can read Greek, it's time to build sentences. In Greek, nouns (people, places, things) are "alive"—they change their shape based on **Gender**, **Case**, and **Number**. 

The most important rule for any Greek student is this:

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Golden Rule**  
> **Never** learn a Greek noun by itself. Always memorize it with its article (**ο, η, το**). The article tells you the gender and how the rest of the sentence must behave.

---

## 1. Grammatical Gender
Every noun in Greek is either Masculine, Feminine, or Neuter. Usually, the last letter of the word tells you the gender. 

However, **grammatical gender does not always correspond to biological sex**. For example, the words for "girl" (*το κορίτσι*) and "boy" (*το αγόρι*) are actually grammatically neuter!

| Gender | Article ("The") | Common Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | ο | -ος, -ας, -ης | ο φίλος (o fílos) | the (male) friend |
| **Feminine** | η | -α, -η | η αγάπη (ee agápi) | the love |
| **Neuter** | το | -ο, -ι, -μα | το σπίτι (to spíti) | the house |

> [!TIP]
> **Heads up! The tricky "-ος" ending**  
> While most nouns ending in -ος are masculine, this is not a universal rule. Some very common ones are **feminine** (like *η οδός* - the street, or *η μέθοδος* - the method). Additionally, many **neuter** nouns also end in -ος (like *το λάθος* - the mistake, or *το κράτος* - the state). Always check the article!

---

## 2. Using "A" or "An" (Indefinite Articles)
When talking about a non-specific thing (a car, a house), use the indefinite article. Notice how the endings often match the noun!

- **Masculine:** **ένας** (*ένας φίλος*) - a friend
- **Feminine:** **μία / μια** (*μια αγάπη*) - a love
- **Neuter:** **ένα** (*ένα σπίτι*) - a house

> [!NOTE]
> **Pronunciation & Spelling Tip:**  
> The feminine *μια* is pronounced as a single syllable, while *μία* is pronounced as two syllables and is generally used when you want to emphasize the number "one". For the masculine and feminine object forms, *έναν* and *μιαν* may optionally drop their final "-ν" depending on the word that follows them.

---

## 3. Intro to Cases: Subject vs. Object
In English, "The man" is always "The man," whether he is doing the action or receiving it. In Greek, there are four cases, meaning the word changes shape depending on its role in the sentence. Today we focus on the two most important:

### The Nominative (The Subject)
The form you find in the dictionary. Used for the person or thing performing the action or being described.
- *Ο πατέρας είναι εδώ.* (The father is here)

### The Accusative (The Object)
Used for the person or thing receiving the action (the Direct Object).
- **Rule for Masculine:** Drop the final **-ς**. The article becomes **τον**. (*Ο πατέρας* -> *τον πατέρα*).
- **Rule for Feminine:** The noun's ending doesn't change. The article becomes **τη(ν)**. 
- **Rule for Neuter:** No changes at all! Neuter nouns and articles (*το*) are exactly the same in both the Subject and Object roles.

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | ο πατέρας | η μητέρα | το παιδί |
| **Object (Acc)** | τον πατέρα | τη(ν) μητέρα | το παιδί |

> [!TIP]
> **The "τη(ν)" Rule:**  
> The feminine article 'την' drops its 'ν' before most consonants. You **must keep the 'ν'** only if the next word starts with a vowel, or with the hard sounds **κ, π, τ, γκ, μπ, ντ, ξ, ψ**. (e.g., *τη Δευτέρα* - on Monday, but *την άνοιξη* - in the spring).

---

## 4. When to use the Definite Article ("The")
Greek uses the definite article much more often than English! 
- **Names take articles:** You don't say "Maria is here." You say "*Η Μαρία είναι εδώ.*" (The Maria is here).
- **Abstract ideas & Classes:** Greek uses "the" for abstract concepts and general categories. You don't say "Fear is an instinct", you say "*Ο φόβος είναι ένστικτο*" (The fear is an instinct).
