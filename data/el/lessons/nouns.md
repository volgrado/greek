# Unit 2: The Basics of Nouns & Articles

Now that you can read Greek, it's time to start building sentences. In English, nouns (words for people, places, and things) are completely static. The word "car" is always "car". 

In Greek, nouns are alive. They change their shape depending on three things: **Gender**, **Number**, and their **Case** (the role they play in the sentence).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Golden Rule of Greek Vocabulary**  
> **Never** learn a Greek noun by itself. Always memorize it with its definite article (the word for "the": **ο, η,** or **το**). The article is the noun's ID card—it tells you the noun's gender and warns you how it will behave in a sentence.

---

## 1. Grammatical Gender

Every noun in Greek belongs to one of three categories: **Masculine**, **Feminine**, or **Neuter**. 

Crucially, **grammatical gender does not equal biological sex**. It's just a category system. For example, the words for "girl" (*το κορίτσι*) and "boy" (*το αγόρι*) are both grammatically neuter!

Usually, the last letter of a noun gives away its gender:

| Gender | The Article ("The") | Typical Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | **ο** (o) | -ος, -ας, -ης | **ο φίλος** (o fílos) | the friend (male) |
| **Feminine** | **η** (ee) | -α, -η | **η αγάπη** (ee agápi) | the love |
| **Neuter** | **το** (to) | -ο, -ι, -μα | **το σπίτι** (to spíti) | the house |

> [!WARNING]
> **The Tricky "-ος" Ending**  
> Most nouns ending in **-ος** are masculine, but there are exceptions! Some very common words are **feminine** (like *η οδός* - the street), and some are **neuter** (like *το λάθος* - the mistake). This is exactly why you must follow the Golden Rule and learn the article!

---

## 2. The Indefinite Articles ("A" or "An")

When you are talking about something non-specific (a car, a house, a friend), you use the indefinite article. Just like the definite article, it has to match the noun's gender.

- **Masculine:** **ένας** (*ένας φίλος* - a friend)
- **Feminine:** **μία / μια** (*μια αγάπη* - a love)
- **Neuter:** **ένα** (*ένα σπίτι* - a house)

> [!TIP]
> **Pronunciation Secret:**  
> The feminine **μια** is pronounced as a single snappy syllable (*mya*). If you see it written as **μία**, it is pronounced as two syllables (*mee-a*) and is usually used when you want to strongly emphasize the number "one" rather than just saying "a".

---

## 3. Introducing Cases: The Subject vs. The Object

In English, "The man" is always "The man," whether he is eating the apple or the apple is hitting him. 

In Greek, a noun actually **changes its shape** depending on what it's doing. This is called a "Case". Today, we focus on the two most important cases:

### The Nominative Case (The Subject)
This is the "dictionary form" of the word. You use this case when the noun is **doing the action**.
- *Ο πατέρας είναι εδώ.* (The father is here.)

### The Accusative Case (The Object)
You use this case when the noun is **receiving the action** (the target of the verb). 

Here is how nouns transform when they become the Object:
- **Masculine Nouns:** Drop the final **-ς**. The article **ο** changes to **τον**. (*Ο πατέρας* becomes *τον πατέρα*).
- **Feminine Nouns:** The noun's ending doesn't change at all! The article **η** changes to **τη(ν)**. 
- **Neuter Nouns:** Total freebie! Neuter nouns and their article (**το**) look *exactly the same* whether they are the Subject or the Object.

| Role | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | **ο** πατέρα**ς** | **η** μητέρα | **το** παιδί |
| **Object (Acc)** | **τον** πατέρα | **τη(ν)** μητέρα | **το** παιδί |

> [!NOTE]
> **The Disappearing "ν" (Nu)**  
> The feminine article **την** often drops its final 'ν'. You **only keep the 'ν'** if the next word begins with a vowel or a hard consonant sound (κ, π, τ, γκ, μπ, ντ, ξ, ψ). So, it's *τη Δευτέρα* (on Monday) but *την άνοιξη* (in the spring).

---

## 4. When to Use "The" 

Greek uses the definite article ("the") constantly—far more often than English. Here are two massive differences you need to know from day one:

1. **Names ALWAYS take articles:** You don't say "Maria is here." You say "*Η Μαρία είναι εδώ.*" (The Maria is here). You don't say "I see Nikos." You say "*Βλέπω τον Νίκο.*" (I see the Nikos).
2. **Abstract Ideas & Broad Categories:** Greek uses the article for general truths. "Fear is an instinct" becomes "*Ο φόβος είναι ένστικτο*" (The fear is an instinct).
