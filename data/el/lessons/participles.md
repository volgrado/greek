# Unit 26: Active & Passive Participles (Οι Μετοχές)

A Participle is a verb dressed up in a costume. It stops acting like a normal verb and starts acting like an adverb (e.g., "He came *running*") or an adjective (e.g., "The *locked* door").

Greek has two powerful participles you must master at the B2 level: the Active Participle (Gerund) and the Passive Perfect Participle.

---

## 1. The Active Participle ("While doing")

This is an uninflected, frozen word used strictly as an **adverb**. It translates to the English "-ing" form. It is used to show *how* someone did something, or to show that two actions are happening at the exact same time.

Because it is an adverb, **it never changes its shape** to match gender or number!

**Formula:** Add the suffix to the Continuous (Present) stem.
- **τρέχω** ➔ **τρέχοντας** (while running / by running).
- **παίζω** ➔ **παίζοντας** (while playing).

> [!CAUTION]
> **Spelling Rule: -οντας vs. -ώντας**  
> How do you know whether to write it with an omicron (ο) or an omega (ω)? It all depends on where you pronounce the stress!
> 1. If you stress the **stem**, use **-ο-** (e.g., τρέχ**ο**ντας, διαβάζ**ο**ντας).
> 2. If you heavily stress the **ending** (like Type B verbs), use **-ώ-** (e.g., αγαπ**ώ**ντας, γελ**ώ**ντας).

- Ο αδελφός της ήρθε **τρέχοντας**. (Her brother came *running*).
- Έφυγε **κλαίγοντας**. (She left *crying*).

---

## 2. The Passive Participle (The Completed State)

Unlike the active participle, the Passive Perfect Participle acts exactly like a normal **adjective** (a chameleon!). It describes a completed state resulting from a past action (e.g., Tired, Broken, Written).

Because it is an adjective, it perfectly agrees with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case!

**Formula:** Add the suffix **-μένος, -μένη, -μένο** to the Passive Simple Stem.
- **κουράζω** (tire) ➔ **κουρασμένος** (tired).
- **χάνω** (lose) ➔ **χαμένος** (lost).
- Η πόρτα είναι **κλεισμένη**. (The door is *closed*).
- Τα **χαμένα** κλειδιά. (The *lost* keys).

> [!TIP]
> **Advanced Spelling: The Consonant "Cheat Codes"**  
> If you remember the consonant groups from Unit 18, you can perfectly predict how to spell these adjectives!
> - **The 'S' Group (ζω, νω, θω):** Uses **-σμένος** (e.g., ξεχνώ ➔ ξεχα**σμ**ένος).
> - **The 'PS' (Lip) Group (πω, βω, φω):** The lips close completely, generating a double 'M': **-μμένος** (e.g., γράφω ➔ γρα**μμ**ένος, κρύβω ➔ κρυ**μμ**ένος).
> - **The 'X' (Throat) Group (κω, γω, χω):** The throat sound softens into a gamma: **-γμένος** (e.g., ανοίγω ➔ ανοι**γμ**ένος).

---

## 3. Native Exceptions

### The "Emotions & States" Exception
Usually, a verb must be able to take a direct object (like "I wash the car") to have a passive form ("The car is washed"). Emotive or bodily verbs (like "I am hungry") shouldn't have a passive form. 

However, Greek famously breaks this rule to create some of the most common adjectives in the language!
- **πεινώ** (I am hungry) ➔ **πεινασμένος** (a hungry person).
- **διψώ** (I am thirsty) ➔ **διψασμένος** (a thirsty person).
- **θυμώνω** (I get angry) ➔ **θυμωμένος** (an angry person).
- **αρρωσταίνω** (I get sick) ➔ **αρρωστημένος** (a sick/morbid person).

### Formal vs. Everyday Verbs (-ευμένος vs -εμένος)
For verbs ending in **-εύω**, there are two valid ways to spell the participle. The formal version keeps the 'υ' (**-ευμένος**), but the informal, everyday street version strips it out completely (**-εμένος**). You should use the everyday version to sound native!
- **παντρεύω** (marry) ➔ **παντρεμένος** (married).
- **μπερδεύω** (confuse) ➔ **μπερδεμένος** (confused).
