# Unit 26: Active & Passive Participles (Οι Μετοχές)

A Participle is a verb dressed up to act like an adjective or an adverb. In English, we use **-ing** (Running) or **-ed** (Locked). Greek has two powerful forms you must master at the B2 level: the Active Participle (Gerund) and the Passive Perfect Participle.

---

## 1. The Active Participle / Gerund (-οντας / -ώντας)
This is an uninflected (frozen) verb form used as an adverb. It translates to the English "-ing" form. It is used to show manner, simultaneous time (two actions happening at the same time), and occasionally cause.

**Formula:** It is formed by adding a suffix to the continuous (imperfective) stem.
*   **τρέχω** ➔ **τρέχοντας** (while running / by running).
*   **παίζω** ➔ **παίζοντας** (while playing).
*   **γελώ** ➔ **γελώντας** (while laughing).

> [!WARNING]
> **Spelling & Stress Rule (-ο vs -ω):**  
> How do you know whether to write **-οντας** (with omicron) or **-ώντας** (with omega)? It all depends on the stress!
> *   If the stress falls on the **stem**, use **-ο-** (e.g., τρέχ**ο**ντας, παίζ**ο**ντας).
> *   If the stress falls on the **ending** (like Type B verbs), use **-ώ-** (e.g., αγαπ**ώ**ντας, γελ**ώ**ντας).

> [!TIP]
> **The Frozen Ending**  
> The active participle is indeclinable. It **never changes** to match gender or number!
*   **Ο αδελφός της ήρθε τρέχοντας.** (Her brother came running).

---

## 2. The Passive Perfect Participle (-μένος)
Unlike the gerund, the passive perfect participle acts exactly like a normal adjective. It describes a completed state resulting from an action (e.g., Tired, Broken, Hidden).

Because it is an adjective, it must perfectly agree with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case.
*   **κουράζω** (tire) ➔ **κουρασμένος** (tired).
*   **χάνω** (lose) ➔ **χαμένος** (lost).
*   **Η πόρτα είναι κλεισμένη.** (The door is closed).
*   **Τα χαμένα λόγια.** (The wasted/lost words).

> [!TIP]
> **Advanced Tip: The Consonant "Cheat Codes"**  
> The passive participle is formed from the *passive perfective stem*. If you remember the consonant groups from Unit 12 and 18, you can predict the exact spelling!
> *   **The 'S' Group (ζω, νω, θω):** Uses **-σμένος** (e.g., ξεχνώ ➔ ξεχα**σμ**ένος).
> *   **The 'PS' (Lip) Group (πω, βω, φω):** The lips close completely, becoming **-μμένος** (e.g., γράφω ➔ γρα**μμ**ένος, κρύβω ➔ κρυ**μμ**ένος).
> *   **The 'X' (Throat) Group (κω, γω, χω):** The throat sound softens to **-γμένος** (e.g., ανοίγω ➔ ανοι**γμ**ένος, τραβώ ➔ τραβη**γμ**ένος).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Native Secret: The "Emotions" Exception**  
> Intransitive verbs (verbs without a direct object) normally do not have passive forms. However, some common verbs related to physical or emotional states *do* have passive perfect participles!
*   **πεινώ** (I am hungry) ➔ **πεινασμένος** (hungry).
*   **διψώ** (I am thirsty) ➔ **διψασμένος** (thirsty).
*   **θυμώνω** (I get angry) ➔ **θυμωμένος** (angry).
*   **αρρωσταίνω** (I get sick) ➔ **αρρωστημένος** (ill).

---

## 3. Formal vs. Informal (-ευμένος vs -εμένος)
Verbs ending in **-εύω** have two participle options. The formal version ends in **-ευμένος** and the informal, everyday version ends in **-εμένος**.
*   **παντρεύω** (marry) ➔ **παντρεμένος** (informal/common) / παντρευμένος (formal).
*   **μπερδεύω** (confuse) ➔ **μπερδεμένος** (confused).
