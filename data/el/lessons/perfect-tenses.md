# The Perfect Tenses (Οι Συντελεσμένοι Χρόνοι)

In Level B1, you learned how to talk about the Past in two ways:
1. **The Imperfect** (I used to play / I was playing) - Continuous
2. **The Aorist** (I played) - Simple

But what if you want to say "**I have played**"? 
This is a very specific type of past. It means you did something in the past, but the result is still important right now. 

This is called the **Present Perfect Tense** (Παρακείμενος), and it is the first of the three "Perfect" tenses.

Good news: The Perfect tenses in Greek are formed *exactly* like they are in English!

---

## 1. The Helper Verb (Έχω)

In English, to make a Perfect tense, you use the helper verb "have" + a past participle: "I **have** eaten."

Greek does the exact same thing. 
You use the verb **έχω** (I have), conjugated for whoever is doing the action.

- (Εγώ) **έχω**
- (Εσύ) **έχεις**
- (Αυτός/ή/ό) **έχει**
- (Εμείς) **έχουμε**
- (Εσείς) **έχετε**
- (Αυτοί/ές/ά) **έχουν**

---

## 2. The Unchanging Ending (-ει)

After the helper verb, you need the actual action word (like "eaten", "written", "played").

In Greek, this word is created using the **Simple Stem** (the Aorist stem you learned in Unit 16) + the ending **-ει**.

And here is the magic: **This word never, ever changes.** It doesn't care if you are a man, a woman, plural, or singular. It is frozen in time. 

Let's use the verb **παίζω** ➔ Simple Stem: **παίξ-**:
The frozen word is **παίξει**.

| Pronoun | The Formula (Helper + Frozen Word) | English |
|---|:---:|---|
| (Εγώ) | **έχω παίξει** | I have played |
| (Εσύ) | **έχεις παίξει** | You have played |
| (Αυτό/ή/ό) | **έχει παίξει** | He/She/It has played |
| (Εμείς) | **έχουμε παίξει** | We have played |
| (Εσείς) | **έχετε παίξει** | You have played |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **έχουν(ε) παίξει** | They have played |

::: example-box 
**Έχω διαβάσει αυτό το βιβλίο.**
(I have read this book.)
::: end

::: example-box 
**Ο Γιώργος έχει αγοράσει ένα αυτοκίνητο.**
(George has bought a car.)
::: end

---

## 3. The Past Perfect (I *had* played)

What if you want to say "I **had** played"? (Before something else happened).

In English, you just change the helper verb to the past tense ("have" ➔ "had"). 
In Greek, you do exactly the same thing! 

Change "έχω" to its continuous past tense (**είχα**). The frozen word (*παίξει*) stays exactly the same!

| Pronoun | The Past Perfect | English |
|---|:---:|---|
| (Εγώ) | **είχα** παίξει | I had played |
| (Εσύ) | **είχες** παίξει | You had played |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **είχε** παίξει | He had played |
| (Εμείς) | **είχαμε** παίξει | We had played |
| (Εσείς) | **είχατε** παίξει | You had played |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **είχαν** παίξει | They had played |

---

## 4. The Future Perfect (I *will have* played)

What if you want to say "I **will have** played" by tomorrow?

Just make the helper verb future tense! (**θα έχω**).
Again, the frozen word never changes!

| Pronoun | The Future Perfect | English |
|---|:---:|---|
| (Εγώ) | **θα έχω** παίξει | I will have played |
| (Εσύ) | **θα έχεις** παίξει | You will have played |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **θα έχει** παίξει | He will have played |
*(And so on...)*

::: example-box 
**Μέχρι αύριο, θα έχω τελειώσει τη δουλειά μου.**
(By tomorrow, I will have finished my work.)
::: end
