# Personal Pronouns (Προσωπικές Αντωνυμίες)

In the lesson on "Είμαι" (I am), you learned the Subject Pronouns (I, You, He, She, We, They). 

You also learned the **Pro-Drop Rule**: Greeks almost never use these words, because the verb ending tells you who is doing the action!

So when *do* we use pronouns? We use them when people are the **Object** of the sentence. 

## 1. Object Pronouns: The Receivers
Just like in English, when a pronoun stops being the "Doer" (Nominative) and becomes the "Receiver" (Accusative), the word changes.

- **English**: **I** see him. He sees **me**.
- **Greek**: **Εγώ** τον βλέπω. Αυτός βλέπει **εμένα**.

In Greek, Object Pronouns come in two flavors: **Strong** and **Weak**.

## 2. Weak Pronouns (The Normal Way)
This is the default way to say "me," "you," "him," etc. in Greek. 

These words are tiny (usually just two letters). They are so weak that they cannot stand on their own. **They must be glued directly in front of the verb.**

| English | Weak Pronoun | Example | Translation |
|---|:---:|---|---|
| Me | **με** | **Με** βλέπεις; | Do you see **me**? |
| You | **σε** | **Σε** θέλω. | I want **you**. |
| Him | **τον** | **Τον** ξέρω. | I know **him**. |
| Her | **τη(ν)** | **Την** αγαπώ. | I love **her**. |
| It | **το** | **Το** έχω. | I have **it**. |
| Us | **μας** | **Μας** ακούνε. | They hear **us**. |
| You (pl) | **σας** | **Σας** ευχαριστώ. | I thank **you**. |
| Them (m) | **τους** | **Τους** βλέπω. | I see **them** (the men). |
| Them (f) | **τις** | **Τις** βλέπω. | I see **them** (the women). |
| Them (n) | **τα** | **Τα** βλέπω. | I see **them** (the kids/things). |

> [!CAUTION]
> **Word Order Warning!**
> In English, the pronoun comes *after* the verb: "I love **you**". 
> In Greek, the weak pronoun MUST come *before* the verb: "**Σε** αγαπώ" (Literally: "You I love"). 
> This feels backwards at first, but your brain will get used to it very quickly!

---

## 3. Strong Pronouns (The Dramatic Way)

Sometimes, you need to emphasize who is receiving the action. Maybe you want to contrast two people, or maybe you are yelling!

For this, Greek uses **Strong Pronouns**. These are longer words, and they are independent—you can put them wherever you want in the sentence, just like in English.

| English | Strong Pronoun |
|---|:---:|
| **ME!** | **εμένα** |
| **YOU!** | **εσένα** |
| **HIM!** | **αυτόν** |
| **HER!** | **αυτή(ν)** |
| **IT!** | **αυτό** |
| **US!** | **εμάς** |
| **YOU ALL!** | **εσάς** |
| **THEM!** | **αυτούς** (m) / **αυτές** (f) / **αυτά** (n) |

### When to use Strong Pronouns?
Only use them when you want to add bold, dramatic emphasis.

::: example-box 
**Situation A: Normal Conversation**
- "Do you love me?"
- Ναι, **σε** αγαπώ. (Yes, I love you). *[Weak pronoun used]*
::: end

::: example-box highlight
**Situation B: The Soap Opera**
- "Do you love Maria?!"
- Όχι! Αγαπώ **εσένα**! (No! I love **YOU**!) *[Strong pronoun used]*
::: end

Notice that in Situation B, the strong pronoun *εσένα* was placed *after* the verb, exactly like in English.
