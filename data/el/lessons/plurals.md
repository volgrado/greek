# Unit 8: Plural Nouns & Adjectives

Welcome to the **A2 Expansion Stage**! You’ve mastered the singular world. Now, it’s time to multiply everything.

In English, making a plural is boring: you just add an "s" to the end of a noun. In Greek, the entire sentence must adapt. When a noun becomes plural, its **Article**, its **Ending**, and its **Adjective** must all change shape to match.

---

## 1. Masculine Plurals (Οι / Τους)

Masculine nouns are the most hyperactive when they become plural. They have different endings depending on whether they are the **Subject** (doing the action) or the **Object** (receiving the action).

| Role | Article | Typical Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | **Οι** (ee) | -οι, -ες | **Οι φίλοι** | The friends |
| **Object (Acc)** | **Τους** (toos) | -ους, -ες | Βλέπω **τους φίλους** | I see the friends |

> [!CAUTION]
> **The -οι vs. -ους Trap**  
> For masculine nouns ending in **-ος** (like *φίλος*), the ending perfectly rhymes with its article!  
> - When they do the action: **Οι** καλ**οί** φίλ**οι** (The good friends).  
> - When they receive it: Βλέπω **τους** καλ**ούς** φίλ**ους** (I see the good friends).

> [!TIP]
> **The "Extra Syllable" Masculines**  
> Many everyday nouns ending in a stressed vowel plus -ς (**-άς, -ής, -ές, -ούς**) bulk up in the plural by adding the extra syllable **-δες**.  
> - ο παπάς -> **οι παπάδες** (the priests)  
> - ο καφές -> **οι καφέδες** (the coffees)

---

## 2. Feminine Plurals (Οι / Τις)

Feminine nouns are much friendlier. The noun ending **never changes** between the subject and the object—only the article flips!

| Role | Article | Typical Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | **Οι** (ee) | -ες | **Οι μητέρες** | The mothers |
| **Object (Acc)** | **Τις** (tees) | -ες | Βλέπω **τις μητέρες** | I see the mothers |

> [!WARNING]
> **The Masked Feminines (-ος)**  
> Remember *η οδός* (the street)? These are feminine nouns masquerading in masculine clothing. In the plural, they take masculine endings (**-οι, -ους**), but they fiercely guard their feminine articles!  
> - Subject: **Οι** οδ**οί** (The streets).
> - Object: **Τις** οδ**ούς** (The streets).

---

## 3. Neuter Plurals (Τα)

Neuter nouns are the most stable of all. Both their article (**τα**) and their noun endings remain exactly the same whether they are hitting the ball or the ball is hitting them. 

| Singular | Plural (Subject & Object) | Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| το βιβλίο | **τα βιβλία** | -ο ➔ -α |
| το παιδί | **τα παιδιά** | -ι ➔ -ια |
| το μάθημα | **τα μαθήματα** | -μα ➔ -ματα |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Ultimate Trap: Neuter Nouns in -ος**  
> There is a huge group of neuter nouns ending in **-ος** (like *το λάθος* - the mistake, *το κράτος* - the state). In the plural, they drop the -ος and add **-η**.  
> Do **NOT** confuse these with masculine nouns! 
> - *το λάθος* ➔ **τα λάθη** (the mistakes).  
> - *το δάσος* ➔ **τα δάση** (the forests).

---

## 4. The Final Equation: Full Agreement

When the noun goes plural, everyone else must follow. The Subject, Adjective, and Verb must agree.

*   **Singular:** **Ο** καλ**ός** φίλ**ος** διαβάζ**ει**. (The good friend reads).
*   **Plural (Masc):** **Οι** καλ**οί** φίλ**οι** διαβάζ**ουν**. (The good friends read).
*   **Plural (Neut):** **Τα** μικρ**ά** παιδι**ά** παίζ**ουν**. (The small children play).
