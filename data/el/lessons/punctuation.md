# Punctuation Marks (Σημεία Στίξης)

You have reached the final piece of the modern Greek writing system: Punctuation. Good news—Greek punctuation is almost identical to English! 

There are only **two major differences** you need to memorize, and they are both guaranteed to trip you up at least once.

---

## 1. The Greek Question Mark (;)

This is the most notorious "gotcha" in the entire language. 
In Greek, **the English semicolon (;) is used as the question mark.**

The English question mark `(?)` does not exist in Greek at all. If you type it, Greeks will be very confused!

::: content-card
**English**: How are you?
**Greek**: Πώς είσαι**;** *(Pós íse?)*
::: end

> [!TIP]
> **Typing Tip**
> If you have a Greek keyboard installed on your phone or computer, you don't need to do anything special. Just hit the key where the "Q" key usually is (on mobile) or the standard semicolon key (on desktop).

---

## 2. The Ano Teleia (·)

Because Greek stole the semicolon to use as a question mark, what do they use when they actually want a semicolon (a pause between two related sentences)?

They use the **Ano Teleia** (Άνω Τελεία), which translates to "Upper Dot". It looks exactly like a period that has floated up to the middle of the line: `·`

It functions exactly like an English semicolon (;) or a colon (:).

::: content-card
**English**: I want water; I am thirsty.
**Greek**: Θέλω νερό**·** διψάω.
::: end

> [!NOTE]
> **Don't Stress About Typing It**
> The Ano Teleia is very rare in casual texting or daily writing. Most Greeks just use a comma or a period instead because the `·` symbol is physically hard to type on most keyboards! You just need to recognize it when reading books or articles.

---

## 3. The Quotation Marks (« »)

Greek uses the exact same period (`.`), comma (`,`), and exclamation mark (`!`) as English. 

However, when quoting speech or a title, Greek traditionally uses **angular quotation marks**, also known as *guillemets*.

::: content-card
**English**: He said "hello".
**Greek**: Είπε **«**γεια**»**.
::: end

In modern digital typing, standard English quotation marks (" ") are becoming more common, but you will still see the angular ones in all printed books and news articles.
