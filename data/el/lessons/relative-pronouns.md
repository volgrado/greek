# Unit 23: Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

Relative pronouns are the glue that links two sentences together. Instead of sounding like a robot ("I saw a man. The man was tall"), you use them to create flow: "I saw a man **who** was tall." 

In Greek, you have two tools for this: the easy, casual way, and the formal, precise way.

## 1. The Easy Way: Που

The word **που** is the ultimate chameleon of the Greek language. It is completely "frozen"—it never changes its shape, regardless of gender, number, or whether you are talking about a human or a chair! It translates to who, which, or that.

- **Ο άντρας που** είδα... (The man *who/whom* I saw...)
- **Το σπίτι που** ζήσαμε... (The house *that/where* we lived...)
- **Η μπύρα που** πίνω... (The beer *which* I am drinking...)

> [!CAUTION]
> **Spelling Alert (που vs. πού;)**  
> Never confuse the relative pronoun **που** with the question word **πού;** (Where?). The question word *always* carries an accent mark; the relative pronoun *never* does. 

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: The "Echo" Pronoun**  
> Because **που** never changes shape, the sentence can sometimes become confusing. To fix this, Greeks frequently add an extra, weak "echo" pronoun *inside* the second half of the sentence to clarify who is doing what!
> - **Η γιατρός που μου τη σύστησες.** (The doctor **who** you recommended **_her_** to me).
> - **Ο φίλος που του έδωσα το βιβλίο.** (The friend **who** I gave the book **_to him_**).

---

## 2. The Formal Way: Ο οποίος

If you are writing an essay, giving a speech, or reading the news, you will encounter the formal phrase **ο οποίος** (who / which). 

Unlike the frozen *που*, this phrase is a massive chameleon. It consists of the definite article (*ο, η, το*) plus the word *οποίος*. It must perfectly match the noun it refers to in Gender and Number!
- **Ο άντρας ο οποίος...** (The man who...)
- **Η γυναίκα η οποία...** (The woman who...)
- **Τα παιδιά τα οποία...** (The children who...)

### How to say "Whose" (Possession)
To say "whose", you simply use the Genitive (Ownership) form of this formal pronoun: **του οποίου, της οποίας, των οποίων**. 
- **Ο φίλος μου, του οποίου η μητέρα είναι δασκάλα...** (My friend, **whose** mother is a teacher...).

---

## 3. The Preposition Rule

Why do we need both *που* and *ο οποίος*? Because of prepositions! 

When you need to use a preposition (like "with," "from," or "in") right before the relative pronoun, the formal **ο οποίος** is strongly preferred in writing.
- Αυτοί είναι οι άνθρωποι **από τους οποίους** πήραμε πληροφορίες. (These are the people **from whom** we got information).
- Το κτήριο **στο οποίο** μένω. (The building **in which** I live).

*(In casual street speech, Greeks bypass this by using **που** plus the "Echo" pronoun trick: Το κτήριο που μένω μέσα...).*

---

## 4. "Whoever" & "Whatever"

If you aren't referring to a specific person or thing, you use the "free" relative pronouns:

- **Όποιος / Όποια / Όποιο:** Whoever. *(e.g., Όποιος θέλει, ας έρθει. - Whoever wants to, let him come).*
- **Ό,τι:** Whatever / That which. *(e.g., Κάνε ό,τι θέλεις. - Do whatever you want).*

> [!WARNING]
> **Spelling Alert (ότι vs. ό,τι)**  
> Notice the comma inside **ό,τι** (whatever). It is absolutely mandatory in writing! It proves you are saying "whatever" and not the conjunction **ότι** (which just means "that," like *Ξέρω ότι είσαι εδώ* - I know that you are here).

### Maximum Emphasis (-δήποτε)
If you want to sound incredibly emphatic (Anyone at all! Absolutely anything!), add the suffix **-δήποτε**.
- **Οποιοσδήποτε:** Anyone / Whoever it may be.
- **Οτιδήποτε:** Anything / Whatever it may be.
