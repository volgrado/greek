# The Simple Subjunctive (Η Απλή Υποτακτική)

In Level A2 (Unit 10), you learned how to translate sentences like "I want *to go*" by using the particle **να** + a conjugated verb: "Θέλω **να** πάω".

As you might have guessed by now, the Subjunctive Mood has two Aspects.

1. **Continuous Subjunctive:** Θέλω να παίζω. 
2. **Simple Subjunctive:** Θέλω να παίξω. 

Look at how similar the Subjunctive is to the Future tense you just learned!
- What particle do you use for the Future? **Θα**. 
- What particle do you use for the Subjunctive? **Να**.
- Does everything else stay exactly the same? **Yes!**

---

## 1. When do I use the Simple Subjunctive?

This gives English speakers a lot of trouble because we don't have aspect in the infinitive. "I want to eat" is the only option in English.

In Greek, you must ask yourself: "Do I want to perform this action over and over again as a habit, or do I want to do it right now and finish it?"

**90% of the time, when we talk about wanting or needing to do something, we want to do it ONCE.**

Look at these two scenarios:

::: example-box 
**Scenario A: The Dreamer (Continuous)**
Θέλω να **παίζω** πιάνο.
(I want to *be playing* the piano. / I want to *be a piano player*.)
*Ongoing state/habit ➔ Continuous Stem.*
::: end

::: example-box 
**Scenario B: The Concert (Simple)**
Θέλω να σου **παίξω** ένα τραγούδι.
(I want to *play* you a song.)
*One-time completed action ➔ Simple Stem.*
::: end

Because we usually ask people to do specific, single actions ("Can you read this?", "I need to sleep"), **the Simple Subjunctive is overwhelmingly more common in daily speech.** You will hear it constantly!

---

## 2. Let's Practice Assembling It

The recipe for the Simple Subjunctive is exactly the same as the Simple Future.

**Να** + **Simple Stem** + **Present Endings**.

Let's use the verb **διαβάζω** (I read) ➔ Simple Stem: **διαβάσ-**.

| Pronoun | The Formula | Final Result | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| (Εγώ) | θέλω να + διαβάσ- + -ω | **Θέλω να διαβάσω** | I want to read |
| (Εσύ) | πρέπει να + διαβάσ- + -εις | **Πρέπει να διαβάσεις** | You must read |
| (Αυτός) | μπορεί να + διαβάσ- + -ει | **Μπορεί να διαβάσει** | He can read |
| (Εμείς) | θέλουμε να + διαβάσ- + -ουμε | **Θέλουμε να διαβάσουμε** | We want to read |
| (Εσείς) | πρέπει να + διαβάσ- + -ετε | **Πρέπει να διαβάσετε** | You must read |
| (Αυτοί) | μπορούν να + διαβάσ- + -ουν | **Μπορούν να διαβάσουν** | They can read |

*(Remember: the first verb, like Θέλω or Μπορώ, must also be conjugated to match the pronoun!)*

---

## 3. The Ultimate Rule: NO AUGMENTS

It is very tempting to look at the Aorist (Past Tense) word for "I wrote" (**έγραψα**) and try to use it with **να**.

**Do not do this!** 

Never say: *Θέλω να έγραψα*. 

The Augment (**έ-**) is strictly a Past Tense marker. It means "this happened in the past". You cannot want to do something in the past! 

You must strip away the augment, take the pure Simple Stem (**γράψ-**), and use the Present Tense endings (**-ω, -εις, -ει...**).
➔ **Θέλω να γράψω.**

---

## 4. The Final Form: Negative Sentences

How do you translate "I don't want to go"? 

You know that **δεν** makes verbs negative. But you just learned that **να** also sits right in front of the verb. 

Wait, two particles? **δεν** + **να**? No! 

Greek has a special rule: You cannot use **δεν** directly next to **να**. Instead, the negative particle **δεν** transforms into a special word: **μη(ν)**.

### The Μην Rule
Whenever you want to negate a Subjunctive verb, use **μη(ν)** instead of **δεν**. 

- Θέλω να **μην πάω**. (I want to *not go*).
- Πρέπει να **μην καπνίζεις**. (You must *not smoke*).

*(Just like δεν and την, μην drops its final 'ν' if the next word starts with a soft consonant).*
