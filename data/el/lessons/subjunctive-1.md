# The Subjunctive Mood (Η Υποτακτική)

Welcome to a uniquely Greek problem.

In English, we have something called the **infinitive**. It's the base form of a verb, usually starting with "to". 
- I want **to go**.
- We need **to sleep**.
- They can **to run** (wait, we say "they can run", but you get the idea).

Here is the problem: **Greek does not have an infinitive.** 

There is no word for "to go". There is only "I go", "You go", "He goes". 

So how do you translate a sentence like "I want to go"?

---

## 1. The Magic Word: Να 

Because Greek verbs are always conjugated (they always have an ending pointing to a person), Greek solves the "to go" problem by using the little word **να** (na).

**Να** acts like the English word "to". But instead of being followed by a base verb, it is followed by a fully conjugated verb!

Let's look at the literal translation:
- **English:** I want **to go**.
- **Greek (Literal):** I want **that I go**.
- **Greek (Actual):** Θέλω **να** πάω.

Let's try another one:
- **English:** Do you want **to play**?
- **Greek (Literal):** Do you want **that you play**?
- **Greek (Actual):** Θέλεις **να** παίζεις;

Notice what happened there? Both verbs have to match the person doing the action! 
If *I* want, and *I* am the one going, then BOTH verbs must end in **-ω**: (Θέλ**ω** να πά**ω**).
If *You* want, and *You* are the one playing, BOTH verbs must end in **-εις**: (Θέλ**εις** να παίζ**εις**;).

This is called the **Subjunctive Mood**.

---

## 2. When to Use "Να"

You will use **να** constantly. Anytime you have two verbs stacked next to each other in English, you will almost certainly need a **να** separating them in Greek.

Here are the most common verbs that trigger the Subjunctive:
- **Θέλω να...** (I want to...)
- **Μπορώ να...** (I can...)
- **Πρέπει να...** (I must...)
- **Ξέρω να...** (I know how to...)

::: example-box 
**Μπορούμε να** κάνουμε κάτι;
(Can we do something?)
*Notice the matching endings: Bορ**ούμε** and κάν**ουμε**.*
::: end

::: example-box 
**Ξέρει να** μιλάει αγγλικά.
(He knows how to speak English.)
*Notice the matching endings: Ξέρ**ει** and μιλά**ει**.*
::: end

---

## 3. The Standalone "Na" (Commands & Wishes)

The word **να** is so powerful that it doesn't even need a first verb! 

If you start a sentence with **να**, it turns the verb into a strong suggestion, a wish, or a mild command.

- **Να** προσέχεις! (Take care! / Careful!)
- **Να** είσαι καλά. (Be well. / *The most common way to say thank you/goodbye!*)
- **Να** πάμε; (Shall we go?)

### The Ultimate Question: Τι να κάνω;
If you are confused, lost, or asking for advice, this is the only phrase you need: **Τι να κάνω;** (What should I do? / literally: What that I do?).
