# Unit 10: Verbal Aspect & The Subjunctive (Να)

Welcome to the great divide in the Greek language. To move from A1 to A2, you must understand a concept called **Aspect**. 

English cares heavily about *when* an action happens (Past, Present, Future). Greek cares far more about *how* it happens. This concept is the key to unlocking the entire rest of the language.

---

## 1. The "Video vs. Photo" Concept 

Whenever a Greek person describes an action, their brain forces them to choose between two lenses:

1. **The Video (Continuous Aspect):** Used for habits, routines, or actions that are currently in progress. You are watching the action happen over time. *(e.g., "I play every day" or "I am playing right now").*
2. **The Photo (Simple Aspect):** Used for a single, specific, or completed event. You view the entire action as a single snapshot. *(e.g., "I played one game" or "I want to play once").*

Because of this, almost every Greek verb has **two different stems**:
- **Continuous Stem:** The one you already know from the Present tense (e.g., **παίζ-**).
- **Simple Stem:** A new stem used for single events (e.g., **παίξ-**). We will learn exactly how to build this in the next unit, but you need to understand *why* it exists first!

---

## 2. The Subjunctive Mood (The Magic Word: Να)

Greek has no "infinitive" form of verbs. You cannot say "I want **to play**." 

Instead, Greek uses a special mood called the Subjunctive. To build it, you use the tiny magic word **να** (which roughly means "that") followed by a conjugated verb. You literally say: "I want *that I play*."

| English | The Greek Logic | Greek Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I want **to go.** | I want *that I go*. | Θέλω **να πάω**. |
| Do you want **to play?** | Want you *that you play*? | Θέλεις **να παίζεις**; |
| He can **help.** | He can *that he helps*. | Μπορεί **να βοηθήσει**. |

### When to use Να:
- **After "Want" & "Can":** After verbs like **θέλω** (I want), **μπορώ** (I can), and **πρέπει** (I must).
- **As a Command / Wish:** You can start a sentence with **Να** to give an order or offer a wish. (*Να προσέχεις!* - Take care! / You should be careful!).
- **To Ask Permission:** (*Να μπω;* - May I come in?).

> [!TIP]
> **Bonus Magic Word: Ας (Let's / Let)**  
> While *να* is the standard, Greek also uses **ας** to express suggestions, permission, or indifference. 
- *Ας πάμε!* (Let's go!)
- *Ας φύγει.* (Let him go / I don't care if he goes.)

---

## 3. The Golden Rule of Negation (Μη vs. Δεν)

This is one of the most unbreakable rules in Greek grammar:

Normal statements (the Indicative mood) are negated with **δεν**. You learned this in Unit 4. (I *do not* want = *δεν* θέλω). 

**However, any verb introduced by να or ας MUST be negated by μη(ν).**

- Θέλω **να μην** έρθει. (I want him *not* to come.)
- Καλύτερα **να μην** πάμε. (It's better that we *don't* go.)
- **Ας μην** μιλήσουμε γι' αυτό. (Let's *not* talk about this.)

*(Note: μη(ν) follows the exact same "dropping 'N'" pronunciation rules you learned for την and δεν!)*

---

## 4. Bringing It Together: Aspect in the Subjunctive

Since **να** is followed by a verb, you must choose which "Aspect" (Video or Photo) to use for that verb! Because the verb has two stems, you have two choices:

- **Θέλω να παίζω** *(Continuous/Video Stem)*: I want to *be playing* (regularly, as a hobby, or continuously).
- **Θέλω να παίξω** *(Simple/Photo Stem)*: I want to *play* (this specific game, right now, just once).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Secret of the Subjunctive**  
> 90% of the time, when you want to do something, or must do something, you are talking about a specific, single event. Therefore, **the Simple (Photo) Stem is by far the most commonly used stem after να!**
