# Advanced Subjunctive Usage

In Level A2 and B1, you learned the golden rule of the Subjunctive: 
**Whenever you want to use an "infinitive" (to go, to eat), you use the word `να` and a conjugated verb instead.**
- Θέλω **να πάω** (I want to go).

But the Subjunctive Mood in Greek is far more powerful than just replacing the infinitive. It is the mood of emotion, subjectivity, doubt, and fear.

In Level C1, you must learn the "hidden" triggers of the subjunctive.

---

## 1. Verbs of Emotion (Χαίρομαι που / να)

When you express an emotion about a fact, you typically use **που** (that).
- Χαίρομαι **που** ήρθες. (I am glad **that** you came).

However, when you express an emotion about *doing an action*, you use the Subjunctive (**να**).
- Χαίρομαι **να** σε βλέπω. (I am glad **to** see you).
- Μ' αρέσει **να** πίνω καφέ. (I like **to** drink coffee).

---

## 2. Expressions of Fear (Φοβάμαι μη)

This is one of the strangest rules for English speakers. 

When you are afraid that something *bad* will happen, Greek uses the negative subjunctive particle **μη(ν)** instead of **να**!

In English, you say: "I am afraid **that** it will rain".
In Greek, you say: "I am afraid **lest** it rains" (Φοβάμαι **μην** βρέξει).

- Φοβάμαι **μην** πέσεις. (I am afraid you might fall).
- Φοβάται **μην** αργήσουμε. (He is afraid we might be late).

Why? Because the underlying logic is: "I am afraid [and I wish that you do] **not** fall."

*(Note: If you are afraid of a confirmed, existing fact, you just use "που" or "ότι": Φοβάμαι **ότι** έχεις δίκιο - I am afraid **that** you are right).*

---

## 3. Doubt and Uncertainty (Αμφιβάλλω αν)

When expressing doubt, Greek doesn't use "να". It uses the conditional word **Αν** (If / Whether).

- Δεν ξέρω **αν** θα έρθει. (I don't know **if/whether** he will come).
- Αμφιβάλλω **αν** είναι αλήθεια. (I doubt **if/whether** it is true).

---

## 4. Final Clauses (Purpose: Για να)

When you want to say "In order to...", you must use **Για να** + Subjunctive.

- Πάω στην Ελλάδα **για να** μάθω τη γλώσσα. 
(I am going to Greece **in order to** learn the language).

- Μιλάω αργά **για να** καταλάβεις.
(I am speaking slowly **so that** you understand).

Notice in the second example, the subjects are different (I am speaking, so that YOU understand). In English, this requires clunky grammar. In Greek, "Για να" handles it effortlessly just by conjugating the second verb!

---

## 5. Indefinite Pronouns + Subjunctive

Finally, the Subjunctive is heavily triggered by "Whatever", "Whoever", and "Wherever" phrases when referring to an unknown future event.

- Ό,τι και **να** γίνει... (Whatever happens...)
- Όπου και **να** πας... (Wherever you go...)
- Όποιος και **να** είσαι... (Whoever you are...)
