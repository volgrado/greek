# Unit 4: Regular Verbs & Negation (Type A)

You’ve learned the nouns; now it’s time to make things happen. Verbs are the workhorses of the Greek sentence. They change their shape to tell you who is doing the action and when.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Verb
Every Greek verb is formed by the combination of a **stem** and an **inflectional ending**. 

Take the verb "to play": **παίζω** (pé-zo).
- **The Stem is παίζ-** (it carries the essential meaning of the verb).
- **The Ending is -ω** (it tells you the person, number, tense, and voice).

Almost all Greek verbs in the dictionary end in **-ω**. This always means "I" am doing the action. Because the Greek verbal ending clearly shows variation in person and number, a subject pronoun becomes redundant and is usually dropped.

---

## 2. Verb Groups (The Conjugations)
Before conjugating, you must know which "team" or conjugation a verb belongs to. Look at the accent mark in the dictionary form:

- **Type A (First-Conjugation):** The accent is on the last syllable of the stem (NOT on the -ω). 
    - *Examples:* πα**ί**ζω, κά**ν**ω, γρά**φ**ω, διαβά**ζ**ω
- **Type B (Second-Conjugation):** The accent is on the final vowel of the ending (-ώ or -άω).
    - *Examples:* αγαπ**ώ** (αγαπ**ά**ω), μιλ**ώ** (μιλ**ά**ω)
- **Type C (Deponent/Passive Verbs):** These verbs have no active forms and end in -ομαι instead of -ω, but they are active in meaning.
    - *Examples:* έρχ**ομ**αι (I come), γίν**ομ**αι (I become)

In this unit, we focus exclusively on **Type A (First-Conjugation) verbs**.

---

## 3. Conjugation (Present Tense)
To change the person (I, you, we...), chop off the -ω and add these endings:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: διαβάζω (I read) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ω** | διαβάζ**ω** |
| (Εσύ) | **-εις** | διαβάζ**εις** |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-ει** | διαβάζ**ει** |
| (Εμείς) | **-ουμε** | διαβάζ**ουμε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-ετε** | διαβάζ**ετε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ουν(ε)** | διαβάζ**ουν(ε)** |

> [!NOTE]
> **Grammar & Pronunciation Tips:**  
> - **Fixed Stress:** For Type A verbs in the active present tense, the stress remains on the exact same syllable throughout all forms (δια**βά**ζω, δια**βά**ζεις, δια**βά**ζουμε, etc.). 
> - **The extra 'ε':** The 3rd person plural form with the extra *-ε* (διαβάζουνε) is very frequent in the spoken colloquial language, but less often used in formal writing.
> - **He = They?:** In English, "He is" and "They are" use different words. In Greek, for irregular verbs like *Είμαι*, they are the exact same word (*είναι*). But for regular verbs like *διαβάζω*, 'They' (-ουν) is distinctly different from 'He' (-ει).

---

## 4. Negation (Δεν vs. Όχι)
To say "I do not," just put the word **δεν** (den) right before the verb. 

- **Δεν παίζω** (I don't play)
- **Δεν κάνω** (I don't do/make)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Δεν vs. Όχι**  
> - Use **Όχι** to answer "No" to a question.
> - Use **Δεν** right before an indicative verb to say "not" (**Δεν καταλαβαίνω** — I don't understand).
> - *Example:* "Όχι, δεν παίζω" (No, I don't play).

> [!TIP]
> **Speak like a Native: Blending "Δεν"**  
> When the final /n/ of the negative particle **δεν** is followed by a word beginning with a hard consonant like **π, τ,** or **κ**, the sounds blend together!
> - δεν + παίζω sounds like *dem-bézo*.
> - δεν + τολμώ sounds like *den-dolmó*.
> - δεν + καταλαβαίνω sounds like *deng-gatalavéno*.
