# Unit 4: Regular Verbs & Negation (Type A)

You’ve learned how to talk about people and things using nouns. Now it’s time to make things happen. Verbs are the engine of every Greek sentence. They change their shape to tell you exactly **who** is doing the action and **when** they are doing it.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Greek Verb

Every Greek verb is built like a puzzle with two pieces: a **stem** and an **ending**. 

Take the verb "to play": **παίζω** (pé-zo).
- **The Stem is παίζ-** (This carries the core meaning: "play").
- **The Ending is -ω** (This tells you *who* is playing and *when*).

Almost all Greek verbs you find in a dictionary will end in **-ω**. This means "I" am doing the action right now. Because the ending tells you exactly who is acting, you usually drop the subject pronoun (Εγώ, Εσύ, etc.), as you learned in Unit 3.

---

## 2. The Three Verb "Teams"

Before you conjugate a verb, you need to know which "team" it plays for. You can figure this out instantly just by looking at where the accent mark falls in the dictionary word.

- **Type A (First Conjugation):** The accent falls on the **stem** (anywhere before the final -ω). 
    - *Examples:* **παίζω** (I play), **κάνω** (I do), **γράφω** (I write).
- **Type B (Second Conjugation):** The accent falls on the **final vowel** (-ώ). We will cover these in a later unit.
    - *Examples:* **αγαπώ** (I love), **μιλώ** (I speak).
- **Type C (Passive/Deponent):** These end in **-ομαι**. We will tackle these in the intermediate course.

In this unit, we are going to master the most common verbs in the language: **Type A verbs**.

---

## 3. Conjugating Type A Verbs (Present Tense)

To change the person (I, you, we, etc.), simply chop off the **-ω** and attach a new ending. 

Let's conjugate **διαβάζω** (I read / I study):

| Pronoun | The Ending | Full Verb | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **(Εγώ)** | **-ω** | διαβάζ**ω** | I read |
| **(Εσύ)** | **-εις** | διαβάζ**εις** | You read |
| **(Αυτός/ή/ό)** | **-ει** | διαβάζ**ει** | He/She/It reads |
| **(Εμείς)** | **-ουμε** | διαβάζ**ουμε** | We read |
| **(Εσείς)** | **-ετε** | διαβάζ**ετε** | You read (pl./formal) |
| **(Αυτοί/ές/ά)** | **-ουν(ε)** | διαβάζ**ουν(ε)** | They read |

> [!TIP]
> **Stress-Free Stress:**  
> The beauty of Type A verbs is that in the present tense, the accent mark NEVER moves. If it's on the 'ά' in διαβ**ά**ζω, it stays on the 'ά' for every single form (διαβ**ά**ζουμε, διαβ**ά**ζετε, etc.). Just lock in the rhythm!

> [!NOTE]
> **The Extra 'ε':** Just like with the verb "to have" (έχουν/έχουνε), Greeks frequently add an extra 'ε' to the 3rd person plural in spoken language (**διαβάζουνε**). It makes the language flow more smoothly.

---

## 4. How to Say "No" (Δεν vs. Όχι)

In English, turning a positive sentence negative is a clunky process ("I play" -> "I do not play"). In Greek, you simply drop the word **δεν** (den) right before the verb!

- **Παίζω.** (I play.)
- **Δεν παίζω.** (I don't play.)
- **Δεν ξέρω.** (I don't know.)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Δεν vs. Όχι**  
> Never mix these up! 
> - Use **Όχι** to answer a question with "No." (Are you going? *Όχι.*)
> - Use **Δεν** right before a verb to mean "Not." (I am *not* going = *Δεν* πάω.)
> - *Combo:* "Όχι, δεν παίζω." (No, I don't play.)

> [!CAUTION]
> **Speak like a Native: Blending "Δεν"**  
> When the "ν" (n) of **δεν** crashes into a hard consonant like **π, τ,** or **κ**, a phonetic magic trick happens. They blend to create the B, D, and G sounds!
> - δεν + πειράζει sounds exactly like *dem-birázi* (It doesn't matter).
> - δεν + ξέρω sounds like *den-gzéro* (I don't know).
