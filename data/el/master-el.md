# Unit 5: The Accusative Case & Basic Prepositions

In Unit 2, you learned that when a noun is the **Object** (receiving the action), it drops its final **-ς** (if masculine) and its article becomes **τον, την,** or **το**. 

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Masculine 'N'**  
> We **always** keep the final **-ν** in the masculine article (**τον**) to avoid confusion with the neuter article (**το**). This is true even if the next word starts with a soft sound!
Now, we will see the Accusative Case in action through verbs and the "Big Three" prepositions.

---

## 1. Verbs that use the Accusative
Most "action" verbs require the object to be in the Accusative case.

- **Βλέπω τον** Γιώργο. (I see George).
- **Έχω την** τσάντα. (I have the bag).
- **Διαβάζω το** βιβλίο. (I read the book).

> [!CAUTION]
> Remember: The verb **Είμαι** (I am) is special. It acts as a mirror, so it keeps everything in the **Nominative** (ο, η, το).  
> - **Είμαι ο Γιώργος.** (I am George).

---

## 2. The Big Three Prepositions (σε, από, με)
In Greek, almost all prepositions force the noun that follows them into the Accusative case.

### A. Σε (To / At / In)
This word shows location or direction. In Greek, we almost **never** say "σε" and "the" separately. They merge into one word:

- σε + τον = **στον**
- σε + την = **στη(ν)**
- σε + το = **στο**

*Example:* **Πάω στο σινεμά.** (I am going to the cinema).

### B. Από (From)
Used for origins. It does **not** merge with the article, but the article must still be in the Accusative.

- **Είμαι από την Ελλάδα.** (I am from Greece).
- **Έρχομαι από τον γιατρό.** (I am coming from the doctor).

> [!TIP]
> **Blend it!** Even though it's written separately, Greeks often blend them when speaking quickly (**από το** sounds like "ap-to")!

### C. Με (With)
Used for company or instruments.

- **Είμαι με τη Μαρία.** (I am with Maria).
- **Θέλω καφέ με ζάχαρη.** (I want coffee with sugar).

---

## 3. Pronunciation: The Dropping 'N'
While the masculine articles (**τον, στον, έναν**) **always** keep their **-ν** in writing to avoid confusion with the neuter, the feminine words (**την, στην**) and particles (**δεν, μην**) are more selective. They only keep the **-ν** if the next word starts with:
1. A vowel (**α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω**)
2. A hard consonant (**κ, π, τ, ξ, ψ**)
3. A digraph (**μπ, ντ, γκ, τσ, τζ**)

- **Στη** δασκάλα (*The teacher - soft 'δ'*)
- **Στην** πόρτα (*The door - hard 'π'*)

If you aren't sure, it is usually safer to include the **-ν**—native speakers will still understand you perfectly!

# Unit 25: Adjective Comparison (Better, Faster)

In Greek, there are two ways to compare things (e.g., "tall" ➔ "taller"). You can use the easy "more" method or the specialized "suffix" method.

---

## 1. The Easy Way: Πιο + Adjective
This is currently the most common way to speak. Just put the word **πιο** (more) in front of the adjective.

- **Μεγάλος** (Big) ➔ **Πιο μεγάλος** (Bigger)
- **Ωραίος** (Beautiful) ➔ **Πιο ωραίος** (More beautiful)

To say "than," use the word **από**.

- Ο Γιώργος είναι **πιο ψηλός από** τον Νίκο. (George is taller than Nikos).

---

## 2. The Native Way: The -τερος Suffix
If you want to sound more traditional or formal, you add the suffix **-τερος, -τερη, -τερο** to the stem of the adjective.

- **Ψηλός** ➔ **Ψηλότερος** (Taller)
- **Νέος** (Young) ➔ **Νεότερος** (Younger)
- **Ωραίος** ➔ **Ωραιότερος** (More beautiful)

---

## 3. Superlatives (The Best)
To say "the most" or "the best," you simply add the **Definite Article** (ο, η, το) in front of your comparison.

- **Ο πιο καλός** (The best / The most good)
- **Ο καλύτερος** (The better one / The best one)

---

## 4. The "Very" Superlative: -τατος
If you want to say something is "extremely" or "very, very" X, use the ancient suffix **-τατος**.

- **Ωραιότατος** (Extremely beautiful)
- **Φθηνότατος** (Super cheap)

---

## 5. The Top 3 Irregulars
These three are used in every sentence. You must learn their "native" comparison forms:

1. **Καλός** (Good) ➔ **Καλύτερος** (Better)
2. **Κακός** (Bad) ➔ **Χειρότερος** (Worse)
3. **Πολύς** (Much) ➔ **Περισσότερος** (More)

---

- [ ] I have memorized the irregulars **καλύτερος** and **χειρότερος**.
- [ ] I know that **ο + comparison** makes it "the most."

# Unit 6: Adjectives, Colors & Numbers

In English, adjectives (describing words) are simple: the word "good" never changes. In Greek, adjectives are **chameleons**. They must perfectly match the noun they describe in **Gender**, **Number**, and **Case**. We call this **Agreement**.

---

## 1. The Chameleon Rule
Every Greek adjective in the dictionary has three versions (Masculine, Feminine, Neuter). Notice how the endings often "rhyme" with the noun!

| Focus | Masculine (-ος) | Feminine (-η/-α) | Neuter (-ο) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Example** | **Ο καλός** πατέρας | **Η καλή** μητέρα | **Το καλό** παιδί |
| **Logic** | The good father | The good mother | The good child |

When you use an adjective as an object (Accusative Case), it must flex with the noun.  

- **Subject**: Ο καλός φίλος.
- **Object**: Βλέπω **τον καλό** φίλο. (The adjective drops the **-ς** just like the noun!)

---

## 2. Colors (Τα Χρώματα)
Most colors are regular adjectives. They help you practice agreement every day.

- **Κόκκινος, κόκκινη, κόκκινο** (Red)
- **Μαύρος, μαύρη, μαύρο** (Black)
- **Πράσινος, πράσινη, πράσινο** (Green)

> [!TIP]
> **Foreign Colors**: Some colors borrowed from other languages do NOT change.  
> **Μπλε** (Blue), **Ροζ** (Pink), and **Καφέ** (Brown) stay the same regardless of gender!  
> - Ο μπλε ουρανός, η μπλε θάλασσα, το μπλε αυτοκίνητο.

---

## 3. Numbers 1-10 (Αριθμοί 1-10)
Learning to count is the key to daily survival.

1. **Ένας / Μία / Ένα** (One)
2. **Δύο** (Two)
3. **Τρία** (Three)
4. **Τέσσερα** (Four)
5. **Πέντε** (Five)
6. **Έξι** (Six)
7. **Επτά** (Seven)
8. **Οκτώ** (Eight)
9. **Εννέα** (Nine)
10. **Δέκα** (Ten)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The number 1 is a chameleon!**  
> Good news: the number "One" is the exact same word as the **indefinite articles** (a/an) you learned in Unit 2! It must match the gender of what you are counting:  
> - **Ένας** άντρας (One man)  
> - **Μία** γυναίκα (One woman)  
> - **Ένα** παιδί (One child)
> 
> *Fun Fact: The numbers **3** and **4** are also mini-chameleons! You will learn their masculine/feminine forms later, but for now, use **Τρία** and **Τέσσερα** when counting out loud.*

---

# Unit 17: Adverbs & Expressions of Manner

Adverbs are words that describe **how**, **where**, **when**, or **how much** an action happens. In Greek, adverbs are "frozen"—they never change their shape, which makes them very easy to learn.

---

## 1. Manner: How?
These describe the "vibe" of an action.

- **Σιγά** (Slowly / Quietly)
- **Γρήγορα** (Quickly)
- **Καλά** (Well)
- **Μαζί** (Together)
- **Έτσι** (Like this / So)

---

## 2. Place: Where?
- **Εδώ** (Here)
- **Εκεί** (There)
- **Πάνω** (Up / On top)
- **Κάτω** (Down / Below)
- **Μέσα** (Inside)
- **Έξω** (Outside)

---

## 3. Time: When?
- **Τώρα** (Now)
- **Σήμερα** (Today)
- **Αύριο** (Tomorrow)
- **Χθες** (Yesterday)
- **Πάντα** (Always)
- **Ποτέ** (Never)

> [!CAUTION]
> **Spelling Alert: πότε vs ποτέ**  
> Be careful with the accent!  
> - **Πότε;** (Accent on first syllable) = **When?**  
> - **Ποτέ** (Accent on last syllable) = **Never.**

---

## 4. Quantity: How much?
- **Πολύ** (Very / Much)
- **Λίγο** (A little)
- **Αρκετά** (Enough / Quite)
- **Καθόλου** (Not at all / At all)

# Unit 14: The Past Tenses (Imperfect vs. Aorist)

Welcome to the capstone of the A2 Expansion stage. You’ve learned how to build stems and how to talk about the future. Now, we look back. In Greek, just like in the future, we have two ways to talk about the past.

---

## 1. The Two Past Tenses
1. **The Aorist (Simple Past)**: A one-time completed action. (e.g., "I played"). **Uses the Simple Stem.**
2. **The Imperfect (Continuous Past)**: An ongoing habit or background action. (e.g., "I was playing"). **Uses the Continuous Stem.**

---

## 2. Past Tense Conjugation
Both past tenses use the same set of endings. The difference is the **Stem** and the **Augment**.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: **γράφω** |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Εγώ** | **-α** | έγραφ-**α** |
| **Εσύ** | **-ες** | έγραφ-**ες** |
| **Αυτός/ή/ό** | **-ε** | έγραφ-**ε** |
| **Εμείς** | **-αμε** | γράφ-**αμε** |
| **Εσείς** | **-ατε** | γράφ-**ατε** |
| **Αυτοί/ές/ά** | **-αν** | έγραφ-**αν** |

> [!TIP]
> **The Augment (έ-)**  
> Notice the **έ-** at the beginning. This is a marker of the past. It only appears when the word is short (1 or 2 syllables). In **Εμείς** and **Εσείς** forms, the word gets too long, and the augment drops off!

---

## 3. Side-by-Side: Video vs. Photo
Let's use the verb **παίζω** (I play).

- Continuous Stem: **παίζ-**
- Simple Stem: **παίξ-**

### A. The Imperfect (The Video)
- **Έπαιζα** τένις κάθε μέρα. (I used to play tennis every day.)
- Use this for background info: "While I **was playing**, it started to rain."

### B. The Aorist (The Photo)
- **Έπαιξα** τένις χθες. (I played tennis yesterday—once).
- Use this for specific events: "I **went** to the park."

---

## 4. The 5 Absolute Irregulars
These are the most common verbs in the past. You must memorize them:
1. **βλέπω** (see) ➔ **είδα** (I saw)
2. **λέω** (say) ➔ **είπα** (I said)
3. **πάω** (go) ➔ **πήγα** (I went)
4. **έχω** (have) ➔ **είχα** (I had - *Imperfect only*)
5. **είμαι** (be) ➔ **ήμουν** (I was - *Imperfect only*)

> [!CAUTION]
> **No Photo for Be & Have**  
> The verbs **έχω** and **είμαι** do not have an Aorist (Photo) form. They only exist in the Imperfect (Video) past tense. You cannot say "I was" or "I had" as a single, completed dot in time in Greek.

---

# The Articles (Τα Άρθρα)

In Greek, almost every noun must be preceded by an **article** (the little words for "the" or "a/an").

You cannot ignore them. If you drop the article in a Greek sentence, you will sound like a caveman ("Me go store. Buy apple.")

The article is the anchor of the sentence. It tells the listener the **gender** (male/female/neuter) and the **number** (singular/plural) of the noun that is about to follow.

---

## 1. The Definite Article ("The")

We use the definite article when we are talking about a *specific* thing (e.g., "The car is red").

In English, we only have one word for this: **The**.

In Greek, "The" shape-shifts based on the gender of the noun. You already learned these in the previous lesson!

| Gender    | "The" (Singular) | Example                                        | Translation |
| --------- | :--------------: | ---------------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Masculine |   **ο**   | **ο** δρόμος *(o dró-mos)*     | the street  |
| Feminine  |   **η**   | **η** γυναίκα *(ee yi-né-ka)* | the woman   |
| Neuter    |  **το**  | **το** παιδί *(to pe-dí)*      | the child   |

> [!CAUTION]
> **Names Take Articles!**
> In English, we never put "The" in front of someone's name. You don't say "The Maria is here."
> In Greek, **you absolutely must.**
>
> - **Ο Γιώργος** είναι εδώ. (George is here).
> - **Η Μαρία** είναι εδώ. (Maria is here).

---

## 2. The Indefinite Article ("A / An")

We use the indefinite article when we are talking about a *non-specific* thing (e.g., "I want a car").

Just like "The", the Greek word for "A" changes based on the gender.

| Gender    |         "A / An"         | Example                                               | Translation |
| --------- | :-----------------------: | ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Masculine |    **ένας**    | **ένας** δρόμος *(é-nas dró-mos)* | a street    |
| Feminine  | **μία / μια** | **μια** γυναίκα *(mya yi-né-ka)*   | a woman     |
| Neuter    |     **ένα**     | **ένα** παιδί *(é-na pe-dí)*        | a child     |

Notice how the indefinite articles share the same endings as the nouns they describe? **ένας** ends in *-ας* (masculine), **μία** ends in *-α* (feminine), and **ένα** ends in *-α* (neuter rule-breaker, but very easy to remember).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Object Form (Accusative)**  
> Just like "The" (*ο* ➔ *τον*), the masculine "A" drops its "s" when it receives the action: **ένας** ➔ **έναν**.  
> - **Έχω έναν σκύλο** (I have a dog).  
> 
> Good news: The feminine (**μία/μια**) and neuter (**ένα**) forms **never change**!  
> - **Έχω μια γάτα** (I have a cat).

> [!TIP]
> **μία vs. μια**
> You will see the feminine article spelled both ways.
>
> - **Μία** *(mí-a)* has two distinct syllables. It is slightly more formal and is also used as the number "1".
> - **Μια** *(mya)* is a single syllable. It is used in fast, casual speech.
>   They mean exactly the same thing.

---

## Example Sentences

Let's look at how replacing "The" with "A" changes the meaning exactly as it does in English.

::: example-box
**Ο** φίλος είναι εδώ. ➔ **The** (specific) friend is here.
**Ένας** φίλος είναι εδώ. ➔ **A** (random) friend is here.
::: end

::: example-box
Θέλω **το** βιβλίο. ➔ I want **the** (specific) book.
Θέλω **ένα** βιβλίο. ➔ I want **a** (random) book.
::: end

---

# Unit 1: The Greek Writing System Masterclass

Welcome to your first step toward mastering Greek! As an English speaker, learning the Greek alphabet is much easier than you might think. Over 25% of English vocabulary has Greek roots—you already know more Greek than you realize.

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to read and write Modern Greek: Single Letters, Digraphs (Sound Combinations), Accents, and Punctuation.

---

## 1. The 24 Letters

We break the alphabet into three groups based on their similarity to English.

### Team A: The "Freebies"

These look and sound exactly like English.

|     Letter     | Name    | Sound    | Example              |
| :-------------: | :------ | :------- | :------------------- |
| **Α α** | Alpha   | `[a]`  | **f*a*ther** |
| **Ε ε** | Epsilon | `[e]`  | **p*e*t**    |
| **Ι ι** | Iota    | `[ee]` | **m*ee*t**   |
| **Κ κ** | Kappa   | `[k]`  | **k*i*te**   |
| **Μ μ** | Mu      | `[m]`  | **m*o*m**    |
| **Ν ν** | Nu      | `[n]`  | **n*o***     |
| **Ο ο** | Omicron | `[o]`  | **t*o*p**    |
| **Τ τ** | Tau     | `[t]`  | **t*o*p**    |
| **Ζ ζ** | Zeta    | `[z]`  | **z*oo***    |

### Team B: The "False Friends"

These *look* like English letters but sound different. **Watch out for these!**

|     Letter     | Name    | Sound    | Warning                                |
| :-------------: | :------ | :------- | :------------------------------------- |
| **Β β** | Beta    | `[v]`  | Like**v*a*n**, not 'B'         |
| **Η η** | Eta     | `[ee]` | Like**m*ee*t**, not 'H'        |
| **Ρ ρ** | Rho     | `[r]`  | Rolled**R**, not 'P'             |
| **Χ χ** | Chi     | `[h]`  | Guttural**H** (Lo**ch**)   |
| **Υ υ** | Upsilon | `[ee]` | Like**m*ee*t**, not 'Y' or 'U' |

### Team C: The "Aliens"

Unique shapes, but simple sounds.

|       Letter       | Name   | Sound     | Example                                                                                       |
| :-----------------: | :----- | :-------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|   **Γ γ**   | Gamma  | `[g/y]` | **y** in **y*es*** (before **e, i**); A soft guttural sound, like a soft French 'r' or Spanish 'g' in 'fuego' otherwise |
|   **Δ δ**   | Delta  | `[th]`  | Soft **th** in **th*i*s**                                                      |
|   **Θ θ**   | Theta  | `[th]`  | Hard**th** in **th*i*nk**                                                       |
|   **Λ λ**   | Lambda | `[l]`   | **l*i*on**                                                                            |
|   **Ξ ξ**   | Xi     | `[ks]`  | **x** in **bo*x***                                                              |
|   **Π π**   | Pi     | `[p]`   | **p*en***                                                                             |
| **Σ σ, ς** | Sigma  | `[s]`   | **s*un*** (use **ς** at end of words)                                          |
|   **Φ φ**   | Phi    | `[f]`   | **f*ish***                                                                            |
|   **Ψ ψ**   | Psi    | `[ps]`  | **ps** in **o*ps***                                                             |
|   **Ω ω**   | Omega  | `[o]`   | Exactly like**Omicron**                                                                 |

---

## 2. Digraphs (Letter Combinations)

Sometimes Greek glues two letters together to make a new sound.

### Vowel Twins

|        Combinations        | Sound    | Example                                       |
| :------------------------: | :------- | :-------------------------------------------- |
|       **αι**       | `[e]`  | **αι**τία *(etía)* - cause      |
| **ει, οι, υι** | `[ee]` | **εί**μαι *(í-me)* - I am       |
|       **ου**       | `[oo]` | **ου**ρανός *(ouranós)* - sky |

### Consonant Twins (The Hard Sounds)

Over centuries, the original B, D, and G sounds in Greek softened. Modern Greek created these new combinations to get those hard sounds back!

|     Combinations     | Sound            | Example                                               |
| :-------------------: | :--------------- | :---------------------------------------------------- |
|    **μπ**    | **B**      | **μπ**α**μπ**άς *(babás)* - dad |
|    **ντ**    | **D**      | **ντ**ύνω *(díno)* - to dress           |
| **γκ / γγ** | **G**      | ά**γκ**υρα *(ágira)* - anchor          |
|    **τσ**    | **TS**     | **τσ**έπη *(tsépi)* - pocket            |
|    **τζ**    | **J / DZ** | **τζ**άκι *(dzáki)* - fireplace         |

---

## 3. Accents & Punctuation

Greek is highly phonetic. One mark changes everything.

### The Tonos (´)

Every word with 2+ syllables has one accent mark on a vowel. This tells you exactly where to put the stress.

- **νερό** (ne-RÓ) - water
- **πρόβλημα** (PRÓ-vli-ma) - problem

### The Question Mark (;)

In Greek, the English semicolon (`;`) is the question mark. The English mark (`?`) is never used.

- **Πώς είσαι;** (How are you?)

### The Upper Dot (·)

Used like an English semicolon or colon for a long pause.

- **Θέλω νερό· διψάω.** (I want water; I am thirsty.)

# Unit 21: Conditionals (The 'If' Clauses)

Conditional sentences are "If X, then Y" statements. In Greek, the word for "If" is **Αν** (An). There are three main ways to build these, depending on how likely the situation is.

---

## 1. Type 1: The Real (Possible)
Use this for facts, plans, or likely events. Just use the normal Present or Future tenses.

**Formula: Αν [Present/Future], then [Future/Command]**

- **Αν βρέχει**, θα μείνω σπίτι. (If it rains, I will stay home).
- **Αν θέλεις**, έλα μαζί μου. (If you want, come with me).

---

## 2. Type 2: The Unreal (Dreaming)
Use this for hypotheticals, dreams, or giving advice. Greek pushes both verbs into the **Continuous Past (Imperfect)**.

**Formula: Αν [Past], then Θα + [Past]**

- **Αν είχα** λεφτά, **θα αγόραζα** ένα πλοίο. (If I had money [now], I would buy a boat).
- **Αν ήμουν** εσύ, **θα του έλεγα** "ναι". (If I were you, I would say yes).

---

## 3. Type 3: The Regret (Past Unreal)
Use this for things that never happened in the past. It’s too late!

**Formula: Αν [Past Perfect], then Θα + [Past Perfect]**

- **Αν είχα δει** εσένα, **θα είχα πει** γεια. (If I had seen you, I would have said hello).
- **Αν είχε διαβάσει**, θα είχε περάσει. (If he had read, he would have passed).

---

# Unit 18: Passive Voice & Deponent Verbs (-ομαι)

Welcome to **Type C** verbs. These verbs don’t end in **-ω**; they end in **-ομαι** *(o-me)*. This ending system is used for two very different things in Greek: the **Passive Voice** and **Deponent Verbs**.

---

## 1. What are Deponent Verbs?
Some of the most common verbs in Greek *look* passive but are actually **active**. These are called Deponent Verbs. They only exist in the -ομαι form.

| Verb | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **έρχομαι** | I come | Από πού **έρχεσαι**; |
| **γίνομαι** | I become / I happen | Τι **γίνεσαι**; (How are you?) |
| **χρειάζομαι** | I need | **Χρειάζομαι** βοήθεια. |
| **σκέφτομαι** | I think | Τι **σκέφτεσαι**; |

---

## 2. Present Tense Conjugation
Both Passive and Deponent verbs use these "Type C" endings:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: **έρχομαι** |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ομαι** | έρχ**ομαι** |
| (Εσύ) | **-εσαι** | έρχ**εσαι** |
| (Αυτό/ή/ό) | **-εται** | έρχ**εται** |
| (Εμείς) | **-όμαστε** | ερχ**όμαστε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-εστε** | έρχ**εστε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ονται** | έρχ**ονται** |

> [!TIP]
> **The Accent Shift**: Notice that in the "We" form (**ερχόμαστε**), the accent mark jumps forward. This is a very common rhythmic rule in Greek!

---

## 3. The True Passive Voice
You can also take an active verb (like **πλένω** - I wash) and flip it to the passive form (**πλένομαι** - I am washed / I wash myself).

- **Active**: Πλένω το αυτοκίνητο. (I wash the car).
- **Passive**: **Πλένομαι**. (I am washing myself / I am taking a bath).

### "By Someone" (Από)
To say "by" in a passive sentence, use **από** + Accusative.

- **Το βιβλίο διαβάζεται από τον Γιώργο.** (The book is read by George).

---

## 4. Past Passive (Aorist)
The past tense of many passive verbs is marked by the letter **Θ** (Theta) and the endings **-ηκα, -ηκες, -ηκε**.

- **Active Past**: Έπλυνα (I washed).
- **Passive Past**: **Πλύθηκα** (I was washed / I bathed).

> [!CAUTION]
> **Not Always Theta!**  
> While **-θηκα** is common, many everyday verbs change their stem to use **στ, φτ,** or **χτ** before the **-ηκα** ending. For example:
> - **ετοιμάζω** ➔ **ετοιμάστηκα** (I got ready)
> - **κρύβω** ➔ **κρύφτηκα** (I hid myself)

---

- [ ] I know that verbs ending in **-ομαι** can be either Active (Deponent) or Passive/Reflexive.
- [ ] I recognize the **-ηκα** ending as a marker of the Past Passive, even if the consonant before it changes.

# Diminutives & Augmentatives (Υποκοριστικά & Μεγεθυντικά)

Greek is a language of intense emotion. You don't just drink a coffee; you drink a *little coffee* with a friend. You don't just see a dog; you see a *huge, monstrous dog*.

To express these feelings of smallness, affection, or enormous size, Greek uses special suffixes attached to the end of nouns.

---

## 1. The Diminutives (Making things small or cute)

Diminutives are incredibly common in spoken Greek. They are used to make things sound smaller, cuter, more polite, or more affectionate. 

When you order food, you rarely order a "τυρόπιτα" (cheese pie). You order a "τυροπιτάκι" (little cheese pie), because it sounds friendlier!

### Suffix 1: -άκι (The universal tiny-maker)
This is by far the most common diminutive. It can be added to almost any noun, and it **always turns the word into a Neuter noun** (Το).

- Το σπίτι (the house) ➔ Το σπιτ**άκι** (the little house)
- Το παιδί (the child) ➔ Το παιδ**άκι** (the little child)
- Η τυρόπιτα (the cheese pie) ➔ Το τυροπιτ**άκι** (the little cheese pie)
- Ο καφές (the coffee) ➔ Το καφεδ**άκι** (the little coffee)

*(Notice how even masculine and feminine nouns become neuter when you add -άκι!)*

### Suffix 2: -ίτσα / -ούλα (For Feminine Nouns)
If you want to keep a word feminine while making it cute, you can use these suffixes. 

- Η πόρτα (the door) ➔ Η πορτ**ίτσα** (the little door)
- Η αγάπη (the love) ➔ Η αγαπ**ούλα** μου (my sweet little love)
- Η Ελένη (Eleni) ➔ Η Ελεν**ίτσα** (Little Eleni)

### Suffix 3: -ούλης / -άκος (For Masculine Nouns)
If you want to make a masculine person or animal sound cute or endearing.

- Ο παππούς (the grandfather) ➔ Ο παππ**ούλης** (the sweet old grandpa)
- Ο Γιώργος (George) ➔ Ο Γιωργ**άκος** (Little George)

---

## 2. The Augmentatives (Making things HUGE)

Sometimes you need to emphasize that something is massive, impressive, or even a bit scary. 

For this, Greek uses Augmentative suffixes. These suffixes **always turn the noun into a Masculine noun** (Ο), or sometimes Feminine (Η) for certain words.

### Suffix 1: -αρος (The Giant)
This is the most common masculine augmentative.

- Το σκυλί (the dog) ➔ **Ο** σκύλ**αρος** (the massive beast of a dog)
- Το αυτοκίνητο (the car) ➔ **Ο** αυτοκινητ**άρας** (the huge, impressive car)
- Ο άντρας (the man) ➔ **Ο** αντρ**αρος** (the big, burly man)

### Suffix 2: -κλα (The Huge Feminine)
This suffix creates a sense of enormous, sometimes overwhelming size.

- Η πόρτα (the door) ➔ Η πορτά**κλα** (the gigantic door)
- Η φωνή (the voice) ➔ Η φωνά**κλα** (the huge, booming voice)

---

## 3. The Power of Emotion

Remember, these suffixes aren't just about physical size. They are about *emotion*. 

If a friend invites you for a `<καφεδάκι>` (little coffee), they aren't talking about a small cup. They are talking about a relaxed, friendly, intimate chat. 

If someone calls their new car an `<αυτοκινητάρας>`, they are expressing pride and awe. 

Mastering these suffixes is the key to sounding like a true native speaker!

---

## 🎯 Wrap-Up Checklist

- [ ] I know that **-άκι** makes words small/cute, and always turns them into **Το** (Neuter).
- [ ] I know the feminine diminutives **-ίτσα** and **-ούλα**.
- [ ] I know the masculine diminutives **-ούλης** and **-άκος**.
- [ ] I know that **-αρος** and **-κλα** are used to make things sound completely massive!

# Unit 11: Future Tenses (Θα)

In Unit 10, you learned the magic of **Aspect**. Now, we apply that "Video vs. Photo" logic to the future. In Greek, we don't just say "I will play"; we specify if it's a habit or a one-time thing.

---

## 1. The Future Particle: Θα
Just like *να* and *δεν*, the word **θα** sits right in front of the verb. It translates to the English "will."

- **English**: I will play.
- **Greek**: **Θα** [verb].

---

## 2. Continuous Future (The Video)
If you want to talk about a habit, a routine, or something you will be doing repeatedly in the future, use the **Continuous Stem** (the Present tense stem).

**Formula: Θα + Present Tense Verb**

- **Θα διαβάζω** κάθε μέρα. (I will [be reading] every day.)
- **Θα έχουμε** χρόνο. (We will have [ongoing] time.)

---

## 3. Simple Future (The Photo)
If you are talking about a single, specific event that will happen and finish, use the **Simple Stem** (Aorist Stem). 

**Formula: Θα + Simple Stem + Present Endings**

- **Θα διαβάσω** το βιβλίο αύριο. (I will read the book tomorrow—*once*).
- **Θα παίξουμε** τένις. (We will play tennis—*one game*).

> [!CAUTION]
> **No Past Markings!**  
> Even though we use the Simple (Aorist) stem, do NOT add the past tense augment (the prefix **ε-**) to the future. It’s *θα γράψω*, never *θα έγραψα*.

---

## 4. The Negation Combo
To say "I will not," put **δεν** before **θα**.

- **Δεν θα** παίζω (I won't be playing regularly).
- **Δεν θα** παίξω (I won't play this specific time).

---

- [ ] I know how to negate the future with **δεν θα**.
- [ ] I understand that the future uses the present tense endings (-ω, -εις, -ει...).

# Unit 15: The Genitive Case (Possession & Relationships)

You’ve mastered **Subjects** (Nominative) and **Objects** (Accusative). Now, we enter the world of **Ownership**. The Genitive case is the Greek equivalent of the English apostrophe "s" ('s) or the word "of."

---

## 1. The Genitive Articles
When a person or thing becomes an "Owner," the article must change. There is a very consistent pattern here:

| Gender | Singular Owner | Plural Owners |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Masculine** | **του** | **των** |
| **Feminine** | **της** | **των** |
| **Neuter** | **του** | **των** |

> [!TIP]
> **The Universal Plural**  
> In the Genitive plural, gender doesn't exist! Every single plural owner uses the article **των** and ends in **-ων**.  
> - Των φίλων, των δασκάλων, των παιδιών.

---

## 2. Noun Endings (Singular)
How does the noun itself change?

- **Masculine (-ος, -ας, -ης)**: Drops the final **-ς**. (e.g., ο πατέρας ➔ **του πατέρα**). *Nouns in -ος change to -ου.*
- **Feminine (-η, -α)**: **Always adds an -ς**. (e.g., η Μαρία ➔ **της Μαρίας**).
- **Neuter (-ο, -ι)**: Nouns ending in **-ο** change to **-ου** (το βιβλίο ➔ **του βιβλίου**). Nouns ending in **-ι** change the final vowel to a diphthong **-ιού** (το παιδί ➔ **του παιδιού**). *Note: Only neuter nouns ending in **-μα** actually add a syllable (το πρόβλημα ➔ **του προβλήματος**).*

---

## 3. Possession: The "Of" Rule
In English, we say "The teacher's book." In Greek, you must say "The book **of the** teacher." The object being owned comes first!

- **Ο σκύλος του Γιώργου** (George's dog / The dog of George).
- **Το σπίτι της Μαρίας** (Maria's house / The house of Maria).
- **Το χρώμα του αυτοκινήτου** (The car's color / The color of the car).

---

## 4. Indirect Objects (To Me / For Me)
As you learned in Unit 13, the Genitive also marks the **Receiver** of an action.

- **Μου** δίνεις... (Give **to me**...)
- **Του** λέω... (I am telling **him**...)

---

- [ ] I know that Greek structures possession as "The Item of the Owner."
- [ ] I can identify the Genitive case as both an "Owner" and a "Receiver."

# Unit 28: Idioms & Ancient Remnants

To reach true fluency (B2/C1), you must understand the "ghosts" of the past. Modern Greek is filled with ancient expressions and colorful idioms that don't follow modern rules.

---

## 1. Ancient Dative Expressions
Ancient Greek had a "Dative" case (for/to) that vanished in modern times, but these frozen phrases survived. You must learn them as whole blocks:

- **Εν τω μεταξύ** (In the meantime).
- **Δόξα τω Θεώ** (Thank God / Glory to God).
- **Εν πάση περιπτώσει** (In any case / Anyway).
- **Τοις μετρητοίς** (In cash).

---

## 2. Advanced Prepositions (Genitive)
While basic prepositions take the Accusative, formal/ancient ones used in news and business take the **Genitive**.

- **Λόγω** + Genitive (Due to): **Λόγω βροχής** (Due to rain).
- **Μέσω** + Genitive (Via): **Μέσω Αθηνών** (Via Athens).
- **Ενώπιον** + Genitive (Before / In front of): **Ενώπιον του νόμου** (Before the law).

---

## 3. Colorful Idioms
These are phrases where the literal meaning is completely different from the actual meaning.

- **Έγινε της κακομοίρας!** (A disaster happened! / Hell broke loose!)
- **Χάνω τα λόγια μου.** (I am lost for words).
- **Πού το πας;** (What are you getting at? / Where are you going with this?)
- **Περαστικά!** (Get well soon! / May it pass!)

---

## 4. Compound "Lego" Words
Greek builds new words by snapping smaller ones together with the letter **"ο"** as glue.

- **βιβλίο** (book) + **πωλείο** (shop) ➔ **βιβλιοπωλείο** (bookstore).
- **ψάρι** (fish) + **ταβέρνα** ➔ **ψαροταβέρνα** (fish tavern).

# Unit 16: The Imperative Mood (Giving Orders)

The Imperative Mood is used to give commands, orders, or strong advice. In Greek, just like in the Future and Past, giving a command is deeply tied to **Aspect** (Video vs. Photo).

---

## 1. Simple Commands (The Photo: Do it once!)
This is the most common way to give a command. You use the **Simple Stem** (Aorist) that you learned in Unit 12.

| Person | Ending | Example: **γράφω** ➔ **γράψ-** |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Εσύ** (Informal) | **-ε** | **Γράψε!** (Write!) |
| **Εσείς** (Formal/Plural) | **-τε** | **Γράψτε!** (Write!) |

*Examples:*

- **Κλείσε** την πόρτα! (Close the door!)
- **Διαβάστε** το βιβλίο! (Read the book!)

---

## 2. Continuous Commands (The Video: Keep doing it!)
Used for habits or ongoing actions. You use the **Continuous Stem** (Present).

- **Τρέχε!** (Keep running!)
- **Διάβαζε!** (Keep reading [as a habit]!)

> [!TIP]
> **Formal/Plural Continuous**: This form is identical to the normal Present tense!  
> - **Τρέχετε!** (Keep running, everyone!)

---

## 3. The 5 Absolute Irregulars
Like most languages, the most common commands in Greek are irregular. You MUST memorize these:

1. **Λέω** (Say) ➔ **Πες! / Πείτε!**
2. **Βλέπω** (Look) ➔ **Δες! / Δείτε!**
3. **Βρίσκω** (Find) ➔ **Βρες! / Βρείτε!**
4. **Πίνω** (Drink) ➔ **Πιες! / Πιείτε!**
5. **Έρχομαι** (Come) ➔ **Έλα! / Ελάτε!**

---

## 4. Negative Commands: Μην
To say "Don't," you use the magic particle **μην** (instead of *δεν*) followed by the Subjunctive form.

- **Μην κλείσεις** την πόρτα. (Don't close the door—this time).
- **Μην τρέχεις.** (Don't run—habitually).

---

# Unit 27: Impersonal Verbs & Expressions

Sometimes a sentence doesn't have a specific "person" doing the action (e.g., "It is raining" or "It is necessary"). In Greek, we use **Impersonal Verbs** which are frozen in the **3rd Person Singular**.

---

## 1. The Power Pair: Πρέπει & Μπορεί
You’ve used these since A1, but now you understand *why* they work this way. They almost always trigger the Subjunctive (**να**).

- **Πρέπει να...** (It is necessary that... / **Must**)
- **Μπορεί να...** (It is possible that... / **Might**)

> [!WARNING]
> **Μπορώ vs Μπορεί**  
> - **Μπορώ** να πάω. (I can go—I have the ability).  
> - **Μπορεί** να πάω. (I might go—it is a possibility).

---

## 2. Essential Intermediate Verbs
These verbs describe allowed actions, appearances, or values. 

| Greek Verb | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Απαγορεύεται** | It is forbidden | **Απαγορεύεται** το κάπνισμα. |
| **Επιτρέπεται** | It is allowed | **Επιτρέπεται** να μπεις. |
| **Φαίνεται** | It seems | **Φαίνεται** ότι θα βρέξει. |
| **Αξίζει** | It is worth | **Αξίζει** να το δεις. |

---

## 3. Impersonal "Είναι" Expressions
You can turn any adjective into a command or observation by using **Είναι** (It is) + **να**.

- **Είναι δύσκολο να...** (It is difficult to...)
- **Είναι κρίμα να...** (It is a pity to...)
- **Είναι λογικό να...** (It makes sense to...)

---

# Unit 7: Asking Questions (Τι, Πού, Ποιος)

You now have a solid vocabulary and know how to conjugate verbs. The final step in your A1 foundation is learning how to ask for things. In Greek, this involves two parts: a special punctuation mark and "Question Words."

---

## 1. The Greek Question Mark (;)
The first thing you must memorize is that the English semicolon (`;`) is the Greek question mark. The English question mark (`?`) does *not* exist in Greek.

- **Πώς είσαι;** (How are you?)
- **Τι κάνεις;** (What are you doing?)

---

## 2. The Fixed "W" Words
These words are easy because they never change their shape.

- **Τι** *(ti)* = **What?** (e.g., Τι είναι αυτό;)
- **Πού** *(poo)* = **Where?** (e.g., Πού είσαι;)
- **Πότε** *(pó-te)* = **When?** (e.g., Πότε φεύγουμε;)
- **Πώς** *(pos)* = **How?** (e.g., Πώς είσαι;)
- **Γιατί** *(ya-tí)* = **Why?** (e.g., Γιατί λες όχι;)

> [!TIP]
> **Accent Mystery:** You learned in Unit 1 that 1-syllable words don't get accents. **Πού** and **Πώς** are the rare exceptions! They keep the accent only to differentiate them from their twins *που* (that/who) and *πως* (that).

---

## 3. The Chameleon "W" Words
Just like adjectives, some question words must match the gender of the noun you are asking about.

### "Who" or "Which" (Ποιος)
*Pronounced as one syllable: **pyos***

- **Ποιος** είναι ο άντρας; (Who is the man?)
- **Ποια** είναι η γυναίκα; (Who is the woman?)
- **Ποιο** παιδί; (Which child?)

> [!NOTE]
> **Accusative Alert!** If "Who" is the object receiving the action, it drops its **-ς** just like the nouns you learned in Unit 2: **Ποιον βλέπεις;** (*Who(m) do you see?*)

### "How Much" or "How Many" (Πόσος)
- **Πόσο κάνει;** (How much does it cost?)
- **Πόσα** παιδιά έχεις; (How many children do you have? *Matching the neuter plural -α*)

---

## 4. Word Order in Questions
Greek is very flexible. To make a statement into a question, you often just change the punctuation and your tone of voice.

- **Statement**: Ο Γιώργος είναι εδώ. (George is here.)
- **Question**: Ο Γιώργος είναι εδώ; (Is George here?)

# Unit 2: The Basics of Nouns & Articles

Now that you can read Greek, it's time to build sentences. In Greek, nouns (people, places, things) are "alive"—they change their shape based on **Gender**, **Case**, and **Number**.

The most important rule for any Greek student is this:  
> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Golden Rule**  
> **Never** learn a Greek noun by itself. Always memorize it with its article (**ο, η, το**). The article tells you the gender and how the rest of the sentence must behave.

---

## 1. Grammatical Gender
Every noun in Greek is either Masculine, Feminine, or Neuter. Usually, the last letter of the word tells you the gender.

| Gender | Article ("The") | Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :---: | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | **ο** | **-ος, -ας, -ης** | **ο** φίλ**ος** *(o fílos)* | the (male) friend |
| **Feminine** | **η** | **-α, -η** | **η** αγάπ**η** *(ee agapi)* | the love |
| **Neuter** | **το** | **-ο, -ι, -μα** | **το** σπίτ**ι** *(to spíti)* | the house |

> [!TIP]
> **Heads up!** While 95% of nouns ending in **-ος** are masculine, some very common ones are actually **feminine** (like **η οδός** - *the street* or **η μέθοδος** - *the method*). Always check the article!

---

## 2. Using "A" or "An" (Indefinite Articles)
When talking about a non-specific thing (a car, a house), use the indefinite article. Notice how the endings often match the noun!

- **Masculine**: **ένας** (ένας φίλος) - a friend
- **Feminine**: **μία / μια** (μια αγάπη) - a love
- **Neuter**: **ένα** (ένα σπίτι) - a house

---

## 3. Intro to Cases: Subject vs. Object
In English, "The man" is always "The man," whether he is doing the action or receiving it. In Greek, the word **changes shape** depending on its role in the sentence.

### The Nominative (The Subject)
The form you find in the dictionary. Used for the person/thing **performing** the action.

- **Ο** πατέρ**ας** είναι εδώ. *(The father is here)*

### The Accusative (The Object)
Used for the person/thing **receiving** the action (the Direct Object). 

- **Rule for Masculine:** Drop the final **-ς**. The article becomes **τον** (always keep the **'ν'** for masculine nouns to avoid confusion with the neuter article **το**!).
- **Rule for Fem/Neu:** The word doesn't change, but the feminine article becomes **τη(ν)**.
    - *Note: The feminine 'την' sometimes drops its 'ν' depending on the next letter, but you will learn that trick later!*

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | **ο** πατέρ**ας** | **η** μητέρ**α** | **το** παιδ**ί** |
| **Object (Acc)** | **τον** πατέρ**α** | **τη(ν)** μητέρ**α** | **το** παιδ**ί** |

> [!TIP]
> **Names take articles!**  
> You don't say "Maria is here." You say **"Η Μαρία είναι εδώ."** (The Maria is here).

---

- [ ] I know the direct articles (**ο, η, το**) by heart.
- [ ] I understand that masculine nouns drop the **-ς** when they are the object of a sentence.

# Unit 13: Direct & Indirect Object Pronouns (me, to me)

In Unit 3, you learned how to say "I see **him**" (**Τον** βλέπω). These are **Direct Objects**. Now, we learn how to handle the receiver of an action: the **Indirect Object** (e.g., "Give **to me**").

---

## 1. The Two Types of Objects
### Direct (Accusative)
The target of the action.

- **Με** βλέπεις; (Do you see **me**?)
- **Τον** ξέρω. (I know **him**.)

### Indirect (Genitive)
The person receiving something. In Greek, we use the **Genitive** case for this!

- **Μου** δίνεις... (Give **to me**...)
- **Σου** λέω... (I am telling **to you**...)

| English | Direct (Acc) | Indirect (Gen) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Me / To me | **με** | **μου** |
| You / To you | **σε** | **σου** |
| Him / To him | **τον** | **του** |
| Her / To her | **τη(ν)** | **της** |
| It / To it | **το** | **του** |

---

## 2. Stacking Pronouns: The Rule
What if you want to say "I give **it** (the book) to **her** (Maria)"? 
In Greek, the person (Indirect) always comes **BEFORE** the thing (Direct).

**Formula: Indirect + Direct + Verb**

- **Της το** δίνω. (I give it to her).
- **Μου το** λες; (Are you telling it to me?)
- **Σας τα** γράφω. (I am writing them [neut] to you all).

---

## 3. Position in the Sentence
Just like negation (**δεν**) and future (**θα**), these pronouns sit right in front of the verb.

- **Δεν σου το** δίνω. (I am not giving it to you).
- **Θα μου το** πεις; (Will you tell it to me?)

---

## 🎯 Unit 13 Mastery Checklist
- [ ] I know the difference between **με** (me) and **μου** (to me).
- [ ] I remember that the person comes before the thing: **Της το** δίνω.
- [ ] I can negate a double-pronoun sentence: **Δεν σου το** λέω.

# Unit 26: Active & Passive Participles (Οι Μετοχές)

A Participle is a verb dressed up to act like an adjective or an adverb. In English, we use **-ing** (Running) or **-ed** (Locked). Greek has two powerful forms you must master at the B2 level.

---

## 1. Active Participle (-οντας)
Used to show two actions happening *at the same time* by the same person. It translates to "**while doing X**" or "**by doing X**."

**Formula: Stem + -οντας (or -ώντας for Type B)**

- **τρέχω** ➔ **τρέχοντας** (while running)
- **μιλάω** ➔ **μιλώντας** (while speaking)

> [!TIP]
> **The Frozen Ending**  
> The active participle is indeclinable. It **never changes** to match gender or number.  
> - Η Μαρία ήρθε **τρέχοντας**. (Maria came running).

---

## 2. Passive Participle (-μένος)
This acts exactly like a normal Adjective. It describes the **final state** of something after an action (e.g., Tired, Broken, Hidden).

**Formula: Stem + -μένος, -μένη, -μένο**

- **κουράζω** (tire) ➔ **κουρασμένος** (tired)
- **κλείνω** (close) ➔ **κλεισμένος** (closed)

Because it is an adjective, it **must match** the noun:

- **Η πόρτα είναι κλεισμένη.** (The door is closed).
- **Οι κλεισμένοι δρόμοι.** (The closed roads).

---

## 3. The "C1" Bonus: -ώντας
For Type B verbs (like *αγαπάω* or *οδηγώ*), the ending becomes **-ώντας** (with an omega).

- **Οδηγώντας** (while driving)
- **Γελώντας** (while laughing)

---

## 🎯 Unit 26 Mastery Checklist
- [ ] I can use **-οντας** to show simultaneous actions.
- [ ] I know that the **-οντας** form never changes its ending.
- [ ] I can use **-μένος** as an adjective to describe a completed state.
- [ ] I know that **-μένος** must match the noun in gender and number.

# Unit 22: Advanced Particles & Spoken Expressions

To sound like a native, you need to master the tiny, untranslatable words that Greeks sprinkle into their speech. These "conversational particles" add flavor, doubt, sarcasm, or intense emotion to any sentence.

---

## 1. Curiosity & Doubt
### Άραγε (I wonder...)
Placed at the beginning or end of a question.

- **Άραγε** θα έρθει η Μαρία; (I wonder if Maria will come?)
- Τι σκέφτεται **άραγε**; (What is he thinking, I wonder?)

### Τάχα & Δήθεν (Supposedly / "Yeah, right")
Used to show extreme sarcasm or disbelief.

- Κλαίει **δήθεν** επειδή χτύπησε. (He is *supposedly* crying [but I know he's faking]).
- **Ναι, τάχα μου!** (Yeah, right! / Allegedly!)

---

## 2. Wishes & Hopes
### Μακάρι (If only / Let's hope)
Triggers the Subjunctive (**να**) or stands alone as a hopeful response.

- **Μακάρι να** περάσω τις εξετάσεις! (I hope I pass!)
- Person A: Θα βρούμε τραπέζι; ➔ Person B: **Μακάρι!** (Let's hope!)

---

## 3. Direction & Commands
### Ας (Let's / Let it be)
Triggers the Subjunctive directly (no *να* needed).

- **Ας πάμε!** (Let's go!)
- **Ας είναι.** (Let it be / Whatever).

### Άντε / Άιντε (Come on!)
The most versatile word in Greek. It can mean "Let's go!", "Hurry up!", or "Get out of here!"

- **Άντε**, σήκω! (Come on, get up!)
- **Άντε πάλι...** (Oh, here we go again...)

---

## 4. Emotional Interjections
- **Μπράβο!** (Well done!)
- **Α μπα;** (Really? / No way!)
- **Αχ!** (Oh! / Ah! - used for both pain and relief).
- **Αλίμονο!** (Alas! / Of course! - depending on context).

---

# Unit 20: The Perfect Tenses (I have done)

In Greek, the Perfect tenses (Present Perfect, Past Perfect, Future Perfect) are built almost exactly like in English. You use a helper verb "**to have**" combined with a special, unchanging action word.

---

## 1. The Helper Verb: Έχω
You already know how to conjugate **έχω**. In this tense, it acts as the "motor" that drives the action.

- **Εγώ έχω** (I have)
- **Εσύ έχεις** (You have)
- **Αυτός έχει** (He has)
- **Εμείς έχουμε** (We have)
- **Εσείς έχετε** (You have)
- **Αυτοί έχουν** (They have)

---

## 2. The Frozen Word (-ει)
To create the action word (e.g., "played", "read", "written"), you take the **Simple Stem** (from Unit 12) and add the ending **-ει**. 

This word **never changes** regardless of the person.

- **παίζω** ➔ παίξ- ➔ **παίξει**
- **γράφω** ➔ γράψ- ➔ **γράψει**
- **διαβάζω** ➔ διαβάσ- ➔ **διαβάσει**

---

## 3. Assembling the Present Perfect
Simply put them together:

- **Έχω παίξει.** (I have played).
- **Έχουν διαβάσει.** (They have read).
- **Έχεις γράψει;** (Have you written?)

---

## 4. Past & Future Perfect
To change the timeline, you only change the helper verb **έχω**. The frozen word stays the same!

### Past Perfect (I had done)
Use the past tense helper **είχα**:

- **Είχα παίξει.** (I had played).

### Future Perfect (I will have done)
Use the future helper **θα έχω**:

- **Θα έχω παίξει** μέχρι αύριο. (I will have played by tomorrow).

---

## 🎯 Unit 20 Mastery Checklist
- [ ] I can form the frozen "-ει" word using the simple stem.
- [ ] I know that the helper verb **έχω** is the only part that conjugates.
- [ ] I can translate "I have", "I had", and "I will have" using this formula.

# Unit 8: Plural Nouns & Adjectives

Welcome to the **A2 Expansion Stage**! You’ve mastered the singular. Now, it’s time to multiply everything. In Greek, when a noun becomes plural, its **Article**, its **Ending**, and its **Adjective** must all change to match.

---

## 1. Masculine Plurals (Οι / Τους)
Masculine nouns are the most "active" during pluralization. The ending depends on the singular form.

| Singular | Plural Subject (Nom) | Plural Object (Acc) | Pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ο φίλος** | **οι** φίλ**οι** | **τους** φίλ**ους** | -ος ➔ -οι / -ους |
| **ο πατέρας** | **οι** πατέρ**ες** | **τους** πατέρ**ες** | -ας ➔ -ες |
| **ο μαθητής** | **οι** μαθητ**ές** | **τους** μαθητ**ές** | -ης ➔ -ες |

> [!WARNING]
> **The -οι vs -ους Trap**  
> For nouns ending in **-ος**, the plural "The" is **οι** (Subject) but becomes **τους** (Object). The noun ending follows: **-οι** matches **οι**, and **-ους** matches **τους**.  
> - Οι καλοί φίλοι. (The good friends - Subject)  
> - Τους καλούς φίλους. (The good friends - Object)

---

## 2. Feminine Plurals (Οι / Τις)
Feminine nouns are much easier. The noun ending **-ες** never changes between subject and object—only the article flips!

- **Singular**: Η μητέρα / Η καλή
- **Plural Subject**: **Οι** μητέρ**ες** / **Οι** καλ**ές**
- **Plural Object**: **Τις** μητέρ**ες** / **Τις** καλ**ές**

> [!CAUTION]
> **The Academic Exception (-εις)**  
> While everyday words use **-ες**, many formal or academic nouns ending in **-ση** or **-ξη** form their plural with **-εις**.  
> - Η λύση (solution) ➔ **Οι λύσεις**
> - Η τάξη (class) ➔ **Οι τάξεις**

> [!WARNING]
> **The Masked Feminines (-ος)**  
> Remember **η οδός** (the street) from Unit 2? Feminine nouns ending in **-ος** take masculine endings in the plural (**-οι, -ους**), but keep their feminine articles!
> - Plural Subject: **Οι** οδ**οί**
> - Plural Object: **Τις** οδ**ούς**

---

## 3. Neuter Plurals (Τα)
Neuter nouns are the most stable. Both the article **τα** and the noun endings remain the same whether they are the subject or the object.

| Singular | Plural (Nom & Acc) | Pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **το βιβλίο** | **τα** βιβλί**α** | -ο ➔ -α |
| **το παιδί** | **τα** παιδ**ιά** | -ι ➔ -ια |
| **το μάθημα** | **τα** μαθή**ματα** | -μα ➔ -ματα |

---

## 4. Verb Agreement
When the subject is plural, the verb must also be plural.

- **Ο** φίλος **διαβάζει**. (The friend reads.)
- **Οι** φίλοι **διαβάζουν**. (The friends read.)

---

- [ ] I can pluralize the articles: **οι/τους**, **οι/τις**, and **τα**.
- [ ] I know that masculine **-ος** nouns change to **-οι** (subject) and **-ους** (object).
- [ ] I can correctly pluralize neuter nouns ending in **-μα** (e.g., *προβλήματα*).

# Unit 24: Reflexive Pronouns (Myself)

Reflexive pronouns are used when the person doing the action and the person receiving it are the same. In English, we use "-self." In Greek, we use the phrase **ο εαυτός μου** (my self).

---

## 1. The Formula
The word **εαυτός** (self) is a masculine noun. To say "myself," you simply add the weak possessive pronoun after it.

| Person | Greek | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st (Singular)** | τον **εαυτό μου** | myself |
| **2nd (Singular)** | τον **εαυτό σου** | yourself |
| **3rd (Singular)** | τον **εαυτό του / της** | himself / herself |
| **1st (Plural)** | τους **εαυτούς μας** | ourselves |
| **2nd (Plural)** | τους **εαυτούς σας** | yourselves |
| **3rd (Plural)** | τους **εαυτούς τους** | themselves |

---

## 2. How to Use them
Because "the self" is receiving the action, it is almost always in the **Accusative Case**.

- **Αγαπώ τον εαυτό μου.** (I love myself).
- **Προσέχεις τον εαυτό σου;** (Do you take care of yourself?)
- **Σκέφτονται μόνο τους εαυτούς τους.** (They only think of themselves).

---

## 3. Reflexive Verbs vs. Pronouns
In Unit 18, you learned that **Passive Verbs (-ομαι)** are often used reflexively.

- **Πλένομαι** (I wash myself).

So when do you use the pronoun? You use the pronoun (**τον εαυτό μου**) for emphasis or with verbs that don't have a passive form.

- **Βλάπτω τον εαυτό μου.** (I am hurting myself).

---

# Unit 23: Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

Relative pronouns link two sentences together. Instead of saying "I saw a man. The man was tall," you say "I saw a man **who** was tall." In Greek, we have two ways to do this: the easy way and the formal way.

---

## 1. The Easy Way: Που
The word **που** is the ultimate chameleon. It is indeclinable (never changes shape) and can mean **who**, **which**, **that**, or **where**.

- **Ο άντρας που είδα...** (The man [whom] I saw...)
- **Το σπίτι που μένω...** (The house [where] I live...)
- **Οι άνθρωποι που ξέρω...** (The people [that] I know...)

> [!TIP]
> **Spelling Alert**: Don't confuse **που** (that/which) with **Πού;** (Where?). The question word always has an accent; the relative pronoun never does.

---

## 2. The Formal Way: Ο οποίος
If you are writing a formal letter or reading the news, you will see **ο οποίος**. Unlike *που*, this word acts like an adjective and must match the noun in **Gender, Number, and Case**.

- **Ο άντρας ο οποίος...** (Masc)
- **Η γυναίκα η οποία...** (Fem)
- **Το παιδί το οποίο...** (Neut)

---

## 3. Using Prepositions
When you need to say things like "the man **to whom**" or "the house **in which**," you must use the formal *ο οποίος* or a combination of *που*.

- **Το σπίτι στο οποίο μένω.** (The house in which I live).
- **Η κοπέλα για την οποία σου μίλησα.** (The girl about whom I told you).

---

## 4. The Relative "What": Ό,τι
When you want to say "whatever" or "what" (meaning "that which"), use **ό,τι**.

- **Κάνε ό,τι θέλεις.** (Do whatever you want).
- **Ό,τι πεις.** (Whatever you say).

---

- [ ] I know the difference between **που** (that) and **πού;** (where?).
- [ ] I recognize **ο οποίος** as the formal version of "who/which."
- [ ] I can use **ό,τι** to mean "whatever."

# Unit 12: Building the Simple Stem (Σ, Ξ, Ψ)

To speak in the Future or the Subjunctive, you need the **Simple Stem** (the "Photo" stem). Finding this stem is like a puzzle: you look at the ending of the verb and swap it for a new one. 

There are three main "Cheat Codes" to master.

---

## 1. The "S" Group (Σ) ➔ Most common
If the verb ends in **-νω, -ζω, -ω,** or **-θω**, the stem usually changes to a **-σ-**.

- **διαβάζω** (read) ➔ **διαβάσ-**
- **κλείνω** (close) ➔ **κλείσ-**
- **πληρώνω** (pay) ➔ **πληρώσ-**
- **νιώθω** (feel) ➔ **νιώσ-**

---

## 2. The "X" Group (Ξ) ➔ Throat sounds
If the verb ends in a "throat" sound like **-κω, -γω, -χω**, or a specific **-ζω**, it explodes into a **-ξ-** (Xi).

- **ανοίγω** (open) ➔ **ανοίξ-**
- **τρέχω** (run) ➔ **τρέξ-**
- **διδάσκω** (teach) ➔ **διδάξ-**
- **παίζω** (play) ➔ **παίξ-** (The main exception!)

---

## 3. The "PS" Group (Ψ) ➔ Lip sounds
If the verb ends in a "lip" sound like **-πω, -βω, -φω**, it explodes into a **-ψ-** (Psi).

- **γράφω** (write) ➔ **γράψ-**
- **λείπω** (be absent) ➔ **λείψ-**
- **κρύβω** (hide) ➔ **κρύψ-**

---

## 4. Why does this matter?
Now that you have the stem (e.g., **γράψ-**), you can use it for everything you learned in Unit 10 and 11:

- **Future**: Θα **γράψ**ω. (I will write).
- **Subjunctive**: Θέλω να **γράψ**ω. (I want to write).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Irregulars**  
> Some verbs are rebels and don't follow these codes. You just have to memorize their simple stems:
> - **βλέπω** (see) ➔ **δ-** (θα δω)
> - **λέω** (say) ➔ **π-** (θα πω)
> - **πάω** (go) ➔ **πα-** (θα πάω)
> - **έρχομαι** (come) ➔ **ερθ-** (θα έρθω)

---

- [ ] I can turn **διαβάζω** into **διαβάσ-**.
- [ ] I can turn **γράφω** into **γράψ-**.
- [ ] I can turn **ανοίγω** into **ανοίξ-**.
- [ ] I have memorized the "Big 4" irregular stems (*δω, πω, πάω, έρθω*).

# Unit 10: Verbal Aspect & The Subjunctive (Να)

Welcome to the great divide in the Greek language. To move from A1 to A2, you must understand **Aspect**. Greek doesn’t just care about *when* something happens; it cares about *how* it happens.

---

## 1. The "Video vs. Photo" Concept
Greek forces you to choose between two ways of looking at an action:

1. **Continuous Aspect (The Video)**: For habits, routines, or ongoing background actions. (e.g., "I am playing", "I play every day").
2. **Simple Aspect (The Photo)**: For a single, specific, or completed event. (e.g., "I want to play *one game*").

Because of this, every active Greek verb has **two stems**:

- **Continuous Stem**: The one you know (παίζ-).
- **Simple Stem**: The "Aorist" stem (παίξ-). *We will learn how to build this in the next units.*

---

## 2. The Subjunctive Mood (Magic word: Να)
Greek has no "infinitive" (like "to go"). Instead, it uses **να** + a conjugated verb.  

| English | Greek (Literal) | Greek (Actual) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I want **to go**. | I want **that I go**. | Θέλω **να** πάω. |
| You want **to play**. | You want **that you play**. | Θέλεις **να** παίζεις; |

### When to use Να:
- **θα + να**: "Θέλω να", "Μπορώ να", "Πρέπει να" (I must).
- **Commands**: **Να** προσέχεις! (Be careful!).
- **Wishes**: **Να** είσαι καλά. (Be well).

---

## 3. Aspect in the Subjunctive
Since **να** is followed by a verb, you must choose the aspect:

- **Θέλω να παίζω** (Continuous): I want to be playing (regularly, as a hobby).
- **Θέλω να παίξω** (Simple): I want to play (this specific game right now).

> [!TIP]
> **The Secret of "Simple"**  
> Most of the time, when you ask for something or want to do something, you are talking about a specific event. Therefore, the **Simple Stem** is the most common one used with *να*.

---

- [ ] I can use **θα + να** to translate English "to" phrases.
- [ ] I know that Greeks conjugate both verbs (e.g., *Θέλω να πάω* vs *Θέλεις να πας*).

# Unit 9: Verb Groups B1 & B2 (The Accented Verbs)

In Unit 4, you learned **Type A** verbs (like *κάνω*), where the accent is on the stem. Now we meet **Type B**—verbs where the accent is on the very last letter (**-ώ**). 

These verbs are the "singers" of Greek; they have a distinct rhythm and are split into two "vowel teams".

---

## Team B1: The "Alpha" Lovers (-άω)
These are the most common Type B verbs. They love the letter **α**. 
*Examples: **αγαπώ (αγαπάω), μιλάω, ρωτάω, ξυπνάω***.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: **μιλάω** (speak) |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-άω** | μιλ**άω** |
| (Εσύ) | **-άς** | μιλ**άς** |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-άει** | μιλ**άει** |
| (Εμείς) | **-άμε** | μιλ**άμε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-άτε** | μιλ**άτε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-άνε** | μιλ**άνε** |

> [!TIP]
> **Spoken vs. Written**  
> You might see **αγαπώ** (Formal) and **αγαπάω** (Spoken). They are the same! For a natural Masterclass feel, we recommend the **-άω** pattern as it’s the most common in modern Greece.

---

## Team B2: The "Oo" Lovers (-ώ)
These verbs are less common but very important. They prefer the **-ούμε** sound in the plural.
*Examples: **μπορώ, ζω, τηλεφωνώ, οδηγώ***.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: **μπορώ** (can) |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ώ** | μπορ**ώ** |
| (Εσύ) | **-είς** | μπορ**είς** |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-εί** | μπορ**εί** |
| (Εμείς) | **-ούμε** | μπορ**ούμε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-είτε** | μπορ**είτε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ούν** | μπορ**ούν** |

---

## How to tell them apart?
- If a verb has an **-άω** version (like *μιλάω*), it's **B1**.
- If it ONLY ends in **-ώ** (like *μπορώ*), it's **B2**.

# Unit 3: Being & Having (Είμαι & Έχω)

To build real sentences, you need verbs. In Greek, just like in English, the two most important verbs are **To Be** (I am) and **To Have** (I have). Together, they unlock hundreds of basic conversations.

---

## 1. The Actors: Personal Pronouns
Before the verbs, we need to know the people. Greek handles "I, You, He" very logically.

| English | Greek | Pronunciation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **I** | **Εγώ** | *(e-gó)* |
| **You** (sing.) | **Εσύ** | *(e-sí)* |
| **He / She / It** | **Αυτός / Αυτή / Αυτό** | *(af-tós / af-tí / af-tó)* |
| **We** | **Εμείς** | *(e-mís)* |
| **You** (plur./formal) | **Εσείς** | *(e-sís)* |
| **They** (m/f/n) | **Αυτοί / Αυτές / Αυτά** | *(af-tí / af-tés / af-tá)* |

> [!NOTE]
> **Rhyme Time**  
> Notice that **Εμείς** (We) and **Εσείς** (You plural) rhyme, and the 3rd person singular pronouns (**Αυτός, Αυτή, Αυτό**) follow the exact same ending rules as the nouns you learned in Unit 2!

> [!TIP]
> **Pronunciation Tip:** When Alpha and Upsilon (**Αυ**) team up before certain letters like Tau (τ), they make an **'af'** sound (like in *af-tós*)!

---

## 2. Conjugating the "Big Two"

### Είμαι (To Be)
Used for identity and state (I am a student, I am here).

- **Είμαι** (I am)
- **Είσαι** (You are)
- **Είναι** (He/She/It is)
- **Είμαστε** (We are)
- **Είστε** (You are)
- **Είναι** (They are — *Surprise! It's the same word as "He is"!*)

### Έχω (To Have)
Used for possession and family. 

- **Έχω** (I have)
- **Έχεις** (You have)
- **Έχει** (He/She/It has)
- **Έχουμε** (We have)
- **Έχετε** (You have)
- **Έχουν(ε)** (They have)

---

## 3. The Pro-Drop Rule: Speak Like a Native
In English, you must say "**I** have a dog." In Greek, you simply say "**Έχω** έναν σκύλο."

> [!NOTE]
> **Notice it's "έναν" and not "ένας"?** Just like the definite articles you learned in Unit 2, the word for "a/an" also changes its ending when it's the **Object** of the sentence receiving the action!

Because the ending of the verb changes for every person (I, you, he), the pronoun **Εγώ, Εσύ, κτλ.** becomes redundant. Greeks **only** use the pronouns when they want to emphasize *who* specifically they are talking about. 

- **Natural**: Είμαι εδώ. (I am here.)
- **Emphatic**: **Εγώ** είμαι εδώ! (***I*** am here!)

---

## 4. The Power Combo
With these two verbs and the Accusative case from Unit 2, you can already speak!

- **Έχεις το βιβλίο;** (Do you have the book?)
- **Όχι, ο Γιώργος έχει το βιβλίο.** (No, George has the book.)
- **Είμαστε φίλοι.** (We are friends.)

---

# Unit 4: Regular Verbs & Negation (Type A)

You’ve learned the nouns; now it’s time to make things happen. Verbs are the workhorses of the Greek sentence. They change their shape to tell you **who** is doing the action and **when**.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Verb
Every Greek verb has a **Stem** (trunk) and an **Ending** (leaves).  
Take the verb "to play": **παίζω** *(pé-zo)*.

- The Stem is **παίζ-** (contains the meaning).
- The Ending is **-ω** (means "I").

Almost all Greek verbs in the dictionary end in **-ω**. This always means "I" am doing the action.

---

## 2. Verb Groups (The Three Teams)
Before conjugating, you must know which "team" a verb belongs to. Look at the **accent mark** in the dictionary form:

1. **Type A (Regular)**: Accent is on the stem (NOT the -ω).  
   *Examples: **παίζω, κάνω, γράφω, διαβάζω***
2. **Type B (Accented)**: Accent is on the ending (**-ώ** or **-άω**).  
   *Examples: **αγαπώ (αγαπάω), μιλώ (μιλάω)***
3. **Type C (Passive)**: Ends in **-ομαι** instead of -ω.  
   *Examples: **έρχομαι, γίνομαι***

**In this unit, we focus exclusively on Type A.**

---

## 3. Conjugation (Present Tense)
To change the person (I, you, we...), chop off the **-ω** and add these endings:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: **διαβάζω** (read) |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ω** | διαβάζ**ω** |
| (Εσύ) | **-εις** | διαβάζ**εις** |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-ει** | διαβάζ**ει** |
| (Εμείς) | **-ουμε** | διαβάζ**ουμε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-ετε** | διαβάζ**ετε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ουν(ε)** | διαβάζ**ουν** |

> [!TIP]
> **He = They?**  
> In English, "He is" and "They are" are different. In Greek, for verbs like **Είμαι**, they are the same (**είναι**). But for regular verbs like **διαβάζω**, They (**-ουν**) is distinct from He (**-ει**).

---

To say "I do not," just put the word **δεν** (*den*) right before the verb.

- **Δεν παίζω** (*I don't play*)
- **Δεν κάνω** (*I don't do/make*)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Δεν vs. Όχι**
> - Use **Όχι** to answer "No" to a question.
> - Use **Δεν** right before a verb to say "not" (**Δεν καταλαβαίνω** — *I don't understand*).
> - Example: "Όχι, δεν παίζω" (*No, I don't play*).
