### Unit 1: The Greek Writing System Masterclass

Welcome to your first step toward mastering Greek! As an English speaker, learning the Greek alphabet is much easier than you might think. Over 25% of English vocabulary has Greek roots—you already know more Greek than you realize.

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to read and write Modern Greek: Single Letters, Digraphs (Sound Combinations), Accents, and Punctuation.

---

#### 1. The 24 Letters
The Greek alphabet consists of 24 letters. To make them easier to learn, we can group them into three categories:

### Team A: The "Freebies"
These look and sound almost exactly like their English counterparts.

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Α α** | Alpha | [a] | **a**pple |
| **Ε ε** | Epsilon | [e] | p**e**t |
| **Ι ι** | Iota | [ee] | m**ee**t |
| **Κ κ** | Kappa | [k] | **k**ite |
| **Μ μ** | Mu | [m] | **m**om |
| **Ν ν** | Nu | [n] | **n**o |
| **Ο ο** | Omicron | [o] | t**o**p |
| **Τ τ** | Tau | [t] | **t**op |
| **Ζ ζ** | Zeta | [z] | **z**oo |

> [!TIP]
> **Pro-Tip for English Speakers:** The voiceless consonants **π [p], τ [t], and κ [k] are pronounced without "aspiration"**. Unlike in English, where you release a puff of air when saying words like pot or cat, in Greek these letters have no expulsion of breath, sounding more like the crisp 'p' in spot or 't' in stay.

### Team B: The "False Friends"
These look like English letters but represent different sounds.

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Β β** | Beta | [v] | **v**an (not 'B') |
| **Η η** | Eta | [ee] | m**ee**t (not 'H') |
| **Ρ ρ** | Rho | [r] | **r**olled R (not 'P') |
| **Χ χ** | Chi | [h] | Scottish **ch** (loch) |
| **Υ υ** | Upsilon | [ee] | m**ee**t (not 'Y' or 'U') |

### Team C: The "Aliens"
These shapes are unique to the Greek alphabet.

| Letter | Name | Sound | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Γ γ** | Gamma | [j] or [g] | **y**es (before e/i) |
| **Δ δ** | Delta | [th] | **th**is (soft) |
| **Θ θ** | Theta | [th] | **th**ink (hard) |
| **Λ λ** | Lambda | [l] | **l**ion |
| **Ξ ξ** | Xi | [ks] | bo**x** |
| **Π π** | Pi | [p] | **p**en |
| **Σ σ, ς** | Sigma | [s] or [z] | **s**un (z before β, γ, δ) |
| **Φ φ** | Phi | [f] | **f**ish |
| **Ψ ψ** | Psi | [ps] | o**ps** |
| **Ω ω** | Omega | [o] | t**o**p |

---

#### 2. Digraphs (Letter Combinations)
Sometimes Greek glues two letters together to create a single new sound.

### Vowel Twins
- **αι** = [e] (αιτία - cause)
- **ει, οι, υι** = [ee] (είμαι - I am)
- **ου** = [oo] (ουρανός - sky)

> [!TIP]
> **The Tricky Vowel Twins (αυ & ευ):**  
> When **α** or **ε** are combined with **υ**, the υ transforms into a consonant sound! 
> - **αυ / ευ** sound like **[af] / [ef]** before voiceless consonants. 
> - **αυ / ευ** sound like **[av] / [ev]** before vowels or voiced consonants.

### Consonant Twins (The Hard Sounds)
Over centuries, some original sounds softened. Modern Greek uses these combinations to regain those "hard" sounds.
- **μπ** = B (μπαμπάς - dad)
- **ντ** = D (ντύνω - to dress)
- **γκ / γγ** = G (άγκυρα - anchor)
- **τσ** = TS (τσέπη - pocket)
- **τζ** = J / DZ (τζάκι - fireplace)

---

#### 3. Accents & Punctuation
Greek is highly phonetic, and its punctuation marks are unique.

### The Tonos (´)
Every word with 2+ syllables has an accent mark to show the stressed syllable.
- **νερό** (ne-RÓ) - water
- **πρόβλημα** (PRÓ-vli-ma) - problem

### Punctuation Marks
- **The Question Mark (;):** The Greek question mark is the English semicolon.
- **The Upper Dot (·):** Used like a colon or semicolon for a medium break.
- **Quotation Marks (« »):** Used for dialogue and quotes.

### Unit 2: The Basics of Nouns & Articles

Now that you can read Greek, it's time to build sentences. In Greek, nouns (people, places, things) are "alive"—they change their shape based on **Gender**, **Case**, and **Number**. 

The most important rule for any Greek student is this:

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Golden Rule**  
> **Never** learn a Greek noun by itself. Always memorize it with its article (**ο, η, το**). The article tells you the gender and how the rest of the sentence must behave.

---

#### 1. Grammatical Gender
Every noun in Greek is either Masculine, Feminine, or Neuter. Usually, the last letter of the word tells you the gender. 

However, **grammatical gender does not always correspond to biological sex**. For example, the words for "girl" (*το κορίτσι*) and "boy" (*το αγόρι*) are actually grammatically neuter!

| Gender | Article ("The") | Common Ending | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | ο | -ος, -ας, -ης | ο φίλος (o fílos) | the (male) friend |
| **Feminine** | η | -α, -η | η αγάπη (ee agápi) | the love |
| **Neuter** | το | -ο, -ι, -μα | το σπίτι (to spíti) | the house |

> [!TIP]
> **Heads up! The tricky "-ος" ending**  
> While most nouns ending in -ος are masculine, this is not a universal rule. Some very common ones are **feminine** (like *η οδός* - the street, or *η μέθοδος* - the method). Additionally, many **neuter** nouns also end in -ος (like *το λάθος* - the mistake, or *το κράτος* - the state). Always check the article!

---

#### 2. Using "A" or "An" (Indefinite Articles)
When talking about a non-specific thing (a car, a house), use the indefinite article. Notice how the endings often match the noun!

- **Masculine:** **ένας** (*ένας φίλος*) - a friend
- **Feminine:** **μία / μια** (*μια αγάπη*) - a love
- **Neuter:** **ένα** (*ένα σπίτι*) - a house

> [!NOTE]
> **Pronunciation & Spelling Tip:**  
> The feminine *μια* is pronounced as a single syllable, while *μία* is pronounced as two syllables and is generally used when you want to emphasize the number "one". For the masculine and feminine object forms, *έναν* and *μιαν* may optionally drop their final "-ν" depending on the word that follows them.

---

#### 3. Intro to Cases: Subject vs. Object
In English, "The man" is always "The man," whether he is doing the action or receiving it. In Greek, there are four cases, meaning the word changes shape depending on its role in the sentence. Today we focus on the two most important:

### The Nominative (The Subject)
The form you find in the dictionary. Used for the person or thing performing the action or being described.
- *Ο πατέρας είναι εδώ.* (The father is here)

### The Accusative (The Object)
Used for the person or thing receiving the action (the Direct Object).
- **Rule for Masculine:** Drop the final **-ς**. The article becomes **τον**. (*Ο πατέρας* -> *τον πατέρα*).
- **Rule for Feminine:** The noun's ending doesn't change. The article becomes **τη(ν)**. 
- **Rule for Neuter:** No changes at all! Neuter nouns and articles (*το*) are exactly the same in both the Subject and Object roles.

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject (Nom)** | ο πατέρας | η μητέρα | το παιδί |
| **Object (Acc)** | τον πατέρα | τη(ν) μητέρα | το παιδί |

> [!TIP]
> **The "τη(ν)" Rule:**  
> The feminine article 'την' drops its 'ν' before most consonants. You **must keep the 'ν'** only if the next word starts with a vowel, or with the hard sounds **κ, π, τ, γκ, μπ, ντ, ξ, ψ**. (e.g., *τη Δευτέρα* - on Monday, but *την άνοιξη* - in the spring).

---

#### 4. When to use the Definite Article ("The")
Greek uses the definite article much more often than English! 
- **Names take articles:** You don't say "Maria is here." You say "*Η Μαρία είναι εδώ.*" (The Maria is here).
- **Abstract ideas & Classes:** Greek uses "the" for abstract concepts and general categories. You don't say "Fear is an instinct", you say "*Ο φόβος είναι ένστικτο*" (The fear is an instinct).

---

### Unit 3: Being & Having (Είμαι & Έχω)

To build real sentences, you need verbs. In Greek, just like in English, the two most important verbs are **To Be** (I am) and **To Have** (I have). Together, they unlock hundreds of basic conversations.

---

#### 1. The Actors: Personal Pronouns
Before the verbs, we need to know the people. Greek handles "I, You, He" very logically.

| English | Greek | Pronunciation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I | Εγώ | (e-gó) |
| You (sing.) | Εσύ | (e-sí) |
| He / She / It | Αυτός / Αυτή / Αυτό | (af-tós / af-tí / af-tó) |
| We | Εμείς | (e-mís) |
| You (plur./formal) | Εσείς | (e-sís) |
| They (m/f/n) | Αυτοί / Αυτές / Αυτά | (af-tí / af-tés / af-tó) |

> [!NOTE]
> **Rhyme Time & Grammar Tip:**  
> Notice that **Εμείς** (We) and **Εσείς** (You plural) rhyme! Also, the 3rd person pronouns (**Αυτός, Αυτή, Αυτό**) are highly versatile—they can be used as demonstrative adjectives meaning "this" (e.g., *this man*, *this woman*) and follow the exact same ending rules as the nouns you learned in Unit 2!

> [!TIP]
> **Pronunciation Tip: The "Αυ" Vowel Twin:**  
> When Alpha (α) and Upsilon (υ) team up, their sound changes depending on the letter that follows them. Before voiceless consonants like Tau (τ), Kappa (κ), or Pi (π), the combination **'αυ'** makes an **[af]** sound, which is why **αυτός** is pronounced *af-tós*. However, before vowels or voiced consonants (like β, δ, γ, λ, ρ), it makes an **[av]** sound. In rapid, casual speech, the initial 'ε' of words like **εγώ** and **εσύ** is sometimes dropped if the previous word ends in a vowel (e.g., *'γώ*, *'σύ*).

---

#### 2. Conjugating the "Big Two"

### Είμαι (To Be)
Used for identity and state (I am a student, I am here).

- **Είμαι** (I am)
- **Είσαι** (You are)
- **Είναι** (He/She/It is)
- **Είμαστε** (We are)
- **Είστε / Είσαστε** (You are) - *Both forms are correct and widely used as alternatives*.
- **Είναι** (They are) — *Surprise! It's the exact same word as "He is"!*

### Έχω (To Have)
Used for possession and family.

- **Έχω** (I have)
- **Έχεις** (You have)
- **Έχει** (He/She/It has)
- **Έχουμε** (We have)
- **Έχετε** (You have)
- **Έχουν(ε)** (They have) - *The optional (ε) at the end is very common in everyday spoken Greek*.

---

#### 3. The Pro-Drop Rule & The Formal "You"

### Speak Like a Native:
In English, you must say "I have a dog." In Greek, because the ending of the verb clearly shows the person and number, the subject pronouns (**Εγώ, Εσύ, κτλ.**) become completely redundant. You simply say "**Έχω έναν σκύλο**." Greeks only use explicit pronouns when they want to highlight a contrast or place special emphasis on *who* is doing the action.
- *Natural:* **Είμαι εδώ.** (I am here.)
- *Emphatic:* **Εγώ είμαι εδώ!** (*I* am here!).

### The Plural of Politeness:
Greek uses the second-person plural to address a single person in a polite and respectful manner. You should always use the plural forms (**Εσείς, είστε, έχετε**) when speaking to someone unknown to you (like a taxi driver or shop assistant), or someone who is older or of higher status. 

---

#### 4. The Power Combo
With these two verbs and the Accusative case from Unit 2, you can already speak! 

- *Έχεις το βιβλίο;* (Do you have the book?)
- *Όχι, ο Γιώργος έχει το βιβλίο.* (No, George has the book.)
- *Είμαστε φίλοι.* (We are friends.)

> [!NOTE]
> **Question Intonation:**  
> Notice that you don't need to change the word order to ask a yes/no question like in English (e.g., *You have* -> *Do you have?*). In Greek, you simply use the standard sentence order with a rising intonation of your voice at the end of the sentence.

---

- [ ] I can conjugate **είμαι** and **έχω** by heart, remembering alternative forms like **είσαστε** and **έχουνε**.
- [ ] I know when to use **Αυτός, Αυτή,** and **Αυτό**, and I know how to pronounce **'αυ'** as **[af]** or **[av]**.
- [ ] I understand that I should drop the subject pronoun when speaking naturally.
- [ ] I know to use the plural forms (**Εσείς, είστε, έχετε**) when I want to be polite to a stranger or elder.

### Unit 4: Regular Verbs & Negation (Type A)

You’ve learned the nouns; now it’s time to make things happen. Verbs are the workhorses of the Greek sentence. They change their shape to tell you who is doing the action and when.

---

#### 1. The Anatomy of a Verb
Every Greek verb is formed by the combination of a **stem** and an **inflectional ending**. 

Take the verb "to play": **παίζω** (pé-zo).
- **The Stem is παίζ-** (it carries the essential meaning of the verb).
- **The Ending is -ω** (it tells you the person, number, tense, and voice).

Almost all Greek verbs in the dictionary end in **-ω**. This always means "I" am doing the action. Because the Greek verbal ending clearly shows variation in person and number, a subject pronoun becomes redundant and is usually dropped.

---

#### 2. Verb Groups (The Conjugations)
Before conjugating, you must know which "team" or conjugation a verb belongs to. Look at the accent mark in the dictionary form:

- **Type A (First-Conjugation):** The accent is on the last syllable of the stem (NOT on the -ω). 
    - *Examples:* πα**ί**ζω, κά**ν**ω, γρά**φ**ω, διαβά**ζ**ω
- **Type B (Second-Conjugation):** The accent is on the final vowel of the ending (-ώ or -άω).
    - *Examples:* αγαπ**ώ** (αγαπ**ά**ω), μιλ**ώ** (μιλ**ά**ω)
- **Type C (Deponent/Passive Verbs):** These verbs have no active forms and end in -ομαι instead of -ω, but they are active in meaning.
    - *Examples:* έρχ**ομ**αι (I come), γίν**ομ**αι (I become)

In this unit, we focus exclusively on **Type A (First-Conjugation) verbs**.

---

#### 3. Conjugation (Present Tense)
To change the person (I, you, we...), chop off the -ω and add these endings:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: διαβάζω (I read) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ω** | διαβάζ**ω** |
| (Εσύ) | **-εις** | διαβάζ**εις** |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-ει** | διαβάζ**ει** |
| (Εμείς) | **-ουμε** | διαβάζ**ουμε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-ετε** | διαβάζ**ετε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ουν(ε)** | διαβάζ**ουν(ε)** |

> [!NOTE]
> **Grammar & Pronunciation Tips:**  
> - **Fixed Stress:** For Type A verbs in the active present tense, the stress remains on the exact same syllable throughout all forms (δια**βά**ζω, δια**βά**ζεις, δια**βά**ζουμε, etc.). 
> - **The extra 'ε':** The 3rd person plural form with the extra *-ε* (διαβάζουνε) is very frequent in the spoken colloquial language, but less often used in formal writing.
> - **He = They?:** In English, "He is" and "They are" use different words. In Greek, for irregular verbs like *Είμαι*, they are the exact same word (*είναι*). But for regular verbs like *διαβάζω*, 'They' (-ουν) is distinctly different from 'He' (-ει).

---

#### 4. Negation (Δεν vs. Όχι)
To say "I do not," just put the word **δεν** (den) right before the verb. 

- **Δεν παίζω** (I don't play)
- **Δεν κάνω** (I don't do/make)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Δεν vs. Όχι**  
> - Use **Όχι** to answer "No" to a question.
> - Use **Δεν** right before an indicative verb to say "not" (**Δεν καταλαβαίνω** — I don't understand).
> - *Example:* "Όχι, δεν παίζω" (No, I don't play).

> [!TIP]
> **Speak like a Native: Blending "Δεν"**  
> When the final /n/ of the negative particle **δεν** is followed by a word beginning with a hard consonant like **π, τ,** or **κ**, the sounds blend together!
> - δεν + παίζω sounds like *dem-bézo*.
> - δεν + τολμώ sounds like *den-dolmó*.
> - δεν + καταλαβαίνω sounds like *deng-gatalavéno*.

---

- [ ] I can tell a Type A (First-Conjugation) verb apart from a Type B by checking if the accent is on the stem.
- [ ] I have memorized the endings: -ω, -εις, -ει, -ουμε, -ετε, -ουν(ε), and know the accent doesn't move.
- [ ] I know that **δεν** is used to negate verbs, while **όχι** is used for "No."

### Unit 5: The Accusative Case & Basic Prepositions

In Unit 2, you learned that when a noun is the Object (receiving the action), it drops its final **-ς** (if masculine) and its article becomes **τον, την,** or **το**. 

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Masculine 'N'**  
> We **always** keep the final **-ν** in the masculine article (**τον, στον, έναν**) in writing to avoid confusion with the neuter article (**το, στο, ένα**). This is true even if the next word starts with a soft sound!

Now, we will see the Accusative Case in action through verbs and the "Big Three" prepositions.

---

#### 1. Verbs that use the Accusative
Most "action" (transitive) verbs require the direct object to be in the Accusative case.

- **Βλέπω τον Γιώργο.** (I see George).
- **Έχω την τσάντα.** (I have the bag).
- **Διαβάζω το βιβλίο.** (I read the book).

> [!CAUTION]
> **The "Mirror" Verb**  
> Remember: The verb **Είμαι** (I am) is special. It acts as a linking verb (a mirror), so it takes a "subject predicate" and keeps everything in the **Nominative case** (ο, η, το). 
> *   *Είμαι ο Γιώργος.* (I am George).
> *   *Η Μαρία είναι γιατρός.* (Mary is a doctor).

---

#### 2. The Big Three Prepositions (σε, από, με)
In Greek, almost all basic prepositions force the noun phrase that follows them into the Accusative case.

### A. Σε (To / At / In / On)
This word shows location in space or time, as well as direction. In Greek, we almost never say "σε" and "the" separately. The preposition *σε* obligatorily merges with the definite article into one word:

- σε + τον = **στον**
- σε + την = **στη(ν)**
- σε + το = **στο**
*(And for plurals: στους, στις, στα)*.

- *Example:* Πάω **στο** σινεμά. (I am going to the cinema).
- *Example:* Το πιάτο είναι **στο** τραπέζι. (The plate is on the table).

### B. Από (From / Since / By)
Used for origins, time, cause, and the agent of an action. It does not merge with the article in writing, but the article must still be in the Accusative.

- Είμαι **από την** Ελλάδα. (I am from Greece).
- Έρχομαι **από τον** γιατρό. (I am coming from the doctor).

> [!TIP]
> **Blend it!** Even though it's written separately, the final vowel of *από* is often optionally deleted before a definite article when speaking quickly. *Από την Αθήνα* sounds like "ap-tin Athina" (απ' την Αθήνα)!

### C. Με (With)
Used for company, instruments, means of transport, or manner.

- Είμαι **με τη** Μαρία. (I am with Maria).
- Θέλω καφέ **με** ζάχαρη. (I want coffee with sugar).
- Θα πάμε **με το** λεωφορείο. (We will go by bus).

---

#### 3. Pronunciation: The Dropping 'N' & The Blending Sounds
While the masculine articles (*τον, στον, έναν*) always keep their **-ν** in writing, the feminine words (*την, στην, μιαν*) and negative particles (*δεν, μην*) are more selective. 

### The Dropping 'N' Rule:
They only keep the **-ν** if the next word starts with:
1.  A **vowel** (α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω)
2.  A **hard consonant** (κ, π, τ, ξ, ψ)
3.  A **digraph** (μπ, ντ, γκ, τσ, τζ)

Otherwise, the 'ν' is normally deleted in both speech and writing.
*   **Στη** δασκάλα (The teacher - drops before the soft 'δ')
*   **Στην** πόρτα (The door - keeps before the hard 'π')

### The Blending Consonants Rule:
When the final 'ν' (from either a masculine or feminine word) meets a hard consonant like **π, τ,** or **κ**, the sounds magically blend together!
- **ν + π = mb:** *τον πατέρα* sounds like *to(m)-batéra*.
- **ν + τ = nd:** *τον τρελό* sounds like *to(n)-dreló*.
- **ν + κ = ng:** *τον κάλεσαν* sounds like *to(ng)-gálesan*.

*(If you aren't sure, it is usually safer to include the -ν in speech—native speakers will still understand you perfectly!)*

### Unit 6: Adjectives, Colors & Numbers

In English, adjectives (describing words) are simple: the word "good" never changes. In Greek, adjectives are **chameleons**. They must perfectly match the noun they describe in **Gender**, **Number**, and **Case**. We call this **Agreement**.

---

#### 1. The Chameleon Rule
Every Greek adjective in the dictionary has three versions (Masculine, Feminine, Neuter). Notice how the endings often "rhyme" with the noun!

| Focus | Masculine (-ος) | Feminine (-η/-α) | Neuter (-ο) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Example** | **Ο καλός** πατέρας | **Η καλή** μητέρα | **Το καλό** παιδί |
| **Logic** | The good father | The good mother | The good child |

When you use an adjective as an object (Accusative Case), it must flex with the noun.

- **Subject:** Ο καλός φίλος.
- **Object:** Βλέπω **τον καλό** φίλο. (The adjective drops the **-ς** just like the noun!)

---

#### 2. Colors (Τα Χρώματα)
Most colors are regular adjectives. They help you practice agreement every day.

- **Κόκκινος, κόκκινη, κόκκινο** (Red)
- **Μαύρος, μαύρη, μαύρο** (Black)
- **Πράσινος, πράσινη, πράσινο** (Green)

> [!TIP]
> **Foreign Colors:** Some colors borrowed from other languages do NOT change.  
> **Μπλε** (Blue), **Ροζ** (Pink), and **Καφέ** (Brown) stay the same regardless of gender!  
> - Ο μπλε ουρανός, η μπλε θάλασσα, το μπλε αυτοκίνητο.

---

#### 3. Numbers 1-10 (Αριθμοί 1-10)
Learning to count is the key to daily survival.

- **Ένας / Μία / Ένα** (One)
- **Δύο** (Two)
- **Τρία** (Three)
- **Τέσσερα** (Four)
- **Πέντε** (Five)
- **Έξι** (Six)
- **Επτά** (Seven)
- **Οκτώ** (Eight)
- **Εννέα** (Nine)
- **Δέκα** (Ten)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The number 1 is a chameleon!**  
> Good news: the number "One" is the exact same word as the **indefinite articles** (a/an) you learned in Unit 2! It must match the gender of what you are counting:  
> - **Ένας** άντρας (One man)  
> - **Μία** γυναίκα (One woman)  
> - **Ένα** παιδί (One child)
> 
> *Fun Fact: The numbers **3** and **4** are also mini-chameleons! You will learn their masculine/feminine forms later, but for now, use **Τρία** and **Τέσσερα** when counting out loud.*

### Unit 7: Asking Questions (Τι, Πού, Ποιος)

You now have a solid vocabulary and know how to conjugate verbs. The final step in your A1 foundation is learning how to ask for things. In Greek, this involves two parts: a special punctuation mark and "Question Words" (interrogative pronouns and adverbs).

---

#### 1. The Greek Question Mark (;)
The first thing you must memorize is that the English semicolon (;) is the Greek question mark. The English question mark (?) does not exist in Greek. Be careful not to confuse the two when reading!

*   **Πώς είσαι;** (How are you?)
*   **Τι κάνεις;** (What are you doing?)

---

#### 2. The Fixed "W" Words (Indeclinable)
These words are easy because they are indeclinable—they never change their shape regardless of gender, number, or case.

*   **Τι** (ti) = What? (e.g., *Τι είναι αυτό;*)
*   **Πού** (poo) = Where? (e.g., *Πού είσαι;*)
*   **Πότε** (pó-te) = When? (e.g., *Πότε φεύγουμε;*)
*   **Πώς** (pos) = How? (e.g., *Πώς είσαι;*)
*   **Γιατί** (ya-tí) = Why? (e.g., *Γιατί λες όχι;*)

> [!TIP]
> **Accent Mystery:** You learned in Unit 1 that 1-syllable words generally don't get accents. **Πού** and **Πώς** are rare exceptions! They keep the accent only to differentiate them from their identical twins **που** (the relative pronoun "that/who") and **πως** (the conjunction "that"). *(Notice that 'Τι' does not have a twin, so it never takes an accent).*

---

#### 3. The Chameleon "W" Words (Declinable)
Just like adjectives, some question words are "chameleons". They must perfectly match the gender, number, and case of the noun you are asking about.

### "Who" or "Which" (Ποιος)
Pronounced as one syllable: *pyos*. It declines like an adjective with the endings -ος, -α, -ο. 
*   **Ποιος** είναι ο άντρας; (Who is the man?)
*   **Ποια** είναι η γυναίκα; (Who is the woman?)
*   **Ποιο** παιδί; (Which child?)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Accusative Alert!** If "Who" is the object receiving the action, it drops its -ς and adds a **-ν**, becoming **Ποιον**. 
> *   *Ποιον βλέπεις;* (Who(m) do you see?)

> [!NOTE]
> **How to ask "Whose?"**  
> To ask "Whose?", you must use the Genitive (possession) case. You can use the formal word **Τίνος** (for masculine and neuter), or the very common colloquial forms: **ποιανού** (whose - masc/neut) and **ποιανής** (whose - fem).
> *   *Ποιανού είναι αυτό το βιβλίο;* (Whose book is this?)

### "How Much" or "How Many" (Πόσος)
This word declines like an adjective with the endings -ος, -η, -ο. 
*   **Πόσο** κάνει; (How much does it cost? - *Neuter*)
*   **Πόση** ζάχαρη θέλεις; (How much sugar do you want? - *Feminine*)
*   **Πόσα** παιδιά έχεις; (How many children do you have? - *Neuter plural*)

---

#### 4. The "Preposition First" Rule
In English, you can end a question with a preposition (e.g., "Where did you buy those shoes *from*?" or "Who are you talking *to*?"). 

**In Greek, you can NEVER separate the preposition from the question word**. The preposition must always come immediately *before* the "W" word:
*   **Από πού** τα αγόρασες; (*From where* did you buy them?)
*   **Με ποιον** μιλάς; (*With whom* are you speaking?)

---

#### 5. Word Order & Intonation
To make a statement into a Yes/No question, you don't need to invert the subject and the verb like in English (e.g., *He is* -> *Is he?*). You often just change the punctuation to **( ; )** and use a questioning tone of voice.

*   *Statement:* Ο Γιώργος είναι εδώ. (George is here.)
*   *Question:* Ο Γιώργος είναι εδώ; (Is George here?)

---

### Unit 8: Plural Nouns & Adjectives

Welcome to the **A2 Expansion Stage**! You’ve mastered the singular. Now, it’s time to multiply everything. In Greek, when a noun becomes plural, its **Article**, its **Ending**, and its **Adjective** must all change to match.

---

#### 1. Masculine Plurals (Οι / Τους)
Masculine nouns are the most "active" during pluralization. The ending depends on the singular form.

| Singular | Plural Subject (Nom) | Plural Object (Acc) | Pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| ο φίλος | **οι** φίλ**οι** | **τους** φίλ**ους** | -ος ➔ -οι / -ους |
| ο πατέρας | **οι** πατέρ**ες** | **τους** πατέρ**ες** | -ας ➔ -ες |
| ο μαθητής | **οι** μαθητ**ές** | **τους** μαθητ**ές** | -ης ➔ -ες |

> [!WARNING]
> **The -οι vs -ους Trap**  
> For nouns ending in **-ος**, the plural "The" is **οι** (Subject) but becomes **τους** (Object). The noun ending follows exactly: **-οι** matches **οι**, and **-ους** matches **τους**.
> *   **Οι** καλ**οί** φίλ**οι** είναι εδώ. (The good friends are here - Subject).
> *   Βλέπω **τους** καλ**ούς** φίλ**ους**. (I see the good friends - Object).

> [!TIP]
> **Pro-Tip: The Shifting Accent**  
> If a masculine noun in -ος has its accent on the third syllable from the end (like ο **άν**θρωπος - the person), the accent moves one syllable to the right in the Accusative plural (**τους ανθρώπους**). 

### The "Extra Syllable" Masculines (Imparisyllabic Nouns)
Many everyday masculine nouns ending in **-άς**, **-ής**, **-ές**, or **-ούς** add an extra syllable (**-δες**) in the plural. 
*   ο παπάς (the priest) ➔ **οι παπάδες**.
*   ο μανάβης (the greengrocer) ➔ **οι μανάβηδες**.
*   ο καφές (the coffee) ➔ **οι καφέδες**.

---

#### 2. Feminine Plurals (Οι / Τις)
Feminine nouns are much easier. The noun ending never changes between subject and object—only the article flips!

| Singular | Plural Subject (Nom) | Plural Object (Acc) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| η μητέρα / η αγάπη | **οι** μητέρ**ες** / **οι** αγάπ**ες** | **τις** μητέρ**ες** / **τις** αγάπ**ες** |

> [!CAUTION]
> **The Academic Exception (-εις)**  
> While everyday words use **-ες**, many formal or academic nouns ending in **-ση**, **-ξη**, or **-ψη** form their plural with **-εις**. 
> *   η λύση (solution) ➔ **οι λύσεις**.
> *   η σκέψη (thought) ➔ **οι σκέψεις**.
> *(Note: If the word has more than two syllables, the accent moves one syllable forward in the plural: η κυ**βέρ**νηση ➔ **οι κυβερνήσεις**)*.

### The "Extra Syllable" Feminines
Just like some masculine nouns, feminine nouns ending in a stressed **-ά** or **-ού** add **-δες** in the plural.
*   η γιαγιά (grandmother) ➔ **οι γιαγιάδες**.
*   η αλεπού (fox) ➔ **οι αλεπούδες**.

> [!WARNING]
> **The Masked Feminines (-ος)**  
> Remember *η οδός* (the street) from Unit 2? Feminine nouns ending in **-ος** take masculine endings in the plural (**-οι, -ους**), but keep their feminine articles!
> *   Subject: **Οι** οδ**οί**.
> *   Object: **Τις** οδ**ούς**.

---

#### 3. Neuter Plurals (Τα)
Neuter nouns are the most stable. Both the article **τα** and the noun endings remain the exact same whether they are the subject or the object.

| Singular | Plural (Nom & Acc) | Pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| το βιβλίο | **τα** βιβλί**α** | -ο ➔ -α |
| το παιδί | **τα** παιδι**ά** | -ι ➔ -ια |
| το μάθημα | **τα** μαθήμα**τα** | -μα ➔ -ματα |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Ultimate Trap: Neuters in -ος**  
> There is a huge group of neuter nouns ending in **-ος** (like το λάθος - the mistake, το κράτος - the state). In the plural, they drop the -ος and add **-η**. Do not confuse these with masculine nouns!
> *   το λάθος ➔ **τα** λάθ**η**.
> *   το δάσος (the forest) ➔ **τα** δάσ**η**.

---

#### 4. Adjective & Verb Agreement
When the subject is plural, the adjective and the verb must also be plural. Adjectives perfectly match the gender and case of the noun.

*   **Singular:** **Ο** καλ**ός** φίλ**ος** διαβάζ**ει**. (The good friend reads).
*   **Plural:** **Οι** καλ**οί** φίλ**οι** διαβάζ**ουν**. (The good friends read).
*   **Neuter Plural:** **Τα** μικρ**ά** παιδι**ά** παίζ**ουν**. (The small children play).

---

*   [ ] I know that masculine **-ος** nouns change to **-οι** (subject) and **-ους** (object).

*   [ ] I can correctly pluralize neuter nouns ending in **-μα** (**μαθήματα**) and **-ος** (**λάθη**).

### Unit 9: Verb Groups B1 & B2 (The Accented Verbs)

In Unit 4, you learned Type A (First-Conjugation) verbs (like **κάνω**), where the accent is fixed on the stem. Now we meet **Type B (Second-Conjugation)**—verbs where the accent is on the very last vowel of the dictionary form (**-ώ**). 

These verbs are the "singers" of Greek; they have a distinct rhythm because the stress falls on the endings rather than the stem. They are split into two "vowel teams".

---

#### 1. Team B1: The "Alpha" Lovers (-άω)
These are the most common Type B verbs (known in grammar as Type A of the Second Conjugation). They are characterized by the **α** vowel in their endings. 
*Examples:* **αγαπώ (αγαπάω), μιλάω, ρωτάω, ξυπνάω, γελάω**.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: μιλάω (speak) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-άω / -ώ** | μιλάω / μιλώ |
| (Εσύ) | **-άς** | μιλάς |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-άει / -ά** | μιλάει / μιλά |
| (Εμείς) | **-άμε / -ούμε** | μιλάμε / μιλούμε |
| (Εσείς) | **-άτε** | μιλάτε |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-άνε / -ούν(ε)** | μιλάνε / μιλούν(ε) |

> [!TIP]
> **Spoken vs. Written (Formal vs. Informal)**  
> You will often see two sets of endings for these verbs. The endings **-άω, -άει, -άμε**, and **-άνε** are regarded as less formal and are extremely common in everyday spoken Greek. The alternative forms (**-ώ, -ά, -ούμε, -ούν**) are slightly more formal. For a natural, conversational feel, we recommend sticking to the **-άω** pattern as your default!

---

#### 2. Team B2: The "Oo" Lovers (-ώ)
These verbs are less common but very important (known in grammar as Type B of the Second Conjugation). They lack the "Alpha" sound and instead use **-ει** and **-ου** sounds in their endings. 
*Examples:* **μπορώ, ζω, τηλεφωνώ, οδηγώ, παρακαλώ**.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: μπορώ (can) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ώ** | μπορώ |
| (Εσύ) | **-είς** | μπορείς |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-εί** | μπορεί |
| (Εμείς) | **-ούμε** | μπορούμε |
| (Εσείς) | **-είτε** | μπορείτε |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ούν(ε)** | μπορούν(ε) |

> [!NOTE]
> **Notice the Pattern!**   
> Except for the first person "I" (**-ώ**) and the "We" and "They" forms, the B2 endings look exactly like the Type A (First-Conjugation) endings you learned in Unit 4 (**-εις, -ει, -ετε, -ουν**), but with an extra vowel "kick" and the **accent firmly placed on the ending**.

---

#### 3. How to tell them apart?
*   If a verb has an **-άω** version in the dictionary or in speech (like *μιλάω / μιλώ*), it's **B1**.
*   If it **ONLY** ends in **-ώ** (like *μπορώ*), it's **B2** (you can never say "μποράω").

> [!WARNING]
> **The "Crossover" Verbs**  
> Greek is flexible! Some verbs can actually follow **either** Team B1 or Team B2. Verbs like **τηλεφωνώ** (telephone), **ακολουθώ** (follow), **προχωρώ** (proceed), and **συζητώ** (discuss) can be conjugated with B1 endings in casual speech (*τηλεφωνάω, συζητάμε*) or with B2 endings in more formal contexts (*τηλεφωνώ, συζητούμε*). When in doubt, B2 endings always sound more formal and polite for these crossover verbs.

---

#### 🎯 Unit 9 Mastery Checklist
*   [ ] I know that B1 and B2 verbs always carry the stress on their endings, not the stem.
*   [ ] I can conjugate **μιλάω** (B1) and **μπορώ** (B2) in the present tense.
*   [ ] I know that B1 verbs use **-άμε** and **-άτε** for 'we' and 'you all', and that the **-άω** forms are more conversational.
*   [ ] I recognize that B2 endings look like Type A endings but carry the stress on the final vowels.
*   [ ] I am aware that some verbs can "cross over" between B1 and B2 depending on formality.

### Unit 10: Verbal Aspect & The Subjunctive (Να)

Welcome to the great divide in the Greek language. To move from A1 to A2, you must understand **Aspect**. Greek doesn’t just care about *when* something happens (Tense); it deeply cares about *how* it happens (Aspect). 

---

#### 1. The "Video vs. Photo" Concept (Aspect)
Greek forces you to choose between two ways of looking at an action:

*   **Continuous (Imperfective) Aspect — The Video:** Used for habits, routines, or ongoing background actions that are in progress or repeated. (e.g., "I am playing", "I play every day").
*   **Simple (Perfective) Aspect — The Photo:** Used for a single, specific, or completed event viewed in its totality. (e.g., "I want to play *one* game").

Because of this, every active Greek verb essentially has two stems:
*   **Continuous Stem:** The one you already know from the Present tense (e.g., **παίζ-**).
*   **Simple Stem:** The "Perfective" stem (e.g., **παίξ-**). We will learn exactly how to build this in the next units, but you will start seeing it everywhere now!

---

#### 2. The Subjunctive Mood (Magic words: Να & Ας)
Greek has no "infinitive" form of verbs (like the English "to go" or "to play"). Instead, it uses the subjunctive mood, formed by the particle **να** followed by a conjugated verb. 

| English | Greek (Literal translation) | Greek (Actual) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I want **to go**. | I want *that I go*. | Θέλω **να πάω**. |
| You want **to play**. | You want *that you play*. | Θέλεις **να παίξεις**; |

### When to use Να:
*   **After Verbs of Wishing, Ability, & Obligation:** This is the most common use. You use it after verbs like **θέλω** (I want), **μπορώ** (I can), and the impersonal **πρέπει** (I must).
*   **Deliberative Questions:** To ask for instructions or offer something. *Να σε ρωτήσω κάτι;* (May I ask you something? / Should I ask you something?).
*   **Commands & Wishes:** **Να** προσέχεις! (Take care / You should be careful!).

> [!TIP]
> **Bonus Magic Word: Ας (Let's / Let)**  
> While *να* is the standard subjunctive particle, Greek also uses **ας** to express suggestions, permission, or indifference. 
> *   *Ας φύγει.* (Let him go.)
> *   *Ας αφήσουμε αυτό το θέμα.* (Let's leave this subject.)

> [!WARNING]
> **The Subjunctive Negation Rule (Μη vs. Δεν)**  
> This is a huge rule! Indicative verbs (normal statements) are negated with **δεν** (I *do not* want = *δεν* θέλω). However, any verb introduced by **να** or **ας** MUST be negated by **μη(ν)**.
> *   Θέλω **να μην** έρθει. (I want him *not* to come.)
> *   **Να μην** το πείτε σε κανέναν. (You shouldn't tell anybody.)
> *(Note: μη(ν) follows the exact same "dropping 'N'" pronunciation rules you learned in Unit 5!)*

---

#### 3. Aspect in the Subjunctive
Since **να** is followed by a verb, you must choose which "Aspect" (Video or Photo) to use for that verb:

*   **Θέλω να παίζω** *(Continuous/Video)*: I want to *be playing* (regularly, as a hobby, or continuously).
*   **Θέλω να παίξω** *(Simple/Photo)*: I want to *play* (this specific game, right now, just once).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Secret of "Simple"**  
> Most of the time, when you ask for something, want to do something, or say you must do something, you are talking about a specific, single event. Therefore, **the Simple (Perfective) Stem is by far the most common one used with να**! 

---

*   [ ] I know that Greek uses **να** + a conjugated verb instead of an infinitive (e.g., Θέλω να πάω vs Θέλεις να πας).
*   [ ] I know how to use **ας** for "Let / Let's".
*   [ ] I remember the golden rule: Subjunctive verbs (after **να/ας**) are ALWAYS negated with **μη(ν)** instead of *δεν*.

### Unit 11: Future Tenses (Θα) & Aspect

In Unit 10, you learned the magic of Aspect. Now, we apply that "Video vs. Photo" logic to the future. In Greek, we don't just say "I will play"; we specify if it's an ongoing habit or a one-time completed thing.

---

#### 1. The Future Particle: Θα
Just like *να* and *ας*, the word **θα** is a particle that sits right in front of the verb to express actions expected to take place in the future. It translates to the English "will".

*   **English:** I will play.
*   **Greek:** **Θα** [verb].

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Pronoun Sandwich**  
> The particle *θα* can only be separated from the verb by weak object pronouns. If you have weak pronouns (like "me", "it", "him"), they must sneak **between** *θα* and the verb.
*   *Θα το κάνω.* (I will do it).
*   *Θα σου το δώσει.* (He will give it to you).

---

#### 2. Continuous (Imperfective) Future: The Video
If you want to talk about a habit, a routine, or something you will be doing repeatedly or continuously in the future, use the **Continuous (Imperfective) Future**.

**Formula:** Θα + Present Tense Verb
*   Θα **διαβάζω** κάθε μέρα. (I will [be reading] every day).
*   Κάθε Τετάρτη θα **τρώμε** στην ταβέρνα. (Every Wednesday we will eat at the taverna).

---

#### 3. Simple (Perfective) Future: The Photo
If you are talking about a single, specific event that will happen and be completed at a particular point in time, use the **Simple (Perfective) Future**. 

**Formula:** Θα + Dependent Form (Perfective Non-Past Stem + Present Endings)
*   Θα **διαβάσω** το βιβλίο αύριο. (I will read the book tomorrow—once).
*   Θα **συναντήσω** τη Μαίρη αύριο. (I will meet Mary tomorrow).

> [!CAUTION]
> **The Augment Trap (No Past Markings!)**  
> Even though the Simple Future relies on the perfective stem (often found by looking at the Simple Past tense), you must **NEVER add the past tense augment** (the prefix **ε-**) to the future. The augment is strictly a past tense marker!
*   *Simple Past:* **έ**γραψα (I wrote).
*   *Simple Future:* θα γράψω (I will write - *Notice the 'ε' is gone!*). 

---

#### 4. The Negation Combo
To negate a future sentence ("I will not" / "I won't"), you must use the indicative negative particle **δεν**. It is always placed immediately before **θα**.

*   **Δεν θα παίζω** (I won't be playing regularly).
*   **Δεν θα παίξω** (I won't play this specific time).
*   **Δεν θα σου το δώσει** (He won't give it to you).

> [!TIP]
> **Bonus Native Secret: "Θα" for Probability**  
> Greeks don't just use *θα* for the future. They also use it to express probability or supposition, similar to the English "must" or "must have"!
*   *Θα έφτασε.* (He must have arrived - *using the Simple Past*).
*   *Θα είναι σπίτι.* (He must be at home - *using the Present*).

### Unit 12: Building the Simple Stem (Σ, Ξ, Ψ)

To speak in the Future or the Subjunctive, you need the **Simple (Perfective) Stem**. This is the "Photo" stem, used for single, completed actions. Finding this stem is like a puzzle: you look at the ending of the verb's Continuous (Present) stem and swap it for a new one. 

There are three main "Cheat Codes" to master for Type A (First-Conjugation) verbs.

---

#### 1. The "S" Group (Σ) ➔ Most common
If the present stem ends in a dental consonant (like **-θω**), a nasal consonant (like **-νω**), the letter **-ζω**, or simply a **vowel** (like -ούω or -ύω), the stem usually changes to a **-σ-**.

*   **διαβάζω** (read) ➔ **διαβάσ-**
*   **κλείνω** (close) ➔ **κλείσ-**
*   **νιώθω** (feel) ➔ **νιώσ-**
*   **ακούω** (hear) ➔ **ακούσ-**

---

#### 2. The "X" Group (Ξ) ➔ Throat sounds
If the verb ends in a velar "throat" sound like **-κω, -γω, -χω,** or **-σκω**, it explodes into a **-ξ-** (Xi). 

*   **ανοίγω** (open) ➔ **ανοίξ-**
*   **τρέχω** (run) ➔ **τρέξ-**
*   **διδάσκω** (teach) ➔ **διδάξ-**

> [!WARNING]
> **The -ζω Exceptions!**  
> While most verbs ending in -ζω go to the "S" group, some very common ones actually belong to this "X" group. You must memorize these:
> *   **παίζω** (play) ➔ **παίξ-**
> *   **αλλάζω** (change) ➔ **αλλάξ-**
> *   **φωνάζω** (shout/call) ➔ **φωνάξ-**
> *   **κοιτάζω** (look at) ➔ **κοιτάξ-**

---

#### 3. The "PS" Group (Ψ) ➔ Lip sounds
If the verb ends in a labial "lip" sound like **-πω, -βω, -φω**, or **-πτω**, it explodes into a **-ψ-** (Psi).

*   **γράφω** (write) ➔ **γράψ-**
*   **λείπω** (be absent) ➔ **λείψ-**
*   **κρύβω** (hide) ➔ **κρύψ-**

> [!TIP]
> **The -εύω / -αύω Rule**  
> When a verb ends in **-εύω** or **-αύω**, the 'υ' transforms into a lip sound! These verbs also take the **-ψ-** ending.
> *   **δουλεύω** (work) ➔ **δουλέψ-**
> *   **παύω** (stop) ➔ **πάψ-**

---

#### 4. Why does this matter?
Now that you have the perfective stem (e.g., **γράψ-**), you simply add the standard present tense endings (-ω, -εις, -ει, etc.). You can use it for everything you learned in Units 10 and 11:

*   **Future (Θα):** Θα γράψω. (I will write—once).
*   **Subjunctive (Να):** Θέλω να γράψω. (I want to write—once).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Stress Rule**  
> For regular Type A verbs, the stress falls on the last syllable of the perfective stem (θα γρ**ά**ψω, να δι**α**βάσω).

---

#### 5. The "1-Syllable Rebels" (Irregulars)
Some verbs are complete rebels. Their perfective stems shrink down to just a single consonant or syllable. Because the stem is so short, **the stress moves to the ending** (e.g., θα δ**ώ**, να π**ει**ς). 

*   **βλέπω** (see) ➔ **δ-** (θα δώ)
*   **λέω** (say) ➔ **π-** (θα πώ)
*   **πίνω** (drink) ➔ **πι-** (θα πιώ)
*   **βρίσκω** (find) ➔ **βρ-** (θα βρώ)
*   **βγαίνω** (go out) ➔ **βγ-** (θα βγώ)
*   **μπαίνω** (go in) ➔ **μπ-** (θα μπώ)
*   **πάω** (go) ➔ **πα-** (θα πάω)
*   **έρχομαι** (come) ➔ **ερθ-** (θα έρθω)

### Unit 13: Direct & Indirect Object Pronouns (Weak Pronouns)

In Unit 3, you learned how to say "I see him" (*Τον βλέπω*). These are **Direct Objects**. Now, we learn how to handle the receiver of an action: the **Indirect Object** (e.g., "Give *to me*"). 

These short, unstressed pronouns are formally known as "weak" or "clitic" pronouns. Mastering them is essential for natural, everyday Greek conversation.

---

#### 1. The Two Types of Objects

### Direct Object (Accusative Case)
The direct target of the action.
*   **Με βλέπεις;** (Do you see me?).
*   **Τον ξέρω.** (I know him.)

### Indirect Object (Genitive Case)
The person receiving something, or to whom/for whom an action is done. In English, we usually add "to" or "for", but in Greek, we simply use the Genitive case form of the pronoun!
*   **Μου δίνεις...** (You give to me...)
*   **Σου λέω...** (I am telling to you...)

| English | Direct (Accusative) | Indirect (Genitive) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Me / To me** | με | μου |
| **You / To you (sg.)** | σε | σου |
| **Him / To him** | τον | του |
| **Her / To her** | τη(ν) | της |
| **It / To it** | το | του |
| **We / To us** | μας | μας |
| **You / To you (pl.)** | σας | σας |
| **Them (m/f/n)** | τους / τις / τα | τους |

> [!TIP]
> **Notice the Plurals!**  
> For "We" (**μας**) and "You plural" (**σας**), the Accusative and Genitive forms are exactly the same! 

---

#### 2. Position in the Sentence

These weak pronouns almost always sit **immediately in front of the verb**. Nothing can separate them from the verb except other weak pronouns.
*   *Statement:* **Σου το** δίνω. (I am giving it to you).
*   *With Particles:* They sit safely *between* the particles (**δεν, μην, θα, να, ας**) and the verb.
    *   **Δεν σου** το δίνω. (I am not giving it to you).
    *   **Θα μου** το πεις; (Will you tell it to me?).

> [!WARNING]
> **The Imperative Exception**  
> There is a major exception to the "front" rule. When you use an Imperative (a command) or a Gerund (an "-ing" word), the weak pronouns move to the **end** of the verb!
> *   *Normal:* Μου το λες. (You tell it to me).
> *   *Command:* Πες **μου το**. (Tell it to me!).
> 
> **Pronunciation Tip (Enclisis):** If adding the pronoun to the end of a command pushes the stress more than three syllables back, the verb gets an **extra accent mark**! (e.g., **Κοίταξέ με!** - Look at me!).

---

#### 3. Stacking Pronouns (The Double Pronoun Rule)

What if you want to say "I give it (the book) to her (Maria)"? In Greek, the person always comes before the thing. The rule is strict: **The Indirect Object (Genitive) always comes BEFORE the Direct Object (Accusative)**.

**Formula:** Indirect (Gen) + Direct (Acc) + Verb

*   **Της το** δίνω. (I give it to her).
*   **Μου το** λες; (Are you telling it to me?).
*   **Σας τα** γράφω. (I am writing them [neut] to you all).
*   **Μη μου τα** δείξεις. (Don't show them to me).

> [!TIP]
> **Native Speaker Secrets:**  
> *   **The "S' to" blend:** In rapid colloquial speech, the combination **σου το** is often squeezed together into a single sound: **σ' το**! (e.g., *Σ' το είπα!* - I told you it!).
> *   **Flexible Commands:** While the Genitive + Accusative order is perfectly strict before a verb, when they attach to the *end* of a command, Greeks are flexible! You can say either *Πες του το* OR *Πες το του* (Tell him it / Tell it him).

---

*   [ ] I have learned the plural forms (**μας, σας, τους**).

*   [ ] I know that pronouns go *after* the verb for commands (**Πες μου**).
*   [ ] I can negate a double-pronoun sentence: **Δεν σου το** λέω.

### Unit 14: The Past Tenses (Imperfect vs. Aorist)

Welcome to the capstone of the A2 Expansion stage. You’ve learned how to build stems and how to talk about the future. Now, we look back. In Greek, just like in the future, we have two ways to talk about the past.

> [!TIP]
> **"Aorist" vs. "Simple Past"**  
> While many traditional textbooks and dictionaries use the ancient term "Aorist", modern grammarians prefer the term **"Simple Past"**, as it describes the contemporary language much more accurately. In this course, we will use both terms interchangeably.

---

#### 1. The Two Past Tenses
*   **The Aorist (Simple Past/Perfective Past):** Presents an action as having been completed at some specific point in the past (e.g., "I played"). **Uses the Simple (Perfective) Stem.**
*   **The Imperfect (Continuous Past/Imperfective Past):** Presents an action, process, or state as occurring habitually or continuously in the past (e.g., "I was playing" or "I used to play"). **Uses the Continuous (Present) Stem.**

---

#### 2. Past Tense Conjugation & The Augment
Both past tenses (Imperfect and Aorist) use the exact same set of past-tense endings. Wait! The difference lies entirely in the Stem and the Augment.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: γράφω (Simple Past) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Εγώ** | **-α** | έ-γραψ-**α** |
| **Εσύ** | **-ες** | έ-γραψ-**ες** |
| **Αυτός/ή/ό** | **-ε** | έ-γραψ-**ε** |
| **Εμείς** | **-αμε** | γράψ-**αμε** |
| **Εσείς** | **-ατε** | γράψ-**ατε** |
| **Αυτοί/ές/ά** | **-αν(ε)** | έ-γραψ-**αν** / γράψ-**ανε** |

> [!NOTE]
> **Colloquial Tip:** The 3rd person plural form with the extra *-ε* (**γράψανε**, **διαβάζανε**) is extremely frequent in everyday spoken language, but less often used in formal writing.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Augment (έ- and ή-)**  
> Past tense forms in Greek always carry their stress on the **third syllable from the end**.
> *   **Syllabic Augment (έ-):** If the verb stem plus the ending only has two syllables, Greek prefixes an **έ-** to the beginning to carry the stress. This is why it appears in short forms (**έ-γρα-ψα**) but completely drops off in longer forms where the stress naturally shifts to the right (**γρά-ψα-με**, **γρά-ψα-τε**).
> *   **Vocalic Augment (ή-):** Verbs starting with a vowel often change their initial vowel to **ή-** in the past (e.g., ελπίζω -> **ή**λπιζα). 

---

#### 3. Side-by-Side: Video vs. Photo
Let's use the verb **παίζω** (I play).
*   *Continuous Stem:* **παίζ-**
*   *Simple Stem:* **παίξ-**

### A. The Imperfect (The Video)
*   **Έπαιζα** τένις κάθε μέρα. (I used to play tennis every day).
*   Use this for background info: "While I *was playing*, it started to rain."

### B. The Aorist (The Photo)
*   **Έπαιξα** τένις χθες. (I played tennis yesterday—once).
*   Use this for specific, completed events: "I went to the park."

---

#### 4. The Absolute Irregulars
These are the most common verbs in the past. You must memorize them. Notice how the first three have completely irregular 1-syllable stems, meaning they **do not** take the *έ-* augment in the Aorist (though they do in the Imperfect!).

1.  **βλέπω** (see) ➔ **είδα** (I saw)
2.  **λέω** (say) ➔ **είπα** (I said)
3.  **πάω** (go) ➔ **πήγα** (I went)
4.  **θέλω** (want) ➔ **ήθελα** (I wanted) - *Uses Vocalic Augment!*
5.  **ξέρω** (know) ➔ **ήξερα** (I knew) - *Uses Vocalic Augment!*

> [!CAUTION]
> **No "Photo" for Being & Having!**  
> The verbs **έχω** (have) and **είμαι** (be) are what grammarians call **Defective Verbs**. This means they only exist in the Continuous (Imperfective) aspect. 
> *   **έχω** ➔ **είχα** (I had)
> *   **είμαι** ➔ **ήμουν(α)** (I was)
> They **do not have** an Aorist (Photo) form. You cannot say "I was" or "I had" as a single, completed dot in time in Greek.

### Unit 15: The Genitive Case (Possession & Relationships)

You’ve mastered Subjects (Nominative) and Objects (Accusative). Now, we enter the world of Ownership. The Genitive case is the Greek equivalent of the English apostrophe "s" ('s) or the word "of". 

---

#### 1. The Genitive Articles
When a person or thing becomes an "Owner," the definite article must change. There is a very consistent pattern here:

| Gender | Singular Owner | Plural Owners |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | του | των |
| **Feminine** | της | των |
| **Neuter** | του | των |

> [!TIP]
> **The Universal Plural**  
> In the Genitive plural, gender doesn't exist! Every single plural owner uses the article **των** and the noun always ends in **-ων**.
> *   **Των** φίλ**ων**, **των** δασκάλ**ων**, **των** παιδι**ών**.

---

#### 2. Noun Endings (Singular)
How does the noun itself change to show possession? 

*   **Masculine (-ος, -ας, -ης):** Nouns ending in -ας or -ης simply drop the final **-ς** (*ο πατέρας* ➔ *του πατέρα*). Nouns in -ος change to **-ου**.
*   **Feminine (-η, -α):** Always add a final **-ς** (*η Μαρία* ➔ *της Μαρίας*).
*   **Neuter (-ο, -ι, -μα):** 
    *   Nouns ending in -ο change to **-ου** (*το βιβλίο* ➔ *του βιβλίου*). 
    *   Nouns ending in -ι change the final vowel to a diphthong **-ιού** (*το παιδί* ➔ *του παιδιού*). 
    *   Nouns ending in -μα actually add an extra syllable, becoming **-ματος** (*το πρόβλημα* ➔ *του προβλήματος*).

> [!WARNING]
> **The Neuter & Feminine "-ος" Traps**  
> *   **Neuters in -ος:** Remember words like *το λάθος* (the mistake)? In the Genitive singular, they change their ending to **-ους** (*το κράτος* ➔ *του κράτους*).
> *   **Feminines in -ος:** Words like *η οδός* (the street) behave like masculine nouns and take an **-ου** ending, but keep their feminine article (*η μέθοδος* ➔ *της μεθόδου*).

> [!TIP]
> **Pronunciation Secret: The Shifting Accent**  
> The Genitive case is famous for moving the stress of a word! If a noun (masculine or neuter) is stressed on the third syllable from the end, **the accent usually moves one syllable to the right** in the Genitive.
> *   ο **άν**θρωπος ➔ του αν**θρώ**που
> *   το **θέ**ατρο ➔ του θε**ά**τρου

---

#### 3. Possession & Relationships: The "Of" Rule
In English, we say "The teacher's book." In Greek, you must say "The book of the teacher." **The object being owned comes first, and both the item and the owner keep their articles**.
*   Ο σκύλος **του Γιώργου** (George's dog / The dog of George).
*   Το σπίτι **της Μαρίας** (Maria's house / The house of Maria).
*   Το χρώμα **του αυτοκινήτου** (The car's color / The color of the car).

The Genitive isn't just for physical ownership. It is used to express a variety of relationships:
*   **Creators and Creations:** *η δημιουργία του Παύλου* (Paul's creation).
*   **Age and Measurements:** *ένα παιδί τεσσάρων χρονών* (a four-year-old child).
*   **Contents or Quality:** *στιγμές ευτυχίας* (moments of happiness).

---

#### 4. Indirect Objects & Prepositions
As you learned in Unit 13, the Genitive also marks the Receiver of an action (the Indirect Object).
*   **Μου** δίνεις... (You give to me...).
*   **Του** λέω... (I am telling him...).

### The "Big Three" Genitive Prepositions
While you learned in Unit 5 that almost all prepositions in Greek take the Accusative case, there are three specific prepositions that **force the noun into the Genitive case**:
1.  **εναντίον** (against): *εναντίον του πολέμου* (against the war).
2.  **εξαιτίας** (because of): *εξαιτίας της βροχής* (because of the rain).
3.  **μεταξύ** (between/among): *μεταξύ μας* (between us).

---

*   [ ] I know the three prepositions that require the Genitive: **εναντίον, εξαιτίας, μεταξύ**.

### Unit 16: The Imperative Mood (Giving Orders)

The Imperative Mood is used to give positive commands, orders, or strong advice. In Greek, just like in the Future and Past tenses, giving a command is deeply tied to **Aspect** (Video vs. Photo). The imperative only has 2nd person forms (singular for "you" and plural for "you all" or formal "you").

---

#### 1. Simple Commands (The Photo: Do it once!)
This is the most common way to give a command. You use the **Simple (Perfective) Stem** that you learned in Unit 12.

### Active Simple Commands:
*   **Εσύ (Informal):** Add **-ε** to the simple stem (e.g., γράψ- ➔ **Γράψε!**). 
*   **Εσείς (Formal/Plural):** The ending depends on the last letter of the stem!
    *   If the stem ends in **λ, ρ, σ, ξ,** or **ψ**, add **-τε** (e.g., γράψ- ➔ **Γράψτε!**).
    *   If it ends in other consonants, add **-ετε** (e.g., πλύν- ➔ **Πλύνετε!**).
    *   *Note:* Verbs ending in 'ν' often take the shorter '-τε' ending in colloquial speech (e.g., **Κάντε!** - Do!).

> [!TIP]
> **Pronunciation Secret (Vowel Dropping)**  
> Active singular commands ending in **λ, ρ, σ, ξ,** or **ψ** often drop their final **-ε** when followed by a weak pronoun (like **το** or **τα**). 
> *   *Γράψ' το!* (Write it!).
> *   *Φέρ' τα βιβλία!* (Bring the books!).

### Passive Simple Commands:
Yes, passive verbs can be commands too! To form the singular, you add **-ου** to the *active* perfective stem. The plural is identical to the passive dependent form.
*   **Χάσου!** (Get lost! / Lose yourself!).
*   **Ετοιμάσου!** (Get ready!).
*   **Ετοιμαστείτε!** (Get ready, you all!).

---

#### 2. Continuous Commands (The Video: Keep doing it!)
Used for habits or ongoing actions. You use the **Continuous (Imperfective/Present) Stem**. There are no passive forms for continuous commands.

### First-Conjugation Verbs (Type A):
*   **Εσύ:** Add **-ε** (e.g., **Τρέχε!**). 
    > [!TIP]
    > For verbs with more than two syllables, the stress jumps back to the third syllable from the end (e.g., **Διάβαζε!**).
*   **Εσείς:** Add **-ετε** (e.g., **Τρέχετε!**). *Note: This form is identical to the normal Present tense!*.

### Second-Conjugation Verbs (The Accented Verbs):
*   **Type A (-άω):** Sg: **-α** (**Αγάπα!**), Pl: **-άτε** (**Αγαπάτε!**).
*   **Type B (-ώ):** They have *no singular form*! The plural is identical to the present tense (e.g., **Θεωρείτε!**).

---

#### 3. The Absolute Irregulars
Like most languages, the most common commands in Greek are irregular. You MUST memorize these:

*   **Λέω** (Say) ➔ **Πες! / Πείτε!** (or Πέστε!)
*   **Βλέπω** (Look) ➔ **Δες! / Δείτε!** (or Δέστε!)
*   **Βρίσκω** (Find) ➔ **Βρες! / Βρείτε!**
*   **Πίνω** (Drink) ➔ **Πιες! / Πιείτε!** (or Πιέστε!)
*   **Έρχομαι** (Come) ➔ **Έλα! / Ελάτε!**
*   **Πηγαίνω / Πάω** (Go) ➔ **Πήγαινε! / Πηγαίνετε!**

---

#### 4. Negative Commands (Μην) & The "Για" Particle
To say "Don't," you use the magic particle **μην** (instead of δεν) followed by the Subjunctive form. The word 'να' can optionally be placed before it.
*   **Μην κλείσεις την πόρτα.** (Don't close the door—this time).
*   **(Να) μην τρέχεις.** (Don't run—habitually).
*   *Note: If you want to shout a single-word command for "Don't!", you just say* ***Μη!***.

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: The "Για" Particle**  
> If you want to add extra encouragement to a positive command, put the hortatory particle **για** in front of it! It translates to "Do..." or "Come on...".
> *   **Για κάτσε!** (Do sit down!).
> *   **Για φέρε μας λίγα παγάκια.** (Do bring us some ice cubes).

*   [ ] I can give a simple command using the Simple Stem (**Γράψε**) and know when to use **-τε** vs **-ετε** for the plural.
*   [ ] I know how to drop the final vowel in phrases like **Γράψ' το**.
*   [ ] I can form continuous commands and know that Type B verbs have no singular continuous command.
*   [ ] I have memorized the irregular commands (**Πες, Δες, Βες, Έλα, Πήγαινε**).

### Unit 18: Passive Voice & Deponent Verbs (-ομαι)

Welcome to Type C verbs. These verbs don’t end in **-ω**; they end in **-ομαι** (o-me). This ending system is used for two very different things in Greek: the Passive Voice and Deponent Verbs.

---

#### 1. What are Deponent Verbs?
Some of the most common verbs in Greek look passive but are actually active in meaning. These are called **Deponent Verbs**. They only exist in the -ομαι form.

*   **έρχομαι** (I come) ➔ *Από πού έρχεσαι;*
*   **αισθάνομαι** (I feel) ➔ *Αισθάνομαι καλά.*
*   **χρειάζομαι** (I need) ➔ *Χρειάζομαι βοήθεια.*
*   **σκέφτομαι** (I think) ➔ *Τι σκέφτεσαι;*
*   **φοβάμαι / ντρέπομαι** (I fear / I am ashamed)

> [!CAUTION]
> **The "Semi-Deponents"**  
> Some very common verbs are "semi-deponent". This means they look passive in the Present and Imperfect tenses, but they suddenly switch to **Active** endings in the Simple Past!
> *   **γίνομαι** (I become) ➔ Past: **έγινα** (I became).
> *   **έρχομαι** (I come) ➔ Past: **ήρθα** (I came).
> *   **κάθομαι** (I sit) ➔ Past: **κάθισα** (I sat).

---

#### 2. Present Tense Conjugation
Both Passive and Deponent verbs of the First Conjugation use these "Type C" endings:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: έρχομαι (I come) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ομαι** | έρχ**ομαι** |
| (Εσύ) | **-εσαι** | έρχ**εσαι** |
| (Αυτό/ή/ό) | **-εται** | έρχ**εται** |
| (Εμείς) | **-όμαστε** | ερχ**όμαστε** |
| (Εσείς) | **-εστε / -όσαστε** | έρχ**εστε** / ερχ**όσαστε** |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ονται** | έρχ**ονται** |

> [!TIP]
> **The Accent Shift & Colloquial Forms**  
> Notice that in the "We" form (**ερχόμαστε**), the accent mark jumps one syllable to the right. This is a very common rhythmic rule in Greek! Also, for the "You all" form, **-εστε** is standard, but **-όσαστε** (with the same accent shift) is a very frequent colloquial alternative.

---

#### 3. The True Passive Voice
You can also take an active verb (like *πλένω* - I wash) and flip it to the passive form (*πλένομαι* - I am washed). The passive voice is often used reflexively in Greek (to do something to oneself).

*   **Active:** Πλένω το αυτοκίνητο. (I wash the car).
*   **Passive/Reflexive:** **Πλένομαι**. (I am washing myself / I am taking a bath).

### "By Someone" (Ο Ποιητής / The Agent)
To say "by" in a passive sentence, you use the preposition **από** + Accusative. The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive one.
*   *Active:* Ο Γιώργος διαβάζει το βιβλίο. (George reads the book).
*   *Passive:* Το βιβλίο διαβάζεται **από τον** Γιώργο. (The book is read by George).

---

#### 4. Past Passive (Aorist)
The simple past tense of passive verbs is characterized by the extra syllable **-ηκ-** (or variations of it) placed right before the past endings. The stress always falls on the third syllable from the end.

| Pronoun | Ending | Example: πλένομαι ➔ πλύθηκα |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| (Εγώ) | **-ηκα** | πλύθηκα (I was washed) |
| (Εσύ) | **-ηκες** | πλύθηκες |
| (Αυτός/ή/ό) | **-ηκε** | πλύθηκε |
| (Εμείς) | **-ήκαμε** | πλυθήκαμε |
| (Εσείς) | **-ήκατε** | πλυθήκατε |
| (Αυτοί/ές/ά) | **-ηκαν / -ήκανε** | πλύθηκαν / πλυθήκανε |

> [!WARNING]
> **The Consonant "Cheat Codes" Return!**  
> Do you remember the simple stem groups (Σ, Ξ, Ψ) from Unit 12? The consonant before the **-ηκα** ending changes depending on the active stem!
> 
> *   **The 'S' Group (ζω, νω, θω):** Uses **-στηκα** or **-θηκα**. 
>     *ετοιμάζω* ➔ ετοιμά**στ**ηκα (I got ready).
>     *δένω* ➔ δέ**θ**ηκα (I was tied).
> *   **The 'PS' (Lip) Group (πω, βω, φω, -εύω):** Uses **-φτηκα** (or formally *-φθηκα*).
>     *κρύβω* ➔ κρύ**φτ**ηκα (I hid myself).
> *   **The 'X' (Throat) Group (κω, γω, χω):** Uses **-χτηκα** (or formally *-χθηκα*).
>     *ανοίγω* ➔ ανοί**χτ**ηκα (I was opened).

### Unit 19: Voices, Moods & Special Forms

Greek verbs are highly inflected, meaning they change their endings to express a complex system of grammatical categories. Today, we review two of the most important concepts—Voices and Moods—and introduce the "Special Forms" of the Greek verb.

---

#### 1. Voices (Οι Φωνές)
Voice is the grammatical category that indicates the relationship between the subject and the action of the verb.

*   **Ενεργητική Φωνή (Active Voice):** The subject is the initiator or performer of the action. 
    *   *Εγώ πλένω το αυτοκίνητο.* (I wash the car).
    *   *Η Ελένη ενοχλεί τον Μάρκο.* (Helen annoys Mark).
*   **Παθητική Φωνή (Passive Voice):** The subject undergoes or is affected by the action, or acts upon itself (reflexive). 
    *   *Εγώ πλένομαι.* (I am being washed / I wash myself).
    *   *Ο Μάρκος ενοχλείται από την Ελένη.* (Mark is annoyed by Helen).

> [!TIP]
> **Reminder: The Deponent Verbs**  
> As we saw in Unit 18, not all verbs have both voices. Some verbs have passive endings but are entirely **active** in meaning. These are called Deponent verbs (e.g., *έρχομαι* - I come, *κοιμάμαι* - I sleep). 

---

#### 2. Moods (Οι Εγκλίσεις)
Mood indicates the attitude of the speaker towards the action (whether it is a fact, a wish, or a command).

*   **Οριστική (Indicative Mood):** Used for facts, reality, and making statements. It is characterized by the absence of the particles **να** or **ας**, and it is always negated by **δεν**. 
    *   *Το παιδί παίζει.* (The child plays).
    *   *Η Ειρήνη αγόρασε νέο αυτοκίνητο.* (Irene bought a new car).
*   **Υποτακτική (Subjunctive Mood):** Used to express wishing, hoping, expecting, planning, and possibilities. It uses the same verb forms as the Indicative, but it is introduced by the particles **να** or **ας**, and it is ALWAYS negated by **μην**. 
    *   *Θέλω να παίξω.* (I want to play).
    *   *Ας φύγει.* (Let him/her go).
*   **Προστακτική (Imperative Mood):** Used to give direct commands, orders, and requests to the hearer. 
    *   *Διάβασε!* (Read!).
    *   *Γράψε το γράμμα!* (Write the letter!).

---

#### 3. Special Forms (Participles & The Non-Finite Form)
Greek verbs have a few invariant or special forms that do not behave like normal conjugated verbs.

### A. The Gerund / Active Participle (Η Μετοχή σε -οντας/-ώντας)
This is an uninflected (frozen) verb form used as an adverb. It corresponds to the English "-ing" form when used to express manner, cause, or two actions happening at the same time.
*   It ends in **-οντας** (if the stress is on the stem) or **-ώντας** (if the stress is on the ending).
*   *Ήρθε τρέχοντας.* (He came running).
*   *Παίζοντας και γελώντας.* (Playing and laughing).

### B. The Passive Perfect Participle (Η Παθητική Μετοχή)
Unlike the gerund, this participle acts exactly like an adjective and must perfectly agree with the noun in gender, number, and case. It indicates a completed state resulting from an action.
*   It always ends in **-μένος, -μένη, -μένο**.
*   *κουρασμένος* (tired).
*   *Ένα καλά οργανωμένο συνέδριο.* (A well-organized conference).

> [!WARNING]
> **The "Infinitive" Myth**  
> English relies heavily on the infinitive ("to play", "to go"). **Greek does not have an infinitive**. When you want to say "I want to play", you use the Subjunctive (*Θέλω να παίξω* - "I want that I play"). 
>
> Greek only has a **Non-Finite form (Απαρέμφατο)**, which ends in **-ει** and looks identical to the 3rd person singular of the dependent. It is **never** used on its own; it is ONLY used directly after the auxiliary verb *έχω* to create the Perfect Tenses!
*   *Έχω γράψει* (I have written).
*   *Είχα χάσει* (I had lost).

---

### Unit 20: The Perfect Tenses (I have done)

In Greek, the Perfect tenses (Present Perfect, Past Perfect/Pluperfect, and Future Perfect) are built almost exactly like in English. You use an auxiliary (helper) verb "to have" combined with a special, unchanging action word. 

---

#### 1. The Helper Verb: Έχω
You already know how to conjugate **έχω**. In these tenses, it acts as the "motor" that drives the action. Depending on the timeline, you will use its Present or Past (Imperfect) forms.

| Pronoun | Present (I have) | Past (I had) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Εγώ** | έχω | είχα |
| **Εσύ** | έχεις | είχες |
| **Αυτός/ή/ό** | έχει | είχε |
| **Εμείς** | έχουμε | είχαμε |
| **Εσείς** | έχετε | είχατε |
| **Αυτοί/ές/ά** | έχουν(ε) | είχαν(ε) |

---

#### 2. The Frozen Word (The Non-finite / Απαρέμφατο)
To create the action word (e.g., "played", "read", "written"), you take the Simple Stem (from Unit 12) and add a specific ending. This special verb form is called the **non-finite** (απαρέμφατο). It is completely "frozen"—it never changes regardless of the person.

### Active Voice (-ει): 
For active verbs, add the ending **-ει** to the active simple stem. The stress always falls on the penultimate (last but one) syllable.
*   παίζω ➔ παίξ- ➔ **παίξει**
*   γράφω ➔ γράψ- ➔ **γράψει**
*   διαβάζω ➔ διαβάσ- ➔ **διαβάσει**

### Passive & Deponent Voice (-εί):
The perfect tenses exist in both active and passive voices. To form the passive non-finite, add **-εί** to the passive perfective stem (the one that usually has a *θ, στ, φτ,* or *χτ*). **Notice that the stress always falls on the final syllable!**. Deponent verbs (like *κοιμάμαι*) only have this passive form, even though their meaning is active.
*   χάνομαι (be lost) ➔ χαθ- ➔ **χαθεί**.
*   κοιμάμαι (sleep) ➔ κοιμηθ- ➔ **κοιμηθεί**.

> [!WARNING]
> **The 1-Syllable Irregulars**  
> The irregular verbs that have a 1-syllable dependent form (like *δω, πω, βρω*) create a 1-syllable non-finite word. 
> *   βρίσκω ➔ **βρει**
> *   βλέπω ➔ **δει**
> *   λέω ➔ **πει**

---

#### 3. Assembling the Present Perfect
Simply put the helper verb and the frozen word together. It is used to express an action completed in the past that has relevance to the present.
*   **Έχω παίξει.** (I have played).
*   **Έχουν διαβάσει.** (They have read).
*   **Έχεις κοιμηθεί;** (Have you slept?).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Pronoun Rule**  
> If you want to use weak object pronouns ("it", "him", "to me"), they **must** be placed immediately before the appropriate form of **έχω**. If the sentence is negative, they go right after **δεν**.
> *   **Το** έχω διαβάσει. (I have read *it*).
> *   **Δεν μας έχει** μιλήσει. ((S)he hasn't spoken *to us*).

---

#### 4. Past & Future Perfect
To change the timeline, you only change the helper verb **έχω**. The frozen word stays exactly the same!

### Past Perfect / Pluperfect (I had done)
Use the past tense helper **είχα** to express an action completed before a specific time in the past.
*   **Είχα παίξει.** (I had played).
*   **Είχα χαθεί.** (I had been lost / I had lost myself).

### Future Perfect (I will have done)
Use the future particle **θα** + the present helper **έχω**. It expresses an action that will be completed by a particular point in the future. 
*   **Θα έχω παίξει** μέχρι αύριο. (I will have played by tomorrow).
*   *Pronoun reminder:* **Θα σου το** έχουν στείλει. (They will have sent *it to you*).

---

*   [ ] I can form the passive/deponent frozen **"-εί"** word, keeping the stress on the final syllable.
*   [ ] I know that the helper verb **έχω / είχα** is the only part that conjugates.

### Unit 21: Conditionals (The 'If' Clauses)

Conditional sentences are "If X, then Y" statements. In Greek grammar, the "If" clause is referred to as the **protasis**, and the main "then" clause is the **apodosis**. 

The standard word for "If" is **Αν**. Depending on how likely the situation is to happen, conditional constructions are divided into two main categories: Factual (Real) and Counterfactual (Unreal).

> [!TIP]
> **Native Alternatives to "Αν"**  
> While **Αν** is the most common word, you will often hear Greeks use **άμα** (if / whenever), the more formal **εάν** (if), or **εφόσον** (provided that) to introduce an "if" clause.

---

#### 1. Type 1: The Factual (Real)
Use this for facts, plans, or likely events. If the condition is fulfilled, the main clause happens. 

**Formula:** Αν + [Any Tense except Pluperfect], then [Any Tense/Command].
Even though you can use almost any tense, the most common combination is Present/Future in the "If" clause, and Future/Imperative in the main clause.
*   **Αν προσπαθείς** να τον πείσεις, **κάνεις** μεγάλο λάθος. (If you are trying to persuade him, you are making a big mistake).
*   **Άμα σε ενοχλεί** ο καπνός, **άνοιξε** το παράθυρο. (If the smoke bothers you, open the window).
*   **Αν τελειώσετε** νωρίς, **ελάτε** να μας δείτε. (If you finish early, come to see us).

> [!WARNING]
> **Negating the "If"**  
> How do you say "If you don't..."? Even though "If" phrases feel hypothetical, conditional clauses introduced by **αν** or **άμα** are strictly negated by the indicative particle **δεν** (NOT μην). 
*   **Αν δεν βρέχει...** (If it does **not** rain...).

---

#### 2. Type 2: The Unreal (Dreaming / Present Counterfactual)
Use this for hypotheticals, dreams, or giving advice that are not being realized in the present. Greek pushes both verbs into the **Continuous Past (Imperfect)**.

**Formula:** Αν + [Imperfect], then Θα + [Imperfect] (The Conditional).
*   **Αν είχα** λεφτά, **θα αγόραζα** ένα πλοίο. (If I had money [now], I would buy a boat).
*   **Αν καταλάβαινες** τη στενοχώρια μου, **δεν θα μιλούσες** έτσι. (If you understood my distress, you wouldn't speak like this).

---

#### 3. Type 3: The Regret (Past Counterfactual)
Use this for things that never happened in the past. It’s too late!

**Formula:** Αν + [Pluperfect], then Θα + [Pluperfect] (The Perfect Conditional).
*   **Αν είχε στείλει** εκείνο το γράμμα, **θα τον είχε πείσει**. (If she had sent that letter, she would have persuaded him).
*   **Δεν θα τον παντρευότανε** ποτέ, **αν δεν τον αγαπούσε**. (She would never have married him, if she didn't love him).

> [!TIP]
> **The Imperfect Secret!**  
> Greek is incredibly flexible here. When referring to the past, you don't *have* to use the long Pluperfect tenses. You can actually just use the **Imperfect tense** (Type 2) to talk about the past!. 
*   **Αν έστελνε** εκείνο το γράμμα, **θα τον έπειθε**. 
> Depending on the context, this can mean *either* "If she sent that letter [now], she would persuade him" OR "If she had sent that letter [yesterday], she would have persuaded him". 

---

*   [ ] I understand the difference between the **protasis** (the "if" clause) and the **apodosis** (the "then" clause).

### Unit 22: Advanced Particles & Spoken Expressions

To sound like a native, you need to master the tiny, untranslatable words that Greeks sprinkle into their speech. Greek has a variety of very important little words called particles, which are highly versatile and often do not correspond precisely to any words in English. These "conversational particles" add flavor, doubt, sarcasm, or intense emotion to any sentence.

---

#### 1. Curiosity & Doubt

### Άραγε (I wonder...)
Placed at the beginning or end of a question. When used in yes/no questions, **άραγε** reduces the directness of the question and makes it sound less abrupt and perhaps more polite.
*   **Άραγε** θα έρθει η Μαρία; (I wonder if Maria will come?)
*   Πού να πήγε, **άραγε**; (Where can he have gone, I wonder?).

> [!TIP]
> **Bonus: Μήπως (By any chance...)**  
> Similar to **άραγε**, you can use **μήπως** (could it be the case that / by any chance) to soften a question and reduce its directness. 
*   **Μήπως** σε ενόχλησε; (Did he bother you, by any chance?).

### Τάχα & Δήθεν (Supposedly / "Yeah, right")
Used to show extreme sarcasm, pretense, or disbelief.
*   Κλαίει **δήθεν** επειδή χτύπησε. (He is supposedly crying [but I know he's faking]).
*   **Ναι, τάχα μου!** (Yeah, right! / Allegedly!)

---

#### 2. Wishes & Hopes

### Μακάρι (If only / Let's hope)
Triggers the Subjunctive (**να**) or stands alone as a hopeful response.
*   **Μακάρι** να περάσω τις εξετάσεις! (I hope I pass!)
*   *Person A:* Θα βρούμε τραπέζι; ➔ *Person B:* **Μακάρι!** (Let's hope!)

### Αχ (Oh! / If only!)
This exclamation of emotion is often combined with **να** and the imperfect tense to express an unfulfilled wish.
*   **Αχ, να σ' έβλεπα!** (Oh, if only I could see you!).

---

#### 3. Direction & Commands

### Ας (Let's / Let it be)
Like **να**, this particle triggers the Subjunctive, but unlike **να**, it is strictly used only in main clauses. In comparison with **να**, expressions with **ας** tend to be much less pressing, because **ας** combines the sense of a command with permission, or even indifference. 
*   **Ας πάμε!** (Let's go!)
*   **Ας έρθουν αν θέλουν.** (Let them come if they want [permission/indifference]).

> [!TIP]
> **The Past Regret**  
> You can also use **ας** with the imperfect tense to express a wish that something had happened (or not happened) in the past. 
*   **Ας μην τον άκουγες.** (If only you hadn't listened to him / You shouldn't have listened to him).

### Για (The Hortatory Particle)
This is a native secret! The hortatory particle **για** is used colloquially to express extra encouragement right before an imperative (command) form of the verb. It translates roughly to "Come on" or "Do...".
*   **Για κάτσε!** (Do sit down!).
*   **Για ρώτα κανένα δάσκαλο.** (Do ask a teacher).

### Άντε / Άιντε (Come on!)
The most versatile word in Greek. It can mean "Let's go!", "Hurry up!", or "Get out of here!"
*   **Άντε**, σήκω! (Come on, get up!)
*   **Άντε πάλι...** (Oh, here we go again...)

---

#### 4. Emotional Interjections
*   **Μπράβο!** (Well done! / Good!).
*   **Α μπα;** (Really? / No way!)
*   **Αλίμονο!** (Alas! / Of course! - depending on context).

### Unit 23: Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That)

Relative pronouns link two sentences together. Instead of saying "I saw a man. The man was tall," you say "I saw a man who was tall." In Greek, we have two ways to do this: the casual "easy" way and the formal way. 

---

#### 1. The Easy Way: Που
The word **που** is the ultimate chameleon. It is an indeclinable relative particle, meaning it never changes shape regardless of the noun's gender, number, or whether it refers to a human or an object. It can mean who, which, that, or where.
*   **Ο άντρας που είδα...** (The man [whom] I saw...)
*   **Το σπίτι που ζήσαμε...** (The house [that/where] we lived...)
*   **Το δέντρο που είδες...** (The tree [that] you saw...)

> [!WARNING]
> **Spelling Alert: που vs. πού;**  
> Don't confuse **που** (that/which) with **πού;** (Where?). The question word always has an accent; the relative pronoun never does. 

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: The "Resumptive" Pronoun**  
> When *που* refers to an object (especially an indirect object), Greeks frequently insert an optional weak pronoun *inside* the relative clause to clarify the meaning. 
*   **Η γιατρός που μου τη σύστησες.** (The (female) doctor **who** you recommended **her** to me / whom you recommended).
*   **Ο νεαρός που του δώσανε την υποτροφία.** (The young man **who** they gave **him** the scholarship / to whom they gave the scholarship).

---

#### 2. The Formal Way: Ο οποίος
If you are writing a formal letter or reading the news, you will see the relative pronoun phrase **ο οποίος**. Unlike *που*, this phrase consists of the definite article followed by the word *οποίος*. It acts like an adjective and must perfectly match the noun in Gender and Number, but its **Case** depends on its syntactic role in its own relative clause. 
*   **Ο άντρας ο οποίος...** (Masc. Nom)
*   **Η γυναίκα η οποία...** (Fem. Nom)
*   **Το παιδί το οποίο...** (Neut. Nom)

> [!TIP]
> **Advanced Tip: How to say "Whose"**  
> To say "whose", you simply use the Genitive case form of this pronoun (**του οποίου, της οποίας, των οποίων**). It can be placed either before or after the noun it possesses.
*   **Ο φίλος μου, του οποίου η μητέρα είναι δασκάλα...** (My friend, **whose** mother is a teacher...).
*   **Ο φίλος μου, η μητέρα του οποίου είναι δασκάλα...** (My friend, the mother **of whom** is a teacher...).

---

#### 3. Using Prepositions
When you need to use prepositions like "with," "from," or "in" before the relative pronoun, the formal **ο οποίος** is strongly preferred. The relative pronoun must immediately follow the preposition.
*   **Αυτοί είναι οι άνθρωποι από τους οποίους πήραμε πληροφορίες.** (These are the people **from whom** we got information).
*   **Το κτήριο δίπλα στο οποίο είναι η εκκλησία.** (The building **next to which** is the church).

In casual speech, Greeks bypass this completely by using *που* plus a prepositional phrase with a weak pronoun later in the sentence!
*   **Η κοπέλα που κάθεται κοντά της ο Νίκος...** (The girl **who** Nick is sitting **near her**...).

---

#### 4. Free Relatives ("Whoever" & "Whatever")
When the relative clause doesn't modify a specific noun, Greek uses "free" correlative pronouns.
*   **Όποιος, όποια, όποιο:** Whoever. (e.g., *Όποιος θέλει...* - Whoever wants...).
*   **Ό,τι:** Whatever or "that which". (e.g., *Κάνε ό,τι θέλεις.* - Do whatever you want).

> [!WARNING]
> **Spelling Alert: ότι vs. ό,τι**  
> Notice the comma in **ό,τι** (whatever). It is mandatory in writing to distinguish it from the complementizer **ότι** (that)!

If you want to sound even more emphatic, add the suffix **-δήποτε**.
*   **Οποιοσδήποτε:** Anyone / Whoever.
*   **Οτιδήποτε:** Anything / Whatever.

---

#### 5. "Of the kind that" (Που να)
If the main clause is negative, interrogative, or expresses a wish, the relative clause is often introduced by **που να** (or *ο οποίος να*) followed by the subjunctive mood. It means "of the kind that".
*   **Δεν ξέρω κανέναν που να μην αγαπάει τα λεφτά.** (I don't know anyone **who** doesn't like money).
*   **Θέλω ένα φίλο στον οποίο / που να μπορώ να έχω εμπιστοσύνη.** (I want a friend **whom** I can trust).

---

### Unit 24: Reflexive Pronouns (Myself)

Reflexive expressions in Greek are used to express a situation in which the subject of the sentence is both the actor of the verb and the patient (the receiver). 

There are three ways of achieving this in Greek: by the reflexive expression **ο εαυτός μου** (myself), by a passive verb form, or by the verbal prefix **αυτο-** attached to a passive verb.

---

#### 1. The Formula (Ο εαυτός μου)
The word **εαυτός** (self) is a masculine noun. To say "myself," you simply add the weak possessive pronoun after it. 

| Person | Greek | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st (Singular)** | τον εαυτό μου | myself |
| **2nd (Singular)** | τον εαυτό σου | yourself |
| **3rd (Singular)** | τον εαυτό του / της | himself / herself |
| **1st (Plural)** | τους εαυτούς μας | ourselves |
| **2nd (Plural)** | τους εαυτούς σας | yourselves |
| **3rd (Plural)** | τους εαυτούς τους | themselves |

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: The Singular Plural**  
> You might logically think that "ourselves" or "themselves" should always use the plural form (*τους εαυτούς μας/τους*). However, the definite article and the noun **εαυτός** are normally in the singular even when the possessive pronoun is in the plural! 
*   **Μόνο τον εαυτό τους κοιτάζουν.** (They only look after themselves).

---

#### 2. How to Use them
The definite article *ο* and the noun *εαυτός* appear most frequently in the Accusative case when the reflexive expression is the direct object of the verb.
*   **Αγαπώ τον εαυτό μου.** (I love myself).
*   **Προσέχεις τον εαυτό σου;** (Do you take care of yourself?).

The reflexive expression may also occur in a prepositional phrase.
*   **Πρέπει να έχεις εμπιστοσύνη μόνο στον εαυτό σου.** (You should only trust yourself).
*   **Η Μαρία έχει μεγάλη ιδέα για τον εαυτό της.** (Mary has a very high opinion of herself).

---

#### 3. Reflexive Verbs vs. Pronouns
In Unit 18, you learned that Passive Verbs (-ομαι) are often used reflexively. The passive verb form expresses reflexivity for some verbs of grooming or bodily care, especially when the subject is human.
*   **Πρέπει να ντύνεσαι ζεστά τον χειμώνα.** (You must dress [yourself] warm in the winter).
*   **Πάλι χτενίζεσαι;** (Are you combing your hair again?).

**So when do you use the pronoun?**  
You use the pronoun phrase (*τον εαυτό μου*) for special emphasis, or with verbs that do not inherently imply bodily care or do not have a reflexive passive form:
*   **Βλάπτω τον εαυτό μου.** (I am hurting myself).

---

#### 4. The Advanced "Αυτο-" Prefix
A few reflexives can be formed with the prefix **αυτο-** attached to the passive verb. This is a true mark of B2/C1 fluency and is often used in news and literature!
*   **Ο άνθρωπος αυτός αυτοκαταστράφηκε.** (This man destroyed himself).

---

### Unit 25: Adjective Comparison (Better, Faster)

In Greek, there are two ways to compare things (e.g., "tall" ➔ "taller"). You can use the easy "more" method or the specialized one-word "suffix" method.

---

#### 1. The Easy Way: Πιο + Adjective
This is currently the most common way to speak. Just put the word **πιο** (more) in front of the positive form of the adjective.
*   **Μεγάλος** (Big) ➔ **Πιο μεγάλος** (Bigger)
*   **Ωραίος** (Beautiful) ➔ **Πιο ωραίος** (More beautiful)

**How to say "than":**  
To express the second term of a comparison (e.g., "taller *than*..."), Greek uses the preposition **από** + Accusative case.
*   **Ο Γιώργος είναι πιο ψηλός από τον Νίκο.** (George is taller than Nikos).

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: The Genitive Pronoun**  
> Instead of using *από εμένα* (than me) or *από εσένα* (than you), native speakers often just attach a weak possessive pronoun in the Genitive case directly after the comparative adjective!
*   **Η Άννα είναι μεγαλύτερή μου.** (Anna is older *than me* / *my* senior).

---

#### 2. The Native Way: The -τερος Suffix
If you want to sound more precise or formal, you form a one-word comparative by adding the suffix **-τερος, -τερη, -τερο** to the stem of the adjective. 

**The Stress Rule:** Comparative forms with this suffix always have their stress on the third syllable from the end (the antepenultimate syllable).
*   **Ψηλός** ➔ **Ψηλότερος** (Taller)
*   **Νέος** (Young) ➔ **Νεότερος** (Younger)
*   **Σπουδαίος** (Important) ➔ **Σπουδαιότερος** (More important)

> [!WARNING]
> **The "Πιο" Only Adjectives**  
> Not all adjectives can take the *-τερος* suffix! You MUST use the *πιο* method for:
> 1. Passive participles ending in **-μένος** (e.g., *πιο κουρασμένος* - more tired).
> 2. Adjectives ending in **-ης, -ια, -ι** or **-ων, -ουσα, -ον**.
> 3. Indeclinable colors like **μπλε** or **ροζ** (e.g., *πιο μπλε*).

---

#### 3. Relative Superlatives (The Best)
To say "the most" or "the best," you are forming the Relative Superlative. You simply add the Definite Article (**ο, η, το**) in front of either of your comparative forms.
*   **Ο πιο καλός** (The best / The most good)
*   **Ο καλύτερος** (The better one / The best one)
*   **Το ψηλότερο κτήριο.** (The tallest building).

---

#### 4. The Absolute Superlative: -τατος
If you want to stress an exceptional property, saying something is "extremely" or "very, very" X, use the absolute superlative suffix **-τατος, -τατη, -τατο**. Like the comparative, the stress always falls on the third syllable from the end.
*   **Ωραιότατος** (Extremely beautiful / Most beautiful)
*   **Βαθύτατος** (Very deep indeed / Profound)
*   **Φθηνότατος** (Super cheap)

---

#### 5. The Top Irregulars
Like English ("good" ➔ "better", not "gooder"), the most common adjectives in Greek are irregular. You must memorize their comparative and absolute superlative forms:

| Positive | Comparative (Better/More) | Absolute Superlative (Extremely/Excellent) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Καλός** (Good) | **καλύτερος** | **άριστος / κάλλιστος** |
| **Κακός** (Bad) | **χειρότερος** | **χείριστος / κάκιστος** |
| **Πολύς** (Much/Many) | **περισσότερος** | **πλείστος** |
| **Μεγάλος** (Big) | **μεγαλύτερος** | **μέγιστος** (Maximal) |
| **Μικρός** (Small) | **μικρότερος** | **ελάχιστος** (Minimal) |
| **Λίγος** (Little) | **λιγότερος** (Less) | **ελάχιστος** (Extremely little) |

---

### Unit 26: Active & Passive Participles (Οι Μετοχές)

A Participle is a verb dressed up to act like an adjective or an adverb. In English, we use **-ing** (Running) or **-ed** (Locked). Greek has two powerful forms you must master at the B2 level: the Active Participle (Gerund) and the Passive Perfect Participle.

---

#### 1. The Active Participle / Gerund (-οντας / -ώντας)
This is an uninflected (frozen) verb form used as an adverb. It translates to the English "-ing" form. It is used to show manner, simultaneous time (two actions happening at the same time), and occasionally cause.

**Formula:** It is formed by adding a suffix to the continuous (imperfective) stem.
*   **τρέχω** ➔ **τρέχοντας** (while running / by running).
*   **παίζω** ➔ **παίζοντας** (while playing).
*   **γελώ** ➔ **γελώντας** (while laughing).

> [!WARNING]
> **Spelling & Stress Rule (-ο vs -ω):**  
> How do you know whether to write **-οντας** (with omicron) or **-ώντας** (with omega)? It all depends on the stress!
> *   If the stress falls on the **stem**, use **-ο-** (e.g., τρέχ**ο**ντας, παίζ**ο**ντας).
> *   If the stress falls on the **ending** (like Type B verbs), use **-ώ-** (e.g., αγαπ**ώ**ντας, γελ**ώ**ντας).

> [!TIP]
> **The Frozen Ending**  
> The active participle is indeclinable. It **never changes** to match gender or number!
*   **Ο αδελφός της ήρθε τρέχοντας.** (Her brother came running).

---

#### 2. The Passive Perfect Participle (-μένος)
Unlike the gerund, the passive perfect participle acts exactly like a normal adjective. It describes a completed state resulting from an action (e.g., Tired, Broken, Hidden).

Because it is an adjective, it must perfectly agree with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case.
*   **κουράζω** (tire) ➔ **κουρασμένος** (tired).
*   **χάνω** (lose) ➔ **χαμένος** (lost).
*   **Η πόρτα είναι κλεισμένη.** (The door is closed).
*   **Τα χαμένα λόγια.** (The wasted/lost words).

> [!TIP]
> **Advanced Tip: The Consonant "Cheat Codes"**  
> The passive participle is formed from the *passive perfective stem*. If you remember the consonant groups from Unit 12 and 18, you can predict the exact spelling!
> *   **The 'S' Group (ζω, νω, θω):** Uses **-σμένος** (e.g., ξεχνώ ➔ ξεχα**σμ**ένος).
> *   **The 'PS' (Lip) Group (πω, βω, φω):** The lips close completely, becoming **-μμένος** (e.g., γράφω ➔ γρα**μμ**ένος, κρύβω ➔ κρυ**μμ**ένος).
> *   **The 'X' (Throat) Group (κω, γω, χω):** The throat sound softens to **-γμένος** (e.g., ανοίγω ➔ ανοι**γμ**ένος, τραβώ ➔ τραβη**γμ**ένος).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Native Secret: The "Emotions" Exception**  
> Intransitive verbs (verbs without a direct object) normally do not have passive forms. However, some common verbs related to physical or emotional states *do* have passive perfect participles!
*   **πεινώ** (I am hungry) ➔ **πεινασμένος** (hungry).
*   **διψώ** (I am thirsty) ➔ **διψασμένος** (thirsty).
*   **θυμώνω** (I get angry) ➔ **θυμωμένος** (angry).
*   **αρρωσταίνω** (I get sick) ➔ **αρρωστημένος** (ill).

---

#### 3. Formal vs. Informal (-ευμένος vs -εμένος)
Verbs ending in **-εύω** have two participle options. The formal version ends in **-ευμένος** and the informal, everyday version ends in **-εμένος**.
*   **παντρεύω** (marry) ➔ **παντρεμένος** (informal/common) / παντρευμένος (formal).
*   **μπερδεύω** (confuse) ➔ **μπερδεμένος** (confused).

---

### Unit 27: Impersonal Verbs & Expressions

Sometimes a sentence doesn't have a specific "person" doing the action (e.g., "It is raining" or "It is necessary"). In English, we use a dummy "it" as the subject. In Greek, we use Impersonal Verbs which have no subject and are permanently frozen in the 3rd Person Singular. 

The most basic examples are weather verbs, like **βρέχει** (it is raining) and **χιονίζει** (it is snowing). But at the B2 level, impersonal verbs are used to express complex opinions, possibilities, and rules.

---

#### 1. The Power Pair: Πρέπει & Μπορεί
You’ve used these since A1, but now you must understand their advanced native nuances. Both verbs are almost always followed by the subjunctive particle **να**.
*   **Πρέπει να...** (It is necessary that... / Must)
*   **Μπορεί να...** (It is possible that... / Might / Maybe)

> [!WARNING]
> **Μπορώ vs. Μπορεί**  
> Remember that *μπορώ* can be conjugated when it means "ability", but stays frozen as *μπορεί* when it means "possibility".
> *   **Μπορώ** να πάω. (I can go—I have the ability).
> *   **Μπορεί** να πάω. (I might go—it is a possibility).

### Advanced B2 Nuances:
**1. Probability:** Just like English, Greeks use **πρέπει να** (must) to express strong probability or deduction about the past, rather than just obligation!
*   **Είδα φώτα. Πρέπει να έχουν γυρίσει.** (I saw lights. They *must have* returned).

**2. The Negation Trap:** Where you put the negative particle with *μπορεί* changes the meaning completely!
*   **Μπορεί να μην έχουν γυρίσει.** (They *may not* have returned).
*   **Δεν μπορεί να έχουν γυρίσει.** (They *can't* have returned - It is impossible!).

---

#### 2. "It's not going to happen": Πρόκειται
Along with *πρέπει*, one of the most common impersonal verbs in Greek is **πρόκειται** (to be about to / be in question). In everyday speech, it is heavily used in the negative to firmly state that something is simply not going to happen.
*   **Δεν πρόκειται να φύγουμε σήμερα.** (We're *not going to / not about to* leave today).
*   **Δεν πρόκειται να τον ξεχάσει.** (She is *not about to* forget him).

---

#### 3. Essential Intermediate Verbs
These verbs are frozen in the **-ει** or **-εται** forms and describe allowed actions, appearances, or values.
*   **Απαγορεύεται** (It is forbidden): *Απαγορεύεται το κάπνισμα.*
*   **Επιτρέπεται** (It is allowed): *Επιτρέπεται να μπεις.*
*   **Φαίνεται** (It seems): *Φαίνεται ότι δεν την πιστεύουν.* (It seems that they do not believe her).
*   **Πειράζει** (It matters / It bothers): *Τον πειράζει που του μιλάει απότομα.* (It bothers him that she speaks to him sharply).
*   **Αξίζει** (It is worth): *Αξίζει να το δεις.* 

> [!TIP]
> **Native Secret: Impersonal "Έχει"**  
> To say "There is" or "There are", Greeks colloquially use the frozen 3rd person singular verb **έχει** (literally "it has") instead of *υπάρχει*!
*   **Έχει πολλούς ανθρώπους εκεί μέσα.** (*There are* a lot of people in there).

---

#### 4. Impersonal "Είναι" Expressions
You can turn any adjective or noun into a command or observation by using **Είναι** (It is) followed by **να**, **ότι**, or **που**.
*   **Είναι δύσκολο να...** (It is difficult to...)
*   **Είναι πιθανόν να...** (It is likely that...).
*   **Είναι φανερό ότι...** (It is obvious that...).
*   **Είναι κρίμα που...** (It is a pity that...).

---

*   [ ] I know that impersonal verbs are frozen in the 3rd person singular (-ει or -εται) and have no subject.
*   [ ] I can use **Πρέπει να** to express probability ("they must have...").
*   [ ] I understand the massive difference between **Δεν μπορεί να** (impossible) and **Μπορεί να μην** (may not).
*   [ ] I can firmly deny something using **Δεν πρόκειται να...** (There's no way / Not about to...).
*   [ ] I know the colloquial trick of using **Έχει** to mean "There is/are".
*   [ ] I can use **Είναι** + Adjective + **να/ότι/που** to make impersonal observations.

### Unit 28: Idioms & Ancient Remnants

To reach true fluency (B2/C1), you must understand the "ghosts" of the past. Modern Greek is filled with ancient expressions and colorful idioms that don't follow modern rules.

---

#### 1. Ancient Dative Expressions
Ancient Greek had a "Dative" case (for/to) that vanished in modern times, but these frozen phrases survived. You must learn them as whole blocks:

*   **Εν τω μεταξύ** (In the meantime).
*   **Δόξα τω Θεώ** (Thank God / Glory to God).
*   **Εν πάση περιπτώσει** (In any case / Anyway).
*   **Τοις μετρητοίς** (In cash).

---

#### 2. Advanced Prepositions (Genitive)
While basic prepositions take the Accusative, formal/ancient ones used in news and business take the **Genitive**.

*   **Λόγω** + Genitive (Due to): **Λόγω βροχής** (Due to rain).
*   **Μέσω** + Genitive (Via): **Μέσω Αθηνών** (Via Athens).
*   **Ενώπιον** + Genitive (Before / In front of): **Ενώπιον του νόμου** (Before the law).

---

#### 3. Colorful Idioms
These are phrases where the literal meaning is completely different from the actual meaning.

*   **Έγινε της κακομοίρας!** (A disaster happened! / Hell broke loose!)
*   **Χάνω τα λόγια μου.** (I am lost for words).
*   **Πού το πας;** (What are you getting at? / Where are you going with this?)
*   **Περαστικά!** (Get well soon! / May it pass!)

---

#### 4. Compound "Lego" Words
Greek builds new words by snapping smaller ones together with the letter **"ο"** as glue.

*   **βιβλίο** (book) + **πωλείο** (shop) ➔ **βιβλιοπωλείο** (bookstore).
*   **ψάρι** (fish) + **ταβέρνα** ➔ **ψαροταβέρνα** (fish tavern).

### Diminutives & Augmentatives (Υποκοριστικά & Μεγεθυντικά)

Greek is a language of intense emotion. You don't just drink a coffee; you drink a *little* coffee with a friend. You don't just see a dog; you see a huge, monstrous dog. 

To express these feelings of smallness, affection, or enormous size, Greek uses special suffixes attached to the stem of nouns. Mastering these suffixes is the key to sounding like a true native speaker!

---

#### 1. The Diminutives (Making things small, cute, or polite)
Diminutives express small size, familiarity, affection, or sometimes even depreciation. They are incredibly common in spoken Greek. When you order food, you rarely order a "τυρόπιτα" (cheese pie). You order a "τυροπιτάκι" (little cheese pie), because it sounds friendlier!

**Suffix 1: -άκι (The Universal Tiny-Maker)**
This is by far the most common diminutive. It can be added to almost any noun, and it always turns the word into a Neuter noun (Το).
*   **το σπίτι** (the house) ➔ **το σπιτάκι** (the little house).
*   **το παιδί** (the child) ➔ **το παιδάκι** (the small child).
*   **ο καφές** (the coffee) ➔ **το καφεδάκι** (the small coffee).
*   **ο μεζές** (the appetizer) ➔ **το μεζεδάκι** (the snack).
*(Notice how even masculine nouns become neuter when you add -άκι!)*

**Suffix 2: -ίτσα & -ούλα (For Feminine Nouns)**
If you want to keep a word feminine while making it cute, you can use these suffixes attached to the stem.
*   **η μπύρα** (the beer) ➔ **η μπιρίτσα** (the little beer).
*   **η ώρα** (the hour) ➔ **η ωρίτσα** (a brief hour or so).
*   **η αδερφή** (the sister) ➔ **η αδερφούλα** (the little sister).
*   **η βάρκα** (the boat) ➔ **η βαρκούλα** (the little boat).
*(These are also very common for female names: η Ελένη ➔ **η Ελενίτσα**).*

**Suffix 3: -ούλης & -άκης (For Masculine Nouns)**
If you want to make a masculine person sound cute or endearing.
*   **ο πατέρας** (the father) ➔ **ο πατερούλης** (daddy).
*   **ο κόσμος** (the world/people) ➔ **ο κοσμάκης** (the ordinary people).
*(The -άκης suffix is also extremely common for male proper names, like Μανωλάκης).*

> [!TIP]
> **C1 Advanced Tip: Diminutive Adjectives!**  
> You can also make *adjectives* cute or "moderate"! Greek uses the suffix **-ούτσικος** to mean "to a moderate extent" (like the English "-ish").
> *   **καλός** (good) ➔ **καλούτσικος** (quite good).
> *   **μεγάλος** (big) ➔ **μεγαλούτσικος** (biggish).
> *   **ξινό** (sour) ➔ **ξινούτσικο** (a little sour).

---

#### 2. The Augmentatives (Making things HUGE)
Sometimes you need to emphasize that something is massive, impressive, or even a bit scary. Augmentatives express large size or admiration.

**Suffix 1: -αρος (The Giant)**
This is the most common masculine augmentative.
*   **το σκυλί** (the dog) ➔ **ο σκύλαρος** (the great big dog).
*   **το παιδί** (the boy/lad) ➔ **ο παίδαρος** (the big, handsome lad).

**Suffix 2: -άρα & -α (The Huge Feminine)**
This creates a sense of enormous, sometimes overwhelming size. 
*   **το αυτοκίνητο** (the car) ➔ **η αυτοκινητάρα** (the huge, impressive car).
*   **η κοιλιά** (the belly) ➔ **η κοιλάρα** (the big belly).
*   **το μπουκάλι** (the bottle) ➔ **η μπουκάλα** (the big bottle).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Native Secret: The "Possessor of a Quality" (-αράς / -ακλάς)**  
> If you want to describe a man who has a HUGE amount of a certain quality, use **-αράς** or **-ακλάς**.
> *   **η δουλειά** (work) ➔ **ο δουλευταράς** (the incredibly hard worker).
> *   **η φωνή** (voice) ➔ **ο φωνακλάς** (the loud-mouth).

---

#### 3. The Pragmatic Power of Emotion
Remember, these suffixes aren't just about physical size. Greek often uses the diminutive forms of nouns and adjectives with acts like offers, compliments, and requests as a device to show affection or to **minimize the imposition** placed by the speaker on the addressee. 

If a friend invites you to their house and says, *"Πάρτε ένα μπισκοτάκι"* (Take a little biscuit), they are using the diminutive to make the offer sound polite and unobtrusive. When you ask a waiter, *"Μας φέρνετε μια μπιρίτσα;"* (Can you bring us a little beer?), it sounds incredibly friendly and casual.

### Unit 30: Compound Words & Prefixation

Greek vocabulary is massive, but it's incredibly logical. Throughout its long history, Greek has always had a great facility for creating new words. Instead of inventing brand new words for every concept, Greeks build words like Lego bricks by modifying stems with prefixes, or snapping two smaller words together. 

Once you understand how the bricks connect, you can usually guess the meaning of a C1/C2 word even if you've never seen it before!

---

#### 1. The Greek "Glue": The letter Omikron (o)
When you attach two Greek words together to form a compound, you almost always need "glue" to connect them. The elements that make up a compound are typically linked by the vowel **-ο-**. 

If the first word ends in a normal vowel (like -ος, -η, or -ι), that vowel simply turns into an **ο**. Then the second word attaches to it!

*   **βιβλίο** (book) + **πωλώ** (I sell) ➔ **βιβλιοπωλείο** (bookstore).
*   **μαχαίρι** (knife) + **πιρούνι** (fork) ➔ **μαχαιροπίρουνο** (knife and fork).
*   **Σάββατο** (Saturday) + **Κυριακή** (Sunday) ➔ **Σαββατοκύριακο** (weekend).

---

#### 2. Prefixes (The Power Bricks)
There are dozens of highly active prefixes (mini-words) that you can slap onto the front of any word to instantly change its meaning.

### Α- / Αν- (The Negative / Un-)
This is the famous Alpha Privative. Greek uses a simple **α-** (if the next letter is a consonant) or **αν-** (if the next letter is a vowel). 

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **C1 Pronunciation Secret:** When adding the alpha privative to form a negative adjective, **the stress normally shifts to the third syllable from the end**!
> *   γνωστός (known) ➔ **άγνωστος** (unknown).
> *   φυσικός (natural) ➔ **αφύσικος** (unnatural).
> *   πιθανός (probable) ➔ **απίθανος** (improbable / amazing).

### Ξε- (The Reverser / Un-)
Prefixed to verbs to reverse the action (like tying and un-tying).
*   **κλειδώνω** (I lock) ➔ **ξεκλειδώνω** (I unlock).
*   **ντύνω** (I dress) ➔ **ξεντύνω** (I undress).
*   **διψώ** (I am thirsty) ➔ **ξεδιψώ** (I quench my thirst).

### Ξανα- (Again / Re-)
If you want to say "do it again" or "re-do", just add **ξανα-** to the verb!
*   **κάνω** (I do) ➔ **ξανακάνω** (I re-do).
*   **διαβάζω** (I read) ➔ **ξαναδιαβάζω** (I reread).

> [!TIP]
> **Bonus C1 Prefix: Κατα- (Very much)**  
> You can stick **κατα-** in front of adjectives to mean "extremely", or verbs to intensify the action. 
> *   άσπρος (white) ➔ **κάτασπρος** (very white).
*   βρέχω (I wet) ➔ **καταβρέχω** (I soak / drench).

---

#### 3. Advanced Combinations
You can stick almost any parts of speech together to create powerful descriptive words. Always remember to use the glue **ο**!

*   **Adjective + Adjective:** **ασπρόμαυρος** (black and white).
*   **Adjective + Noun:** **κοκκινομάλλης** (red-haired), **στενόμυαλος** (narrow-minded).
*   **Adjective/Adverb + Participle:** **φρεσκοβαμμένος** (freshly painted), **μαυροντυμένος** (dressed in black).

### 🎭 The Action Combos (Verb + Verb)
Greek frequently creates compounds by sticking two verbs together, usually connected with the **ο** glue. 
*   **ανοίγω** (open) + **κλείνω** (close) ➔ **ανοιγοκλείνω** (I open and close).
*   **πηγαίνω** (go) + **έρχομαι** (come) ➔ **πηγαινοέρχομαι** (I come and go / I pacing).
*   **ανεβαίνω** (go up) + **κατεβαίνω** (go down) ➔ **ανεβοκατεβαίνω** (I go up and down).

---

#### 4. Famous Greek Roots
Once you hit C1, you will see these roots everywhere. 

*   **-φίλος** (Lover / Friend): **ζωόφιλος** (animal lover), **φιλόξενος** (hospitable).
*   **-φόβος** (Fearer / Hater): **ξενοφοβία** (fear of foreigners), **υψοφοβία** (fear of heights).
*   **-λόγος** (Speaker / Student of): **ψυχολόγος** (psychologist).
*   **-γράφος** (Writer): **μυθιστοριογράφος** (novelist).

### Ancient Remnants in Modern Greek

Welcome to Level C2: Mastery.

Modern Greek is a living, breathing language, but it carries the ghosts of its 3,000-year history everywhere it goes. 

In the 19th and 20th centuries, Greece had two official languages: 
1. **Demotic (Δημοτική):** The natural spoken language of the people. 
2. **Katharevousa (Καθαρεύουσα):** An artificial, highly purified, "ancient-sounding" language used by the government, schools, and news anchors.

In 1976, Demotic officially won and became the sole language of Greece. However, the 150 years of Katharevousa left deep scars on the modern language. 

To achieve true C2 fluency, you must understand the "Ancient Remnants" that are still used in the news, literature, and formal speech today.

---

#### 1. Dative Expressions (The Lost Case)
Ancient Greek had a 5th case: The **Dative Case** (used to show "to" or "for" someone). Modern Greek replaced this entirely by using "σε + Accusative". 

However, dozens of Dative phrases survived as frozen, highly formal expressions. You must memorize them as whole blocks:
*   **Εν τω μεταξύ** (In the meantime)
*   **Δόξα τω Θεώ** (Glory to God / Thank God)
*   **Εν πάση περιπτώσει** (In any case / Anyway)
*   **Εν τέλει** (In the end / Finally)
*   **Τοις μετρητοίς** (In cash)

> [!NOTE]
> The tiny word **εν** (in) is an ancient preposition that ALWAYS triggers the Dative case. You will see it constantly in C2 vocabulary!

---

#### 2. Ancient Prepositions
C2 Greek resurrects several ancient prepositions that demand the **Genitive case**. They are used in formal contexts, journalism, and academic writing.

| Ancient Preposition | Meaning | Modern Equivalent | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ενώπιον** (+ Gen) | Before / In the presence of | Μπροστά σε | Ορκίστηκε ενώπιον του δικαστηρίου. |
| **Λόγω** (+ Gen) | Due to / Because of | Επειδή / Εξαιτίας | Κλειστό λόγω βροχής. |
| **Μέσω** (+ Gen) | Via / Through | Από | Έφτασα μέσω Αθηνών. |
| **Άνευ** (+ Gen) | Without | Χωρίς | Διασταύρωση άνευ σηματοδότη. |
| **Κατά** (+ Gen) | Against | Εναντίον | Διαδήλωση κατά της κυβέρνησης. |

---

#### 3. The 3rd Declension Nouns (Singular -εως / Plural -εις)
Katharevousa dragged a group of ancient "3rd Declension" nouns into modern Greek. These are almost always abstract feminine nouns ending in **-η** (ancient **-ις**). You will recognize them because they almost all end in **-ση, -ξη, or -ψη**!

Their defining feature is their formal Genitive singular ending: **-εως**.
*   **Η πόλη** (The city) ➔ Το κέντρο **της πόλεως** (The center of the city).
*   **Η απόφαση** (The decision) ➔ Η λήψη **της αποφάσεως** (The taking of the decision).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Accent Shift Rule:**  
> If the word has more than two syllables (like *κυβέρνηση*), the stress **must move one syllable forward** in the formal *-εως* genitive (➔ **κυβερνήσεως**). If it has only two syllables (like *πόλη*), the stress stays in the same place (**πόλεως**).

**The Plural (-εις):**  
In the **plural**, these nouns ALWAYS use the ancient endings: Nominative/Accusative **-εις** and Genitive **-εων**.
*   Οι **αποφάσεις** (The decisions) ➔ Των **αποφάσεων**.
*   Οι **πόλεις** (The cities) ➔ Των **πόλεων**.

---

#### 4. Bonus: C2 Adjectives & Participles
To truly master formal Greek, you must recognize two ancient adjective classes that survive perfectly intact:

**The -ης / -ες Adjectives:**  
These do not have separate forms for masculine and feminine. In the Genitive singular (for all genders), they take the ancient ending **-ους**.
*   **ακριβής** (exact/precise), **διεθνής** (international), **συνεχής** (continuous).
*   *Genitive:* Το αεροδρόμιο του **διεθνούς** δικτύου.

**The -ων / -ουσα / -ον Participles:**  
These are ancient active participles that act like adjectives (similar to the English "-ing"). 
*   **ενδιαφέρων** (Masc), **ενδιαφέρουσα** (Fem), **ενδιαφέρον** (Neut) = Interesting.
*   **υπάρχων** (existing), **μέλλων** (future).

### Participles in the Genitive Absolute (Γενική Απόλυτη)

In Level B2, you learned about basic Participles. But there is a "secret" third type of participle structure—a ghost from Ancient Greek that survived in modern, highly formal Katharevousa-style texts.

It is called the **Genitive Absolute (Η Γενική Απόλυτη Μετοχή)**. 

You will rarely hear this spoken in a coffee shop, but you will absolutely see it in newspapers, official documents, literature, and news broadcasts.

---

#### 1. What is the Genitive Absolute?
Sometimes you want to connect two actions that happen at the same time, but they have two different subjects.

In normal everyday Greek, you use the word **ενώ** (while) or **επειδή** (because):
*   *Επειδή έβρεχε, μείναμε μέσα.* (Because it was raining, we stayed inside).

To sound incredibly formal and elevated, C2 speakers rip out the connector words, erase the verb, and replace the whole clause with Nouns and Participles in the **Genitive Case**.

---

#### 2. The Formula & The Grammar
Instead of a normal sentence with a Nominative subject and a normal verb, you create an "Absolute" bubble out of Genitive words.

**Formula:** [Noun in Genitive] + [Participle in Genitive]

### A. The Ancient Active Participles (-ων, -ουσα, -ον)
To understand absolute expressions, you must recognize a class of ancient active participles that survive in formal Modern Greek. The stem for the Genitive forms ends in **-οντ-**. 
*   *Genitive:* του τρέχ**οντ**ος (of the running one).
*   *Plural Genitive:* των τρέχ**οντ**ων (of the running ones).

### B. Passive Participles & Reduplication (-μένος)
In highly formal C2 Greek, many passive participles feature an ancient prefix (an extra syllable) called **reduplication**.
*   **δίνω** (I give) ➔ **δεδομένος** (given).
*   **τελώ** (I perform) ➔ **τετελεσμένος** (accomplished).

> [!TIP]
> **Example in Context:**
> *Normal Greek:* Επειδή αυτά τα πράγματα είναι δεδομένα...  
> *Genitive Absolute:* **Δεδομένων αυτών των πραγμάτων...** (These things being given...)

---

#### 3. The Most Common Frozen Expressions
In Modern Greek, people almost exclusively rely on a handful of highly recognizable, frozen expressions. 

| The Genitive Absolute | Literal Translation | True Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Θεού θέλοντος** | God willing | Hopefully / God willing |
| **Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων** | Things having well | All being well / Hopefully |
| **Απουσιάζοντος του καθηγητού** | The teacher being absent | With the teacher absent |
| **Τρέχοντος του έτους** | The year running | During the current year |
| **Μη γένοιτο!** | Let it not happen | God forbid! |
| **Τουναντίον...** | Of the opposite | On the contrary... |

*   *Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων, θα τελειώσουμε το έργο αύριο.* (All going well, we will finish the project tomorrow).

---

*   [ ] I have memorized the most common C2 expressions: *Θεού θέλοντος* and *Καλώς εχόντων των πραγμάτων*.

### Irregular & Defective Words

You have mastered the rules of Greek grammar. You know how to decline nouns, conjugate verbs, and build complex sentences. But every language has its rebels—words that refuse to follow the rules, and words that are "defective" (missing pieces!).

---

#### 1. Nouns with Multiple Personalities (Double Plurals)
Some nouns change gender when they become plural, or offer two different plural forms with distinct meanings.

### The Gender-Benders (Masculine to Neuter)
Several common masculine nouns ending in **-ος** become Neuter ending in **-α** in the plural. Often, both plural forms exist but mean different things:

*   **Ο χρόνος** (year/time) ➔ **Τα χρόνια** (years). *(Use "οι χρόνοι" only for grammar tenses!)*.
*   **Ο λόγος** (reason/word) ➔ **Τα λόγια** (the words). *("Οι λόγοι" = the reasons/speeches)*.
*   **Ο αδελφός** (brother) ➔ **Τα αδέλφια** (siblings). *("Οι αδελφοί" = specifically brothers)*.
*   **Ο δεσμός** (bond) ➔ **Οι δεσμοί** (relationships) vs. **Τα δεσμά** (literal shackles).

### The "A-La-Carte" Plurals (-ούδες)
Loanwords and words ending in stressed vowels often add an extra syllable in the plural:
*   **Η αλεπού** (fox) ➔ **οι αλεπούδες**.
*   **Η μαϊμού** (monkey) ➔ **οι μαϊμούδες**.
*   **Η γλωσσού** (gossip) ➔ **οι γλωσσούδες**.

---

#### 2. Nouns that Refuse to Decline (Indeclinables)
Foreign loanwords often remain frozen. They rely entirely on the **Article** to show case and number.

*   **Το σπορ** (The sport) ➔ Βλέπω **τα σπορ** (I see the sports).
*   **Η τζαζ** (jazz), **η ελίτ** (the elite), **το ασανσέρ** (the lift).
*   **Common Gender:** ο/η ρεπόρτερ, ο/η μάνατζερ.

> [!NOTE]
> Even the letters of the Greek alphabet are neuter indeclinable nouns, like **το άλφα** and **το βήτα**!

---

#### 3. Defective Verbs (Missing Tenses)
A "defective" verb (ελλειπτικό ρήμα) exists exclusively in the **imperfective aspect**—it lacks a Simple Past (Aorist) or Simple Future.

*   **ανήκω** (I belong).
*   **οφείλω** (I owe / I am obliged).
*   **τρέμω** (I tremble).
*   **ξέρω** (I know) - Has the imperfect **ήξερα**, but no Simple Past!

### Impersonal Ghosts
*   **Πρέπει** (must) and **Πρόκειται** (is about to) are frozen in the 3rd person singular.
*   **Επρόκειτο:** The formal past (Imperfect) of *Πρόκειται*.

---

#### 4. The Verb "Είδα" & Ancient Remnants
While **είδα** (I saw) is a standard irregular verb, its ancient command form **Ιδού** (Behold) survives as a frozen remnant.

> [!TIP]
> **Famous Proverb:**  
> *"Ιδού η Ρόδος, ιδού και το πήδημα!"*  
> (Behold Rhodes, behold the jump!) — meaning: "Prove it right here, right now!"

---

### C2 Vocabulary: Ancient Phrases in Modern Greek

Modern Greek is heavily sprinkled with direct survivals from Ancient Greek. These phrases are used in formal settings, the news, and even everyday conversation to sound educated or poetic.

<!-- START LESSON: vocabulary-irregular-everyday -->
### C2 Vocabulary: Irregular Nouns & Verbs in the Wild

To achieve true mastery, you must know the common words that completely break the standard grammatical rules you learned in A1-B2.

#### 1. The "Ancient" Plurals (Irregular Neuter Nouns)
Some neuter everyday words take an extra syllable (-ακ-) when pluralized or put in the genitive.

- **το κρέας** *(to kré-as)* = the meat ➔ Plural: **τα κρέατα**
- **το τέρας** *(to té-ras)* = the monster ➔ Plural: **τα τέρατα**
- **το φως** *(to fos)* = the light ➔ Plural: **τα φώτα** / Genitive: **του φωτός**

#### 2. Unchanging Foreign Words
These words, heavily used in daily life, never change their endings no matter the case or number. The only thing that changes is the article (ο, η, το)!

- **το σπορ** *(to spor)* = the sport ➔ **τα σπορ** (the sports)
- **το κομπιούτερ** *(to kom-byoó-ter)* = the computer ➔ **τα κομπιούτερ**
- **το μπαρ** *(to bar)* = the bar
- **το ταξί** *(to ta-xí)* = the taxi (Grammatically foreign words don't change, e.g. *τα ταξί*, but colloquially you will often hear native speakers say **τα ταξιά**!)

#### 3. The Rebellious Adjectives
The adjective "much/many" and "big" are famously tricky to decline. Learn these key forms:

- **πολύς, πολλή, πολύ** *(po-lís)* = much / many
    - *Plural Forms:* **πολλοί** (masc), **πολλές** (fem), **πολλά** (neut)
- **μέγας, μεγάλη, μέγα** *(mé-gas)* = great / huge (from ancient Greek, now used alongside 'μεγάλος')

#### 4. The 3rd Person "Frozen" Genitive Absolute
Sometimes, Greeks use ancient participle constructions that seem disconnected from the sentence (Genitive Absolute).

- **όντως** *(ón-dos)* = indeed / in reality / factually
- **όντος** *(ón-dos)* = being (Ancient genitive participle) (e.g. **όλων των πραγμάτων όντων...** = all things being...)
- **τρέχοντος (μήνα)** *(tré-hon-dos)* = of the current (month)

| **Η γυναίκα φοράει το παλτό.** <br> *(The woman wears the coat.)* | Change object to **Plural** | **Η γυναίκα φοράει τα παλτά.** |
| **Το μικρό παιδί παίζει.** <br> *(The small child plays.)* | Change subject to **Plural** | **Τα μικρά παιδιά παίζουν.** |
| **Αγοράζω το ακριβό ρούχο.** <br> *(I buy the expensive piece of clothing.)* | Change object to **Plural** | **Αγοράζω τα ακριβά ρούχα.** |
| **Ο πωλητής μιλάει πολύ.** <br> *(The salesperson talks a lot.)* | Change subject to **Plural** | **Οι πωλητές μιλούν πολύ.** |

#### 4. The Grammar-Saturated Story

Read this short story three times: first for accuracy, second for comprehension, and third *as fast as possible*. This forces your brain to "soak" in the patterns of Plural Nouns and Group B Verbs.

> «Τα μαγαζιά είναι ακριβά. Οι γυναίκες αγαπούν τα ωραία ρούχα. Δοκιμάζουν τα μικρά φορέματα και τα μεγάλα παλτά. Οι πωλητές μιλούν πολύ. Τα ρούχα κοστίζουν πολλά ευρώ. Όλοι γελάνε.»
